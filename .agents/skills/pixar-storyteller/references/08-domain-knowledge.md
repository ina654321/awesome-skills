## The 22 Rules of Storytelling

Created by Emma Coats, Pixar story artist, these rules capture the studio's storytelling wisdom:

### Character Rules (1-6)

**Rule 1**: You admire a character for trying more than for their successes.
- Effort and vulnerability create connection
- Perfect characters are boring
- Struggle is relatable

**Rule 2**: You gotta keep in mind what's interesting to you as an audience, not what's fun to do as a writer.
- Audience experience > writer ego
- What entertains you may not entertain others
- Stay audience-focused

**Rule 3**: Trying for theme is important, but you won't see what the story is actually about til you're at the end of it. Now rewrite.
- Theme emerges through writing
- First drafts reveal true themes
- Rewrite to strengthen discovered themes

**Rule 4**: Once upon a time there was ___. Every day, ___. One day ___. Because of that, ___. Because of that, ___. Until finally ___.
- The Story Spine structure
- Cause-and-effect storytelling
- Simple but powerful framework

**Rule 5**: Simplify. Focus. Combine characters. Hop over detours. You'll feel like you're losing valuable stuff but it sets you free.
- Less is more
- Focus on core story
- Cut what doesn't serve

**Rule 6**: What is your character good at, comfortable with? Throw the polar opposite at them. Challenge them. How do they deal?
- Comfort zones prevent growth
- Opposition creates drama
- Reveals true character

### Structure Rules (7-12)

**Rule 7**: Come up with your ending before you figure out your middle. Seriously. Endings are hard, get yours working up front.
- Endings define journeys
- Know your destination
- Work backwards from climax

**Rule 8**: Finish your story, let go even if it's not perfect. In an ideal world you have both, but move on. Do better next time.
- Perfect is the enemy of done
- Learn and iterate
- Next story will be better

**Rule 9**: When you're stuck, make a list of what WOULDN'T happen next. Lots of times the material to get you unstuck will show up.
- Elimination generates clarity
- Negative space reveals positive
- Counter-intuitive but effective

**Rule 10**: Pull apart the stories you like. What you like in them is a part of you; you've got to recognize it before you can use it.
- Analyze what resonates
- Your taste reveals your voice
- Learn from masters

**Rule 11**: Putting it on paper lets you start fixing it. If it stays in your head, a perfect idea, you'll never share it with anyone.
- Execution > ideas
- Visible work is fixable work
- Share to improve

**Rule 12**: Discount the 1st thing that comes to mind. And the 2nd, 3rd, 4th, 5th – get the obvious out of the way. Surprise yourself.
- First ideas are obvious ideas
- Dig deeper for originality
- Surprise creates delight

### Creative Rules (13-18)

**Rule 13**: Give your characters opinions. Passive/malleable might seem likable to you as you write, but it's poison to the audience.
- Characters need agency
- Opinions create conflict
- Passivity kills engagement

**Rule 14**: Why must you tell THIS story? What's the belief burning within you that your story feeds off of? That's the heart of it.
- Personal connection drives passion
- Authenticity resonates
- Find your why

**Rule 15**: If you were your character, in this situation, how would you feel? Honesty lends credibility to unbelievable situations.
- Emotional truth transcends logic
- Authenticity creates connection
- Feel first, write second

**Rule 16**: What are the stakes? Give us reason to root for the character. What happens if they don't succeed? Stack the odds against.
- Stakes create engagement
- Risk drives interest
- Consequences matter

**Rule 17**: No work is ever wasted. If it's not working, let go and move on – it'll come back around to be useful later.
- Everything teaches something
- Skills compound
- Trust the process

**Rule 18**: You have to know yourself: the difference between doing your best and fussing. Story is testing, not refining.
- Perfectionism vs excellence
- Testing reveals truth
- Don't polish too early

### Advanced Rules (19-22)

**Rule 19**: Coincidences to get characters into trouble are great; coincidences to get them out of it are cheating.
- Setup must precede payoff
- Earn your resolutions
- Don't rely on luck

**Rule 20**: Exercise: take the building blocks of a movie you dislike. How would you rearrange them into what you DO like?
- Analyze failure to find success
- Same elements, different arrangement
- Structure matters

**Rule 21**: You gotta identify with your situation/characters, can't just write 'cool'. What would make YOU act that way?
- Personal investment required
- Emotional connection essential
- Write what you understand

**Rule 22**: What's the essence of your story? Most economical telling of it? If you know that, you can build out from there.
- Find the core
- Simplicity is strength
- Build from essence

## Story Department Structure

### Roles and Responsibilities

**Story Artist**:
- Draw storyboards
- Pitch sequences
- Iterate based on feedback
- Collaborate with directors

**Story Supervisor**:
- Lead story team
- Oversee storyboard quality
- Coordinate with other departments
- Manage schedules

**Head of Story**:
- Studio-wide story oversight
- Talent development
- Process improvement
- Quality standards

**Director**:
- Final creative authority
- Vision keeper
- Decision maker
- Team leader

### The Story Process Timeline

**Months 1-6: Concept**
- Pitch development
- Treatment writing
- Early visual development

**Months 7-18: Storyboarding**
- Rough boards
- Pitching sequences
- Braintrust reviews
- Major revisions

**Months 19-30: Reels**
- Animatics with temp voices
- Timing and pacing
- Multiple review rounds
- Story locking

**Months 31-48: Production**
- Animation
- Lighting
- Rendering
- Final edit
