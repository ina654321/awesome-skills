# Project Jarvis

## Overview
Project Jarvis is Blue Origin's internal initiative to develop a reusable second stage for the New Glenn rocket. Named after Tony Stark's AI assistant in the Marvel universe, the project represents Blue Origin's pursuit of full reusability.

## Context

### Current New Glenn Architecture
- **First Stage:** Reusable (lands on Jacklyn ship)
- **Second Stage:** Expendable (BE-3U engines, discarded after each flight)
- **Fairing:** Expendable

### The Reusability Challenge
While SpaceX achieved first-stage reusability with Falcon 9, full vehicle reusability remains the holy grail of launch cost reduction. Starship aims for full reusability, but New Glenn currently expends its upper stage on every flight.

## Project Objectives

### Primary Goal
Develop a reusable second stage that can survive atmospheric reentry, return to Earth, and be refurbished for reflight.

### Economic Question
> "The goal for the expendable stage is to become so cheap to manufacture that reusability never makes sense. The goal for the reusability stage is to become so operable that expendability never makes sense."
> — Jeff Bezos

Project Jarvis is essentially a "horse race" between two competing approaches:
1. **Reusable Second Stage:** High upfront development, low per-flight cost
2. **Cheap Expendable Stage:** Low development, higher per-flight cost but very cheap manufacturing

## Technical Challenges

### Atmospheric Reentry
| Challenge | Consideration |
|-----------|---------------|
| Velocity | ~7.8 km/s orbital velocity must be shed |
| Heat Load | Significantly higher than first stage return |
| Thermal Protection | Must protect engines, avionics, propellant tanks |
| Control | Stable reentry orientation without propulsion |

### Vehicle Design Trade-offs
| Material | Advantages | Disadvantages |
|----------|------------|---------------|
| **Aluminum** | Light weight, familiar manufacturing | Lower thermal tolerance |
| **Stainless Steel** | High thermal tolerance, durable | Heavier, requires more propellant for same performance |

### BE-3U Integration
- Current expendable stage uses BE-3U engines
- Reusable version must protect engines during reentry
- Potential for engine reuse across multiple flights

## Development Status

### Current Phase
- Active development since 2021
- Focus on thermal protection system (TPS) development
- Materials trade study ongoing (aluminum vs. stainless steel)

### Testing Activities
- Component-level TPS testing
- Sub-scale model reentry testing
- Thermal analysis and simulation

### No Public Commitment
Blue Origin has not publicly committed to fielding a reusable second stage. The decision depends on the outcome of the economic and technical trade studies.

## Comparison to SpaceX Approach

### Falcon 9
- **Initial Goal:** Full reusability (both stages)
- **Final Approach:** First stage only; minimize upper stage cost
- **Rationale:** Upper stage recovery too mass-intensive for performance needs

### Starship
- **Architecture:** Both stages fully reusable from inception
- **Approach:** Stainless steel construction, integrated TPS, large scale
- **Status:** Booster recovery achieved; upper stage recovery in development

### New Glenn Potential
| Approach | Pros | Cons |
|----------|------|------|
| **Reusable Upper Stage** | Full reusability, lowest per-flight cost | Heavy development cost, performance penalty |
| **Cheap Expendable** | Preserve performance, simpler operations | Higher per-flight cost, manufacturing demand |

## Strategic Implications

### Competitive Position
- SpaceX Starship aims for full reusability
- If successful, Starship could undercut partially reusable vehicles
- Project Jarvis represents Blue Origin's path to competitiveness

### Economic Considerations
| Factor | Impact |
|--------|--------|
| Flight Rate | High rate favors reuse; low rate favors expendable |
| Manufacturing Cost | If expendable stages cost <$5M, reuse may not pencil out |
| Refurbishment Cost | Must be minimal for reuse to make sense |
| Payload Penalty | Reuse hardware reduces payload capacity |

### Gradatim Ferociter Application
Blue Origin's methodical approach means:
- Not rushing to match Starship's full reusability claim
- Thoroughly analyzing economics before committing
- Potentially accepting expendable upper stage if numbers don't support reuse

## Future Possibilities

### Outcome Scenarios

**Scenario A: Reusable Second Stage Selected**
- Significant development investment
- New Glenn becomes fully reusable
- Payload capacity reduced but cost per flight minimized
- 3-5 year development timeline

**Scenario B: Cheap Expendable Selected**
- Focus on manufacturing automation
- Upper stage cost driven below $5M
- Accept expendable as cost-effective solution
- Preserve maximum payload performance

**Scenario C: Hybrid Approach**
- Develop reusable stage for some missions
- Use expendable for maximum performance missions
- Customer choice based on payload value

### Beyond New Glenn
- Lessons learned applicable to future vehicles
- Potential "New Armstrong" lunar-class rocket
- Full reusability as default for next-generation designs
