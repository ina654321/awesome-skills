# Retirement Planning Guide

## Overview

Comprehensive retirement planning involves accumulation strategies, withdrawal planning, Social Security optimization, and healthcare considerations. This guide covers the Vanguard approach to retirement success.

## Accumulation Phase Strategies

### Savings Rate Targets

| Starting Age | Target Savings Rate | Notes |
|--------------|---------------------|-------|
| 20s | 10-15% | Compound interest works best |
| 30s | 15-20% | Catch-up if starting late |
| 40s | 20-25% | Aggressive if behind |
| 50s | 25-30%+ | Max all accounts + catch-up |

### The 4% Rule

**Concept:** Withdraw 4% of initial portfolio balance in year 1, then adjust for inflation annually.

```
Example:
- Portfolio at retirement: $1,000,000
- Year 1 withdrawal: $40,000
- Year 2 withdrawal: $40,000 + inflation (e.g., 3% = $41,200)
- Success probability (30 years): ~95%
```

**Assumptions:**
- 50/50 or 60/40 stock/bond allocation
- 30-year retirement
- Historical market returns
- No adjustments for market conditions

**Current Research Suggests:**
- 4% may be aggressive for current valuations/low yields
- 3.5% or dynamic strategies may be safer
- Flexibility improves success rates

## Social Security Optimization

### When to Claim

| Claim Age | Benefit | Best For |
|-----------|---------|----------|
| 62 | 70% of PIA | Poor health, need income |
| 67 (FRA) | 100% of PIA | Average life expectancy |
| 70 | 124% of PIA | Longevity, spouse survivor |

*PIA = Primary Insurance Amount (full benefit at FRA)*

### Break-Even Analysis

```
Claiming at 62 vs. 70:
- Early: Receive 8 more years of payments
- Late: Receive 32% higher payments for life

Break-even age: ~80-82

If you live past 82, delaying was optimal.
If you die before 82, claiming early was optimal.
```

### Survivor Benefit Strategy

```
For Married Couples:
- Higher earner should delay to 70
- Survivor gets 100% of higher earner's benefit
- Effectively provides longevity insurance for both

Example:
- Spouse A (high earner): $3,000 at 70
- Spouse B (low earner): $1,200 at 67
- If A dies first, B gets $3,000
```

### Spousal Benefits

| Rule | Details |
|------|---------|
| Eligibility | Married 1+ year, or divorced 10+ years |
| Amount | 50% of spouse's PIA (at spouse's FRA) |
| Reduction | Reduced if claimed before own FRA |
| Coordination | Can claim spousal, delay own benefit |

## Withdrawal Strategies

### Dynamic Withdrawal Methods

#### 1. Guardrails Approach (Guyton-Klinger)

```
Rules:
- Increase withdrawals by inflation each year
- IF portfolio up significantly: increase 10%
- IF portfolio down significantly: decrease 10%
- IF below floor: adjust spending

Example:
- Year 1: $40,000 (4% of $1M)
- Portfolio drops to $800k
- Floor: 4% of $800k = $32,000
- Next year withdrawal: $32,000 (guardrail hit)
```

#### 2. VPW (Variable Percentage Withdrawal)

```
Concept: Withdraw percentage based on age and portfolio value
- Higher percentage as you age (fewer years remaining)
- Adjusts to market conditions automatically
- Never runs out of money (percentage of remaining)

Example (Vanguard calculation):
- Age 65, 60/40 portfolio: ~4.5% rate
- Age 75, 50/50 portfolio: ~5.5% rate
- Age 85, 40/60 portfolio: ~7.0% rate

Link: Vanguard Personal Finance → Retirement Income Calculator
```

#### 3. Floor-and-Ceiling

```
Set boundaries on withdrawals:
- Ceiling: Cannot increase more than 5% above prior year
- Floor: Cannot decrease more than 2.5% below prior year
- Flexibility for market conditions
```

### Account Withdrawal Sequence

#### Standard Sequence

```
Phase 1 (Retirement to RMDs):
1. Taxable accounts (let tax-deferred grow)
2. Roth IRA contributions (tax-free)
3. Traditional IRA/401k (fill lower brackets)

Phase 2 (RMD Age+):
1. RMDs from Traditional accounts (required)
2. Additional needs from Taxable
3. Roth IRA (preserve for heirs/emergencies)
```

#### Alternative: Roth First (Tax Rate Arbitrage)

```
If current tax rate < expected future rate:
1. Roth IRA withdrawals
2. Taxable accounts
3. Traditional accounts (delay)

Rationale: Pay taxes when rates are lower
```

## Healthcare in Retirement

### Medicare Basics

| Part | Coverage | Typical Cost |
|------|----------|--------------|
| A | Hospital | Free (if qualified) |
| B | Medical | $174.70/month (2024) |
| C | Advantage | Varies by plan |
| D | Prescription | Varies by plan |

### Medicare Premium Brackets (IRMAA)

| MAGI (Single) | MAGI (MFJ) | Part B Premium | Part D Surcharge |
|---------------|------------|----------------|------------------|
| ≤$103,000 | ≤$206,000 | $174.70 | $0 |
| $103k-$129k | $206k-$258k | $244.60 | $12.90 |
| $129k-$161k | $258k-$322k | $349.40 | $33.30 |
| $161k-$193k | $322k-$386k | $454.20 | $53.80 |
| $193k-$500k | $386k-$750k | $559.00 | $74.20 |
| >$500k | >$750k | $594.00 | $81.00 |

**Key Point:** Small increase in MAGI can trigger large premium increases. Manage Roth conversions carefully!

### Healthcare Bridge Strategy (Pre-Medicare)

```
Ages 60-65 (if retiring early):
- COBRA: 18 months continuation
- ACA Marketplace: Subsidies available based on income
- Keep MAGI below 400% FPL for subsidies

Strategy:
- Use taxable accounts (don't increase MAGI)
- Roth withdrawals don't count as income
- Control income to maximize ACA subsidies
```

### Long-Term Care Considerations

| Option | Pros | Cons |
|--------|------|------|
| Self-insure | Flexibility, keep assets | Risk of catastrophic costs |
| LTC Insurance | Transfers risk | Expensive, rate increases |
| Hybrid policies | Death benefit if unused | Complex, high premiums |
| Medicaid spend-down | Ultimate backstop | Limited choices, asset depletion |

## Sequence of Returns Risk

### The Danger Zone

```
Bad returns in first 5-10 years of retirement can devastate portfolios,
even if long-term average returns are good.

Example:
Portfolio: $1,000,000
Withdrawal: $40,000/year (4%)

Scenario A (Good early returns):
- Years 1-5: +10% average
- Portfolio withstands withdrawals
- Long-term success

Scenario B (Bad early returns):
- Years 1-5: -5% average
- Portfolio depleted faster
- May not recover
```

### Mitigation Strategies

| Strategy | Implementation |
|----------|----------------|
| Cash Buffer | 2-3 years expenses in cash/CDs |
| Bond Ladder | Short-term bonds for early years |
| Dynamic Spending | Reduce spending when portfolio down |
| Annuities | Guaranteed income floor |
| Glide Path | Conservative allocation early retirement |

### The "Bond Tent" Strategy

```
Allocation glide path approaching and entering retirement:

Accumulation (Age 40-50): 80/20 or 90/10
Pre-retirement (50-60): Begin increasing bonds
At retirement (60-65): Peak bond allocation (50/50 or 60/40)
Early retirement (65-75): Maintain conservative
Late retirement (75+): Can increase stocks if desired

Rationale: Protect against sequence risk when most vulnerable
```

## Estate Planning

### Beneficiary Designations

| Account Type | Primary | Contingent |
|--------------|---------|------------|
| 401(k) | Name beneficiaries | Update after life events |
| IRA | Name beneficiaries | Check annually |
| Roth IRA | Name beneficiaries | Tax-free inheritance |
| HSA | Name beneficiaries | Spouse = HSA, Others = taxable |

### Inherited IRA Rules (SECURE Act)

| Relationship | Distribution Rule |
|--------------|-------------------|
| Spouse | Can treat as own IRA (stretch) |
| Minor child | Stretch until age 21, then 10-year rule |
| Disabled/chronic illness | Stretch allowed |
| Within 10 years of age | Stretch allowed |
| All others | 10-year rule (empty account) |

### Roth IRA for Estate Planning

```
Benefits:
- No RMDs for original owner
- Tax-free inheritance for heirs
- Heirs subject to 10-year rule but no taxes
- Can "stretch" tax-free growth for 10 years

Example:
- $500,000 Roth IRA inherited at age 50
- Heir lets grow for 10 years at 7%
- After 10 years: ~$983,000 tax-free
```
