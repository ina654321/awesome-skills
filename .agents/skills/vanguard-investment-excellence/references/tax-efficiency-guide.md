# Tax-Efficient Investing Guide

## Overview

Tax-efficient investing is about maximizing after-tax returns, not pre-tax returns. Since taxes are often the largest expense for investors, optimizing for tax efficiency can add 0.20-0.50% annually to returns.

---

## Account Type Hierarchy

### 1. Tax-Advantaged Accounts (Fill First)

| Account Type | Tax Benefit | Best For |
|--------------|-------------|----------|
| **401(k)** | Pre-tax contributions, tax-deferred growth | High earners, tax reduction now |
| **Traditional IRA** | Tax-deductible, tax-deferred growth | Income eligible, 401k not available |
| **Roth 401(k)** | Tax-free growth, tax-free withdrawals | Lower earners, tax diversification |
| **Roth IRA** | Tax-free growth, tax-free withdrawals | Emergency backup, estate planning |
| **HSA** | Triple tax advantage | Medical expenses, stealth IRA |
| **529** | Tax-free growth for education | College savings |

### 2. Taxable Brokerage Accounts

| Feature | Implication |
|---------|-------------|
| Contributions | After-tax |
| Growth | Taxed as capital gains/dividends |
| Withdrawals | Only gains taxed |
| Best For | Tax-efficient investments, extra savings |

### Why International in Taxable?

The **Foreign Tax Credit** allows you to claim a credit for taxes paid to foreign governments on international investments.

- **VEA (Developed Markets):** Eligible for foreign tax credit
- **VXUS (Total International):** Eligible
- **VWO (Emerging Markets):** Eligible

**Example:**
```
VEA distributes $1,000 in dividends
Foreign taxes paid: $70 (7% average)
You can claim $70 credit on U.S. taxes
Effective tax rate on dividends reduced
```

Note: Foreign tax credit only applies to taxable accounts, not IRAs.

## Tax-Loss Harvesting (TLH)

### What is TLH?

Selling investments at a loss to offset capital gains or ordinary income (up to $3,000/year), while maintaining market exposure through similar (not identical) investments.

### TLH Rules

| Rule | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Wash Sale Rule** | Cannot buy "substantially identical" security 30 days before or after sale |
| **$3,000 Limit** | Can offset up to $3,000 of ordinary income annually |
| **Carryforward** | Excess losses carry forward indefinitely |
| **Short vs Long** | Short-term losses first offset short-term gains (higher tax rate) |

### Vanguard TLH Partners

| Primary | TLH Partner | Notes |
|---------|-------------|-------|
| VTI (Total Stock) | VV (Large-Cap) | Different indexes, similar exposure |
| VTI | VOO (S&P 500) | VOO is subset of VTI |
| VXUS (Total Intl) | VEA + VWO | Split developed/emerging |
| VEA (Developed) | VEU | Slightly different coverage |
| VWO (Emerging) | IEMG (iShares) | Different provider, similar index |
| BND (Total Bond) | AGG (iShares) | Different provider |

### TLH Example

```
Scenario: Market drops 20%

Action:
1. Sell VTI with $50,000 loss
2. Buy VV (Large-Cap) immediately
3. Maintain market exposure
4. After 31 days, can switch back to VTI

Tax Benefit:
- $50,000 loss harvested
- Offsets $50,000 in capital gains (tax-free), OR
- Offsets $3,000 ordinary income + $47,000 carried forward
- At 15% capital gains rate: $7,500 tax savings
- At 24% ordinary income rate: $720 tax savings (plus future)
```

## Roth Conversion Strategies

### When to Consider Roth Conversions

| Situation | Strategy |
|-----------|----------|
| Low-income year | Convert at low tax bracket |
| Early retirement (before RMDs) | Fill lower brackets with conversions |
| Market downturn | Convert when account value is low |
| Expect higher future taxes | Lock in current rate |

### Roth Conversion Ladder (Early Retirement)

```
Years -5 to 0: Final working years, max traditional 401k
Year 1: Retire, rollover 401k to Traditional IRA
Year 2: Convert one year of expenses to Roth IRA
Year 3: Convert next year's expenses
Year 4: Withdraw Year 2 conversion (5-year rule met)
Repeat annually

Benefit: Access retirement funds before 59.5 without penalty
```

## Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs)

### RMD Basics

| Age | RMD Required? | Percentage |
|-----|---------------|------------|
| 73 (born 1951-1959) | Yes | ~3.65% starting |
| 75 (born 1960+) | Yes | Starting at 75 |
| Before 73 | No | N/A |

### RMD Calculation

```
RMD = Prior Year-End Balance / Life Expectancy Factor

Example:
- Age 73: $1,000,000 balance
- Life expectancy factor: 26.5
- RMD = $1,000,000 / 26.5 = $37,736
```

### Qualified Charitable Distributions (QCDs)

| Feature | Details |
|---------|---------|
| Age Eligible | 70.5+ |
| Maximum | $100,000/year |
| Tax Treatment | Excluded from income |
| RMD Status | Counts toward RMD |
| Best For | Charitably inclined, don't need full RMD |

## Social Security Taxation

### When Social Security Becomes Taxable

| Income Level | Taxation |
|--------------|----------|
| Below $25,000 (single) / $32,000 (MFJ) | 0% taxable |
| $25,000-$34,000 (single) / $32,000-$44,000 (MFJ) | Up to 50% taxable |
| Above $34,000 (single) / $44,000 (MFJ) | Up to 85% taxable |

### Social Security Tax Torpedo

```
As provisional income increases through thresholds:
- Each additional dollar of income can cause
- $0.50 or $0.85 of Social Security to become taxable
- Effective marginal rate can reach 46.25%

Strategy: Smooth income, avoid spikes
```

## Tax-Efficient Withdrawal Sequence

### Standard Withdrawal Order

```
1. Taxable Accounts
   └── Let tax-advantaged accounts grow
   └── Pay favorable capital gains rates

2. Tax-Deferred Accounts (Traditional IRA/401k)
   └── Before RMDs force larger withdrawals
   └── Fill lower tax brackets

3. Roth Accounts
   └── Last (tax-free growth continues)
   └── Emergency buffer
   └── Estate planning (no RMDs for original owner)
```

### Modified Order (Early Retirement)

```
1. Taxable Accounts (same)
2. Roth Contributions (can withdraw penalty-free)
3. Roth Conversions (after 5 years)
4. Tax-deferred accounts
```
