# Scenario Examples

## 9.1 Zoning Variance Request

**User:** "A property owner wants to build closer to the property line than zoning allows. They're arguing hardship. What do you tell them?"

**Expert:**
> **Zoning Variance Analysis:**
>
> | Requirement | Variance Type |
> |------------|---------------|
> | **Area variance** | Setback, height, lot coverage |
> | **Use variance** | Changing permitted use |
>
> **Legal Standards (Federal Case Law):**
> - **Area variance**: Balance harm to applicant vs. neighborhood
> - **Use variance**: Hardship must be unique, not self-created
> - **Due process**: Notice and hearing required
> - **Equal treatment**: Similar variances decided consistently
>
> **Process:**
> 1. Applicant submits variance application
> 2. Staff reviews and provides recommendation
> 3. Zoning Board of Appeals (ZBA) holds public hearing
> 4. Neighbor notification (typically 500ft radius)
> 5. ZBA deliberates and votes
> 6. Decision appealable to court
>
> **Key Questions:**
> - Is the hardship unique to this property?
> - Would denial prevent any reasonable use?
> - Would the variance harm the neighborhood?
> - Can the applicant demonstrate good faith?

## 9.2 Tenant Displacement

**User:** "A landlord is renovating a building and wants to displace all tenants. What housing protections apply?"

**Expert:**
> **Tenant Displacement Analysis:**
>
> | Protection | Trigger | Requirement |
> |-----------|---------|-------------|
> | **Ellis Act (CA)** | Building withdrawal | Relocation assistance |
> | **J-51 (NYC)** | Tax abatement | Tenant protections required |
> | **Uniform Relocation Act** | Federal funding | Comparable replacement |
> | **Just Cause Eviction** | State/local law | Must state valid reason |
>
> **Checklist:**
> 1. Does the jurisdiction require just cause eviction?
> 2. Is relocation assistance required?
> 3. Are there rent stabilization protections?
> 4. Does owner need to provide alternative housing?
> 5. What are the notice requirements?
>
> **Owner Options:**
> - Owner move-in (if primary residence, limits apply)
> - Substantial rehabilitation (verify permits)
> - Demolition (may require replacement housing)
> - Ellis Act withdrawal (must offer back first)

## 9.3 Infrastructure Failure

**User:** "A watermain breaks during rush hour, flooding several blocks. The mayor's office is calling. Walk me through the immediate response."

**Expert:**
> **Infrastructure Emergency Response:**
>
> | Phase | Timeline | Actions |
> |------|----------|---------|
> | **Immediate** | 0-2 hrs | Shut off water, assess damage, 911 if injuries |
> | **Short-term** | 2-24 hrs | Emergency repair, traffic control, water distribution |
> | **Medium-term** | 1-7 days | Permanent repair, infrastructure assessment |
> | **Long-term** | 1-6 months | System upgrade, preventive maintenance review |
>
> **Communication Priorities:**
> 1. Public safety: Avoid flooded areas, don't drive through water
> 2. Water service: Boil water advisory, distribution of bottled water
> 3. Traffic: Detours, signal outage management
> 4. Business impact: Restaurant closures, affected businesses
> 5. Recovery: What went wrong, system age, capital improvements
>
> **Notification Chain:**
> - Public works director
> - Mayor/CMO
> - Fire/police (traffic control)
> - Schools (potential closure)
> - Hospital (water supply critical)
> - Media/social media
