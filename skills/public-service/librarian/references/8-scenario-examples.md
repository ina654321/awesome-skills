### Example 1: Reader Advisory Service

**Context:** Patron seeking reading recommendations

**User:** "I really loved The Night Circus by Erin Morgenstern. Can you recommend something similar?"

**Librarian Response:**

> **Reader Advisory Analysis:**
>
> **What they loved about The Night Circus:**
> - Magical realism with intricate world-building
> - Atmospheric, lyrical prose
> - Romance woven through fantasy
> - 19th/early 20th century setting
> - Slow-building, character-driven plot
>
> **Read-Alikes:**
> | Title | Author | Why It Works | Appeal Factor |
> |-------|--------|--------------|---------------|
> | The Starless Sea | Erin Morgenstern | Same author; similar magic/literary style | ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ |
> | The Invisible Life of Addie LaRue | V.E. Schwab | Immortal woman; atmospheric; romance | ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ |
> | Jonathan Strange & Mr Norrell | Susanna Clarke | 19th century magicians; dense world-building | ⭐⭐⭐⭐ |
> | The Ten Thousand Doors of January | Alix E. Harrow | Portal fantasy; lyrical; early 1900s | ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ |
> | The Binding | Bridget Collins | Magical bookbinding; romance; Victorian | ⭐⭐⭐⭐ |
> | The House in the Cerulean Sea | TJ Klune | Gentle fantasy; found family; whimsical | ⭐⭐⭐⭐ |
>
> **Follow-up Questions:**
> - Would you prefer standalone or series?
> - Do you enjoy YA crossover titles?
> - How do you feel about darker themes?
>
> **Recommendation:** Start with The Invisible Life of Addie LaRue for similar themes and tone, then The Ten Thousand Doors of January for more portal fantasy elements.

### Example 2: Collection Diversity Audit

**Context:** Library assessing representation in children's collection

**User:** "We need to audit our children's collection for diversity and inclusion. How should we approach this?"

**Librarian Response:**

> **Diversity Audit Framework:**
>
> **1. Define Scope:**
> | Dimension | Definition | Data Source |
> |-----------|------------|-------------|
> | Race/ethnicity | BIPOC characters/authors | Visual inspection; author research |
> | Gender identity | Trans/nonbinary representation | Subject headings; content review |
> | Disability | Characters with disabilities | Subject headings; plot review |
> | LGBTQ+ | Queer representation | Subject headings; content analysis |
> | Religion | Non-Christian representation | Subject headings; content review |
> | Socioeconomic | Working class/poverty representation | Plot analysis; setting |
>
> **2. Sampling Method:**
> - Sample size: 20% of collection (random stratified)
> - Stratify by: Picture books, early readers, chapter books, middle grade
> - Sample size per category: ~500 titles
> - Review period: 3 months with 2 reviewers
>
> **3. Data Collection Tool:**
> | Field | Options | Notes |
> |-------|---------|-------|
> | Title/Author | Free text | |
> | Publication year | Year | |
> | Main character race | White/Black/Latino/Asian/Indigenous/Multiracial/Unknown | Visual/content |
> | Author race | White/BIPOC/Unknown | Research required |
> | Disability rep | Yes/No/Unknown | |
> | LGBTQ+ rep | Yes/No/Unknown | |
> | #OwnVoices | Yes/No/Unknown | Author shares character identity |
>
> **4. Analysis:**
> | Metric | Current | Target | Gap |
> |--------|---------|--------|-----|
> | BIPOC characters | 18% | 40% (community demo) | -22% |
> | BIPOC authors | 12% | 30% | -18% |
> | Disability rep | 3% | 15% (population) | -12% |
> | LGBTQ+ rep | 5% | 10% | -5% |
> | #OwnVoices | 60% of diverse titles | 80% | -20% |
>
> **5. Action Plan:**
> ```
> Immediate (0-6 months):
> ├── Prioritize purchasing diverse titles
> ├── Create diverse booklists and displays
> └── Training for selectors on diverse sources
>
> Short-term (6-12 months):
> ├── Dedicated diversity purchasing budget
> ├── Community input on collection needs
> └── Weed outdated/problematic titles
>
> Long-term (1-3 years):
> ├── Ongoing diversity audits (annual)
> ├── Community advisory committee
> └── Partnership with diverse publishers
> ```
>
> **6. Recommended Review Sources for Diverse Books:**
> - We Need Diverse Books resources
> - The Brown Bookshelf
> - Disability in Kidlit
> - American Indians in Children's Literature

### Example 3: Information Literacy Instruction

**Context:** Teaching college students to evaluate online sources

**User:** "I need to design a workshop on evaluating online information for first-year college students. What's your approach?"

**Librarian Response:**

> **Information Literacy Workshop Design:**
>
> **Learning Outcomes:**
> By the end of this session, students will be able to:
> 1. Identify characteristics of credible online sources
> 2. Apply the SIFT method to evaluate information
> 3. Distinguish between news, opinion, and sponsored content
> 4. Recognize common misinformation tactics
>
> **Workshop Structure (50 minutes):**
>
> **Opening (5 min):**
> - Hook: Show viral misinformation example
> - "How many of you have shared something online that turned out to be false?"
> - Introduce importance of evaluation skills
>
> **Concept 1: Lateral Reading (15 min)**
> ```
> SIFT Method:
> S - STOP: Pause before sharing
> I - INVESTIGATE the source: Who are they? What's their reputation?
> F - FIND better coverage: What do other sources say?
> T - TRACE claims to original context: Did they say that?
> ```
> - Demonstration: Evaluate sample website together
> - Hands-on: Students practice on provided examples
>
> **Concept 2: Source Types (15 min)**
> | Type | Characteristics | Example |
> |------|-----------------|---------|
> | News reporting | Facts; multiple sources; neutral tone | Reuters, AP |
> | Opinion/Editorial | Argument; first-person; subjective | Columnists |
> | Sponsored content | Paid placement; often labeled | "Presented by..." |
> | Native advertising | Designed to blend with content | Product features |
>
> - Activity: Identify the source type (group work)
>
> **Concept 3: Red Flags (10 min)**
> Common misinformation tactics:
> - Emotional manipulation (outrage, fear)
> - False urgency ("Share before it's deleted!")
> - Impersonation (fake logos, similar URLs)
> - Out of context (old photos, manipulated quotes)
>
> **Closing (5 min):**
> - Practice: Evaluate real social media post
> - Takeaway: "When in doubt, check it out"
> - Resources: Library guide to misinformation
>
> **Assessment:**
> - Exit ticket: Apply SIFT to example
> - Follow-up assignment: Evaluate sources for research paper

### Example 4: Community Needs Assessment

**Context:** Library developing strategic plan

**User:** "Our library needs to update its strategic plan. How do we assess community needs?"

**Librarian Response:**

> **Community Needs Assessment Framework:**
>
> **1. Data Sources:**
> | Source | Type | What It Tells Us |
> |--------|------|------------------|
> | Census data | Quantitative | Demographics; education; income |
> | ILS data | Quantitative | Usage patterns; popular materials |
> | Program attendance | Quantitative | Program success; gaps |
> | Community survey | Mixed | Perceptions; needs; barriers |
> | Focus groups | Qualitative | In-depth feedback; stories |
> | Stakeholder interviews | Qualitative | Partner perspectives |
> | Community forums | Qualitative | Public input; engagement |
>
> **2. Survey Design:**
> ```
> Section 1: Use of Library
> - Frequency of visits
> - Services used
> - Barriers to use
>
> Section 2: Community Priorities
> - Rate importance of various services
> - Identify top 3 priorities
> - Suggestions for improvement
>
> Section 3: Demographics
> - Age; ZIP code; languages spoken
> - Optional: Income; race/ethnicity
> ```
> - Sample size goal: 500+ responses (representative)
> - Distribution: In-library; online; community partners
> - Incentives: Entered to win gift card
>
> **3. Focus Group Protocol:**
> | Group | Recruitment | Questions |
> |-------|-------------|-----------|
> | Parents with young children | Preschools; storytime | Children's services; family needs |
> | Teens | High schools; social media | Teen space; programs; homework help |
> | Older adults | Senior centers; active living | Large print; technology help; social |
> | Non-users | Community organizations | Why don't they use library? Barriers? |
> | Small business owners | Chamber of Commerce | Business resources; meeting space |
>
> **4. Analysis Framework:**
> | Need Category | Evidence | Priority |
> |---------------|----------|----------|
> | Digital inclusion | 30% lack home broadband | High |
> | ESL services | 25% speak Spanish at home | High |
> | Workforce development | Unemployment 8% | High |
> | Youth services | Declining teen attendance | Medium |
> | Accessibility | ADA compliance gaps | High |
>
> **5. Strategic Priority Areas:**
> Based on assessment, focus on:
> 1. Digital equity (hotspots; digital skills)
> 2. Language access (Spanish services; ESL)
> 3. Economic opportunity (job search; skills training)
> 4. Youth engagement (redesigned teen space)

### Example 5: E-Resource Selection and Management

**Context:** Evaluating new database subscription

**User:** "We're considering a new genealogy database. How do we evaluate whether to subscribe?"

**Librarian Response:**

> **E-Resource Evaluation Framework:**
>
> **1. Needs Assessment:**
> | Factor | Current Situation | Gap |
> |--------|-------------------|-----|
> | User requests | 50+ requests/year for genealogy | Unmet need |
> | Existing resources | Free resources only (limited) | Coverage gap |
> | Usage of similar tools | Ancestry Library Edition heavily used | Demand demonstrated |
> | Community interest | Genealogy club with 100+ members | Active user base |
>
> **2. Product Evaluation:**
> | Criterion | Product A (Proposed) | Comparison (Ancestry) |
> |-----------|---------------------|----------------------|
> | Content | 4B records; US focus | 20B records; global | 
> | Coverage | 1650-present | 1500-present |
> | Unique content | Military; immigration | Census; DNA |
> | Interface | Modern; mobile-friendly | Dated; desktop focus |
> | Training | Webinars; tutorials | Limited |
>
> **3. Cost-Benefit Analysis:**
> | Cost Item | Amount | Notes |
> |-----------|--------|-------|
> | Annual subscription | $8,000 | Population-based pricing |
> | Staff training | $500 | 2 hours staff time |
> | Marketing | $300 | Promotional materials |
> | **Total Year 1** | **$8,800** | |
> | **Ongoing Annual** | **$8,000** | |
>
> | Benefit | Value |
> |---------|-------|
> | Usage estimate | 2,000+ sessions/year |
> | Cost per use | $4.40 |
> | User satisfaction | High (based on requests) |
> | Community value | Supports genealogy club; historical research |
>
> **4. Licensing Review:**
> | Term | Negotiation |
> |------|-------------|
> | Authorized users | Walk-in + cardholders + remote (negotiate) |
> | Simultaneous users | Unlimited (required) |
> | Interlibrary loan | Permitted (required) |
> | Course reserves | Permitted (required) |
> | Perpetual access | Post-cancellation rights (negotiate) |
> | Price cap | 5% annual increase max (negotiate) |
>
> **5. Decision:**
> **RECOMMEND SUBSCRIPTION**
> - Strong demonstrated demand
> - Reasonable cost per use
> - Unique content not duplicated elsewhere
> - Supports strategic priority (lifelong learning)
>
> **Implementation:**
> - Trial period: 30 days with usage tracking
> - Launch: Genealogy month promotion
> - Evaluation: Usage stats at 6 months; user survey
