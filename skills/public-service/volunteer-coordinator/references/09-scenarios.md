# Scenario Examples

## 9.1 Volunteer Misconduct

**User:** "A long-term volunteer was found taking photographs of clients without permission. They're denying it. What do I do?"

**Expert:**
> **Volunteer Misconduct Response:**
>
> | Step | Action |
> |------|--------|
> | 1 | **Immediate safety** | Are clients harmed? Stop the activity |
> | 2 | **Documentation** | Who saw it? When? What device? |
> | 3 | **Confrontation** | Meet with volunteer privately |
> | 4 | **Investigate** | Review photos, interview witnesses |
> | 5 | **Determine action** | Written warning, suspension, termination |
> | 6 | **Remediation** | Delete photos, notify affected clients |
> | 7 | **Policy change** | Strengthen photography policies |
>
> **Volunteer vs. Employee:**
> - Volunteers aren't employees — no employment law protections
> - But: Volunteer agreements create contractual obligations
> - Same misconduct standards apply
> - Document everything — protect the organization
>
> **Notification:**
> - Clients whose photos were taken: Yes
> - Staff: Yes
> - Board: Depends on severity
> - Legal counsel: If photos shared publicly

## 9.2 Sensitive Population Volunteers

**User:** "We run a youth mentoring program. A volunteer applicant has a 10-year-old DUI conviction. The crime is not related to working with children. Do I accept them?"

**Expert:**
> **Background Check Analysis:**
>
> | Factor | Consideration |
> |--------|---------------|
> | **Nature of offense** | DUI — not directly related to child safety |
> | **Time elapsed** | 10 years — significant rehabilitation period |
> | **Pattern of behavior** | One offense, no repeat |
> | **Supervision** | Volunteer would not be alone with children |
> | **Program rules** | What does your policy say? |
> | **Legal risk** | Negligent retention if offense relates to role |
>
> **Key Principle:**
> - Background checks inform decision, not make it
> - Consider: Is this person likely to pose a risk NOW?
> - Document your decision-making process
> - Consistent application: Don't treat similarly situated volunteers differently
> - Consult legal counsel for high-risk decisions
>
> **Best Practice:**
> - Set clear criteria in volunteer policy
> - Apply criteria consistently
> - Document everything
> - Allow appeal process

## 9.3 Volunteer No-Show Emergency

**User:** "It's the morning of our big community event. Half the volunteers didn't show up. We have 500 attendees expected. What do I do?"

**Expert:**
> **Emergency Volunteer Shortage:**
>
> | Tactic | Application |
> |--------|-------------|
> | **Call backup list** | People who were interested but not assigned |
> | **Staff surge** | Can current staff fill gaps? |
> | **Simplify roles** | Can one person cover multiple stations? |
> | **Recruit on-site** | Sign up community members who show up |
> | **Reduce scope** | Close some activities, focus on core |
>
> **Prevention for Next Time:**
> - Confirmation calls/texts 48 hours before event
> - Require backup contact info
> - Over-recruit by 20-30%
> - Day-of reminder
> - Clear cancellation policy
> - Build in shift overlap so no critical gaps
>
> **Communication:**
> - Alert all volunteers who did show up
> - Keep them informed of changes
> - Thank them for flexibility
> - Debrief after event about what worked
