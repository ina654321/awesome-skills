# Scenario Examples

## 9.1 Client Onboarding Scenario

**User:** "I just adopted a rescue dog with unknown history. How do I handle my first walk with her?"

**Expert:**
> **Analysis:**
>
> Rescue dog walks require extra preparation. Here's the recommended approach:
>
> 1. **Pre-walk:** Contact the foster/adoption coordinator for behavioral notes, triggers, and confidence level.
> 2. **First impression:** Let the dog come to you. Avoid reaching over the head or looming.
> 3. **Equipment check:** Use a front-clip harness instead of collar to prevent lunging.
> 4. **Start slow:** 15-minute walk in low-stimulation area (empty parking lot, quiet block).
> 5. **Watch body language:** Tucked tail, whale eye, lip licking = stress signals.
>
> This approach builds trust while minimizing overwhelming the dog with new stimuli.

## 9.2 Reactive Dog Scenario

**User:** "A client's dog goes absolutely wild when seeing other dogs. How do I manage pack walks?"

**Expert:**
> **Comprehensive Solution:**
>
> Reactive dogs require careful management, especially in pack situations:
>
> 1. **Assess reactivity level** (scale 1-5: from mild alert to lunging/biting)
> 2. **Never force proximity** with triggers
> 3. **Use barrier methods:** Walk reactive dog on opposite side of street
> 4. **Engage the dog's brain:** Scatter treats to redirect attention
> 5. **Build positive associations:** Systematic desensitization over weeks
>
> Key considerations:
> - Dogs rated 4-5 on reactivity scale should never be in pack walks
> - Owner must be informed of all incidents
> - Document trigger distances and recovery time
> - Consider muzzle training for severe cases

## 9.3 Heat Emergency Scenario

**User:** "The owner says 'she loves walks' but it's 92°F today. What do I do?"

**Expert:**
> **Special Handling:**
>
> In this case, standard walk procedures need modification:
>
> - **Negotiate alternative times:** Early morning (before 8am) or evening (after 8pm)
> - **Offer indoor enrichment instead:** Puzzle toys, training sessions, nose work
> - **Document the conversation** in writing with owner
> - **Know your legal rights:** Many states protect walkers who refuse unsafe conditions
>
> Consult with the NDWA Code of Ethics if owner pushes back. A dog's "love of walks" is not worth heatstroke risk.

## 9.4 Multi-Dog Household Scenario

**User:** "I need to walk three dogs from the same family at once. One is old, one is a puppy, one is high-energy."

**Expert:**
> **Special Handling:**
>
> Multi-dog walks require balancing different needs:
>
> 1. Walk puppy first when energy is high (exploration phase)
> 2. Senior dog sets the pace and determines rest stops
> 3. High-energy dog goes on a different handler hand (opposite side)
> 4. Use a 6-foot minimum between dogs initially
> 5. Split into separate walks if energy levels clash too much
>
> Document gait/pace for each dog and note if any show fatigue.

## 9.5 Off-Leash Park Incident Scenario

**User:** "A client's unleashed dog attacked my leashed dog. What do I do?"

**Expert:**
> **Incident Response:**
>
> This is a common scenario requiring immediate action:
>
> 1. Separate dogs using break-stick technique if meshed
> 2. Do NOT insert yourself physically between fighting dogs
> 3. Call owner of attacking dog for contact info
> 4. Seek veterinary care for injuries regardless of severity
> 5. File police report if bites occur (legal requirement varies by jurisdiction)
> 6. Contact your insurance provider within 24 hours
> 7. Document everything: photos, vet records, witness info
