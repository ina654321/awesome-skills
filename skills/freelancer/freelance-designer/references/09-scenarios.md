# Scenario Examples

## 9.1 Client Wants "Something Like This" (Reference Image)

**User:** "A client shows me a competitor's design and says 'I want something like this but different.' How do I respond?"

**Expert:**
> **Analysis:**
>
> This is one of the most common client requests. Handle diplomatically:
>
> 1. **Analyze the reference**: What specifically appeals to them? Color? Layout? Typography? Style?
> 2. **Ask clarifying questions**: "Which elements of this do you want me to adapt?"
> 3. **Set expectations**: "I can create something inspired by these principles while making it distinctly yours"
> 4. **Address directly**: "I can't copy this design directly (it's trademarked), but I can create something that achieves the same impact"
> 5. **Educate**: "This is actually [style]. I can explore alternatives like [alternative A] or [alternative B] that might work even better for your brand"
>
> Never reproduce competitor work directly. Always transform inspiration into original work.

## 9.2 Client Refuses to Pay Without Unspecified Changes

**User:** "Client says they won't pay the final 50% until 'it feels right.' We've done 3 revision rounds. What do I do?"

**Expert:**
> **Comprehensive Solution:**
>
> Scope creep without payment requires firm boundaries:
>
> 1. **Review the contract**: How many revisions were included?
> 2. **Document what's been done**: List every deliverable completed
> 3. **Set a deadline**: "I'm happy to make additional changes. Per our contract, additional revisions are billed at $X/hour. Would you like me to proceed?"
> 4. **Escalation options**:
>    - Pause work until payment received
>    - Offer one more focused revision round
>    - Propose a design review call to clarify vision
>
> Key considerations:
> - Don't do free work indefinitely
> - "It feels right" is not actionable feedback - ask for specific direction
> - Retain rights to work until final payment (copyright reverts on full payment)

## 9.3 Scope Creep Mid-Project

**User:** "The client now wants animated versions of every deliverable. This was never discussed. How do I handle this?"

**Expert:**
> **Special Handling:**
>
> Scope creep is a relationship test. Handle professionally:
>
> 1. **Acknowledge the request**: "Great idea - animation adds a lot of value"
> 2. **Quantify the impact**: "Animation typically adds 30-50% to project time. I can prepare an addendum for this work"
> 3. **Offer options**:
>    - Full animation of key deliverables
>    - Simplified GIF versions (quicker, less expensive)
>    - Priority: "I can animate these 2 pieces now and save the rest for a phase 2"
> 4. **Get it in writing**: Always document scope changes before proceeding
>
> Never absorb additional work for the original price. Scope creep without compensation is how projects become unprofitable.

## 9.4 Client's Internal Designer Wants to "Help"

**User:** "The client's in-house designer is inserting themselves into the project and changing my designs. The client seems fine with it."

**Expert:**
> **Special Handling:**
>
> When clients involve third parties, protect your work and relationships:
>
> 1. **Clarify roles**: "I want to make sure we work together effectively. Should I direct feedback to [designer] or directly to you?"
> 2. **Set boundaries**: "I'm happy to incorporate feedback, but revisions should come through a single point of contact to avoid conflicting direction"
> 3. **Credit the work**: "I'm glad the in-house team finds value in the work. As the original designer, I'd appreciate being credited appropriately"
> 4. **Protect final say**: "Ultimately, design decisions should be coordinated through me to ensure consistency"
>
> Document in writing if a third party will be providing direction. Multiple cooks spoil the design.

## 9.5 Client Uses Work Without Paying Final Invoice

**User:** "I found my design being used on their website but they haven't paid the final invoice. What do I do?"

**Expert:**
> **Special Handling:**
>
> This is a serious breach. Take action immediately:
>
> 1. **Document everything**: Screenshot the usage, date you found it
> 2. **Send formal notice**: "Per our contract, final payment was due on [date]. Usage of deliverables prior to payment constitutes a breach of contract"
> 3. **Offer resolution window**: 48-72 hours to pay or remove work
> 4. **Consult attorney if unresolved**: DMCA takedown, cease-and-desist, or small claims court
>
> Prevention is key: Watermark previews, include "property of" in file metadata, send PDF invoices with explicit terms, and collect larger deposits upfront (50% minimum).
