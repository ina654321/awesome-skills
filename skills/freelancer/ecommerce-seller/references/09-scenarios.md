# Scenario Examples

## 9.1 Item Arrives Damaged - Buyer Opens Case

**User:** "A buyer says the item arrived broken but the tracking shows it was delivered intact. What do I do?"

**Expert:**
> **Analysis:**
>
> Damaged-in-transit claims require careful handling:
>
> 1. **Ask for photos** - request clear images of both product and packaging damage
> 2. **Check if packaging was adequate** - was the item protected for shipping conditions?
> 3. **Review carrier footage** if available (request from carrier)
> 4. **Offer solution first** - buyer satisfaction often prevents escalation
>
> Acceptable resolutions:
> - Full refund (if proof of damage is clear)
> - Partial refund (if item is partially functional)
> - Free replacement (if you have stock)
>
> If no proof of damage: "I'm sorry for the inconvenience. Unfortunately, I cannot process a refund without evidence of damage on arrival."

## 9.2 Buyer Claims Item Not as Described

**User:** "A buyer is saying the item color is different from photos. I photographed the actual product. What do I do?"

**Expert:**
> **Comprehensive Solution:**
>
> "Not as described" disputes require documentation:
>
> 1. **Respond with your evidence**: original photos, accurate description, measurements
> 2. **Acknowledge their concern** professionally
> 3. **Offer resolution options**:
>    - Return for full refund (you pay return shipping if you made error)
>    - Partial refund to compensate perceived difference
>    - Replacement with accurate photos provided
>
> Key considerations:
> - Color variation is common with natural materials or lighting differences
> - Always photograph with color calibration reference when possible
> - Update listing photos if you discover discrepancy
> - If buyer escalates: platform usually sides with buyer for color mismatch

## 9.3 Intellectual Property Complaint

**User:** "I received an IP complaint on my listing. I didn't know the design was trademarked. What do I do?"

**Expert:**
> **Special Handling:**
>
> IP complaints are serious and can result in account suspension:
>
> - **Remove the listing immediately** - do not relist until resolved
> - **Do not contact the complainant** directly
> - **If you believe the complaint is wrong**: file a counter-notice through the platform
> - **Going forward**: only sell products you have authorization to sell
> - **Consult an IP attorney** if you face significant financial loss
>
> Consult the USPTO TESS database (tess2.uspto.gov) before sourcing products to verify trademarks.

## 9.4 Competitor Buying Stock to Leave Negative Reviews

**User:** "A buyer with no purchase history bought 5 items and left 1-star reviews across all of them. I suspect a competitor."

**Expert:**
> **Special Handling:**
>
> Suspicious review patterns can be addressed:
>
> - **Report to platform** - use the "unusual voting activity" or review manipulation report option
> - **Document evidence** - similar review patterns, accounts created same date, same IP
> - **Respond publicly** - politely note that reviews appear to be from non-customers
> - **Do not retaliate** - do not buy reviews or attack the competitor
> - **Focus on legitimate buyers** - encourage real customers to leave reviews
>
> Most platforms have systems to detect and remove coordinated fake reviews. Patience and legitimate growth is the only long-term solution.

## 9.5 Tax Compliance Question

**User:** "I've made $15,000 selling on eBay this year. Do I need to pay taxes?"

**Expert:**
> **Special Handling:**
>
> Tax obligations for ecommerce sellers:
>
> - **Report all income** over $600 per platform receives 1099 form
> - **Self-employment tax** applies to net profit (15.3% Social Security + Medicare)
> - **Track business expenses** - shipping, supplies, platform fees, home office
> - **Quarterly estimated taxes** due if you owe over $1,000/year
> - **Keep records** - bank statements, invoices, shipping receipts for 3-7 years
>
> Consult a CPA familiar with ecommerce for your specific situation. Setting aside 25-30% of gross profit is a common guideline.
