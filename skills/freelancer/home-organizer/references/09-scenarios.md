# Scenario Examples

## 9.1 Client Becomes Emotional During Sorting

**User:** "A client broke down crying when sorting through family photos. They're now refusing to continue the session. What do I do?"

**Expert:**
> **Analysis:**
>
> Emotional responses to possessions are common and should be handled with empathy:
>
> 1. **Stop immediately**: Never push through emotional distress
> 2. **Acknowledge feelings**: "This makes complete sense. These memories are precious"
> 3. **Offer space**: "Would you like some time alone, or should I step out of the room?"
> 4. **Don't minimize**: Avoid "it's just a photo" or "you have plenty more"
> 5. **Pivot activity**: Suggest moving to a different category
> 6. **End session if needed**: "I think we've accomplished a lot today. Let's pick this up next time"
>
> Know your limits: If grief, trauma, or hoarding disorder is significantly impacting the session, consider referring to a therapist or hoarding specialist.

## 9.2 Client Insists on Keeping Everything

**User:** "My client says they might use every single item 'someday' and won't agree to discard anything. How do I proceed?"

**Expert:**
> **Comprehensive Solution:**
>
> When decluttering hits a wall:
>
> 1. **Understand the fear**: "Can you help me understand what concerns you about letting this go?"
> 2. **Use the "someday" test**: "If you could only keep 10 things from this category, which would they be?"
> 3. **Offer alternatives**: "What if we put these in a box and store it in the garage for 3 months? If you haven't needed anything from it, we can revisit"
> 4. **Shift the goal**: Focus on organization, not elimination, if client isn't ready
> 5. **Document the boundary**: "I respect your decisions about your belongings. My role is to help you organize what's important to you"
>
> Key considerations:
> - You cannot force someone to discard
> - Building trust is more important than immediate results
> - Chronic disorganization may require ongoing coaching

## 9.3 Hidden Hoarding Discovery Mid-Project

**User:** "During a kitchen project, I discovered the client has been hiding bags of garbage and rotting food. The situation is worse than initially disclosed."

**Expert:**
> **Special Handling:**
>
> Discovered hoarding requires immediate recalibration:
>
> 1. **Stop work**: This is beyond standard organizing
> 2. **Document**: Take photos (if safe) for assessment purposes
> 3. **Communicate privately**: "I found some items that concern me. Can we talk about what's been happening?"
> 4. **Assess safety**: Are there health hazards? Blocked exits? Fire hazards?
> 5. **Recommend professional help**: "Based on what I'm seeing, I'd recommend working with a therapist who specializes in this area"
> 6. **Decide on engagement**: Can you continue at a reduced scope, or should you step back entirely?
>
> Biohazard situations: Do not handle without proper PPE. Recommend professional cleaning service.

## 9.4 Client's Family Member Interfering

**User:** "The client's adult child keeps undoing the organizing work and criticizing the process. The client seems intimidated."

**Expert:**
> **Special Handling:**
>
> Family interference complicates professional engagement:
>
> 1. **Address with client privately**: "I've noticed [child's name] has opinions about the organizing. How do you want me to handle that?"
> 2. **Set boundaries**: "My sessions are focused on your goals. Family members are welcome to share feedback directly with you, but I need clear direction from you"
> 3. **Invite adult child**: If appropriate, include them in one session with client consent
> 4. **Respect the family system**: You can't change family dynamics
> 5. **Reassess engagement**: If interference prevents progress, may need to end professional relationship
>
> Document all communications with third parties. Always take direction from the paying client.

## 9.5 Safety Hazard Discovered

**User:** "I found a significant mouse infestation during a basement cleanout. There are droppings everywhere. What do I do?"

**Expert:**
> **Special Handling:**
>
> Health hazard discovered requires immediate action:
>
> 1. **Stop work immediately**: Do not continue in contaminated area
> 2. **Notify client**: "I've discovered a pest issue that needs professional remediation before I can continue"
> 3. **Do not clean up**: Pest droppings require hazmat protocol
> 4. **Recommend pest control**: Professional exterminator required
> 5. **Document**: Note the extent of the problem for your records
> 6. **Renegotiate scope**: Once remediated, reassess whether to continue
>
> Worker safety comes first. You are not required to work in unsafe conditions. Discuss safety concerns with clients upfront and include in service agreement.
