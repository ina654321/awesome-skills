# Scenario Examples

## 9.1 Common Scenario: Fire in Engine Room During Watch

**User:** You smell smoke coming from the engine room and hear the fire alarm. The engineer is off-watch. What do you do?

**Expert:**
> **Analysis:**
> Engine room fire is a serious emergency requiring immediate action:
>
> **Immediate actions (first 30 seconds):**
> 1. **Sound emergency alarm** (3 long bells or general alarm) — alert the entire crew
> 2. **Call the chief engineer** immediately via shipboard communication
> 3. **Do not enter the engine room alone** — oxygen depletion and toxic gases are invisible killers
>
> **Muster to engine room fire station:**
> 1. Don full PPE: fire suit, SCBA (self-contained breathing apparatus)
> 2. Establish team of minimum 2 people: one to fight, one to standby
> 3. Review fire plan: know fire damper locations, fixed firefighting systems
> 4. Attempt to identify the fire source through observation port or partial door opening
>
> **Fixed firefighting systems:**
> 1. CO2 system: engine room typically has fixed CO2 — 30% oxygen reduction
> 2. Foam system: deck foam system for oil fires
> 3. Water mist: newer vessels have water mist flooding
> 4. Do NOT use water on electrical fires — use CO2 or foam
>
> **Command structure:**
> 1. Master (Captain) assumes command of emergency
> 2. Chief Engineer commands firefighting operations in engine room
> 3. Chief Officer manages muster and external coordination
> 4. You follow orders from senior officers — do not act independently
>
> **After the fire:**
> 1. Ventilate thoroughly before re-entering
> 2. Assess damage
> 3. File incident report: cause, response, damage, corrective action
> 4. Drug/alcohol test required for all involved personnel (per USCG regulations)

## 9.2 Complex Scenario: Man Overboard During Cargo Operations

**User:** A crew member fell from the deck into the water during cargo ops at night. There is cargo operation ongoing and you cannot immediately stop. What do you do?

**Expert:**
> **Comprehensive Solution:**
> Every second counts in MOB recovery. Speed of response determines survival:
>
> **Immediate actions (first 60 seconds):**
> 1. **SHOUT "MAN OVERBOARD STARBOARD/PORT"** — establish direction
> 2. **THROW the nearest life ring/buoys** — immediately, do not wait
> 3. **Assign someone to POINT at the person in water** — maintain visual contact
> 4. **Press MOB button on GPS** — automatically marks the position (ERS/WHMS)
> 5. **CALL the Captain immediately** — Master takes command
>
> **Maintaining visual contact:**
> 1. Never take eyes off the person in water — even for a second
> 2. Use night-vision equipment if available
> 3. Request additional lighting from deck (flashlights, searchlights)
>
> **Recovery operations:**
> 1. **Do NOT stop cargo ops without Captain's order** — stopping ballast or cargo ops suddenly can destabilize the vessel
> 2. Master will order maneuvering: Williamson Turn (most common) or Anderson Turn
> 3. Prepare rescue boat: quick-release davit, rescue harness, warm blankets
> 4. Have crew on standby with reaching/pulling equipment
>
> **In water survival time:**
> - 32°F water: 15–45 minutes survival
> - 50°F water: 1–3 hours survival
> - 60°F water: 2–12 hours survival
> - Wearing survival suit extends survival to 6+ hours in any temperature
>
> **Post-recovery:**
> 1. Treat for hypothermia: remove wet clothing, warm blankets, no alcohol
> 2. Medical evaluation: all MOB survivors need medical assessment
> 3. File incident report
> 4. Review prevention: why did the person fall? Update safety procedures

## 9.3 Edge Case: Drug/Alcohol Test Required After Safety Incident

**User:** The mate on watch was involved in a near-collision. The Captain ordered drug/alcohol testing but the mate refused, saying it violates their rights. What do you do?

**Expert:**
> **Special Handling:**
>
> Substance abuse testing is federally mandated in the maritime industry:
>
> **Legal framework:**
> 1. **33 CFR Part 95** — Required for reasonable cause or involvement in marine casualty
> 2. **USCG Authority:** The Coast Guard can require testing as part of their investigation
> 3. **Refusal is a violation:** A mate who refuses a required test can lose their credentials
>
> **Captain's authority:**
> 1. Captain has full authority over vessel safety — this includes requiring tests
> 2. A refusal should be documented: written statement, witness signatures
> 3. The Captain should contact USCG Marine Inspection Office for guidance
>
> **If the mate refuses:**
> 1. **Remove the mate from duty immediately** — safety of vessel comes first
> 2. **Document the refusal** in writing with date, time, witnesses
> 3. **Notify USCG** — they will pursue the credential action
> 4. **Arrange for drug/alcohol testing anyway** — contact certified lab
> 5. **Hold the mate's credentials** pending investigation if authorized
>
> **Post-incident procedure:**
> 1. Conduct post-incident drug/alcohol test within 32 hours (mandatory minimum)
> 2. File the incident report with USCG
> 3. Cooperate with USCG investigation
> 4. Document all observations: was the mate impaired before or after the incident?
>
> **Reasonable cause indicators:**
> - Slurred speech, unsteady gait, smell of alcohol
> - Unusual behavior, drowsiness, difficulty with routine tasks
> - Witness reports of substance use
