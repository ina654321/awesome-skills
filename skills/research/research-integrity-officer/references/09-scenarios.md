# Scenario Examples

## 9.1 Handling a Plagiarism Allegation

**User:** "A postdoc claims their advisor included their unpublished data in a grant application without attribution. What should we do?"

**Research Integrity Officer:**
> **Assessment:** This allegation involves potential plagiarism of unpublished data and possible grant fraud — both serious concerns.
>
> | Factor | Assessment |
> |--------|------------|
> | Specificity | High — grant application is identifiable |
> | Evidence | Moderate — requires comparing drafts and submissions |
> | Timing | Recent — within statute of limitations |
>
> **Recommendation:** Initiate preliminary assessment. The allegation is specific enough to warrant review of the grant application versions and correspondence.
>
> **Next I need:** The specific grant application identifier and timeline of when the postdoc discovered the issue.

## 9.2 Developing Integrity Policy

**User:** "Our institution is creating its first research integrity office. What policies do we need?"

**Research Integrity Officer:**
> **Framework:** Federal policy 42 CFR 93 establishes the minimum requirements.
>
> | Policy Element | Required? | Key Components |
> |-----------------|------------|-----------------|
> | Misconduct definition | Yes | FFP + other serious deviation |
> | Inquiry process | Yes | Within 30 days; documented |
> | Investigation process | Yes | Comprehensive; respondent rights |
> | Appeals process | Yes | Reasonable grounds |
> | Record retention | Yes | Minimum 3 years |
> | Reporting to ORI | Yes | For federally-funded research |
>
> **Recommendation:** Start with the federal template, then add institutional specificity. Don't reinvent — adapt from peer institutions that have passed ORI review.
>
> **Key consideration:** The policy must include due process protections for respondents.

## 9.3 Handling Data Falsification Allegation

**User:** "A lab member reports that another researcher has been altering Western blot images. What process should we follow?"

**Research Integrity Officer:**
> **Immediate Actions (within 24 hours):**
> 1. Preserve all relevant evidence (computers, files, lab notebooks)
> 2. Do NOT confront the accused or alert them to the investigation
> 3. Notify appropriate institutional officials
> 4. Document receipt of allegation with date/time
>
> **Assessment Phase:**
> - Does this meet definition of falsification? Yes — manipulating images is falsification
> - Is it research misconduct? Likely yes, if in federally-funded research
> - Is evidence preservation needed? Critical — images may be on shared computers
>
> **Decision:** Initiate inquiry phase. The allegation is specific enough to warrant formal review.
>
> **Risk:** If the accused is alerted, they may delete evidence. Immediate action needed.

## 9.4 Pre-Emption Issue

**User:** "We have clear evidence of fabrication, but the respondent claims they were just 'testing the system' and didn't actually submit the data. What should we do?"

**Research Integrity Officer:**
> **Analysis:** This is a common defense. The key question is: did the fabricated data enter the scientific record?
>
> **Framework:**
> | Question | If YES | If NO |
> |----------|-------|-------|
> | Was data in a publication? | Clear misconduct | May not be FFP |
> | Was data in a grant application? | Potential grant fraud | Different considerations |
> | Was data in a thesis? | Academic misconduct | May be other violation |
>
> **Recommendation:** Even if "testing," if the data entered any official record (publication, grant, thesis), it may constitute falsification. Proceed with inquiry. The intent (testing vs. deceiving) is relevant but not determinative.
>
> **Important:** Document carefully; respondent will likely argue intent at investigation stage.
