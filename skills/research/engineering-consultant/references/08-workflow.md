# Engineering Consultant Workflow Reference

Common research workflow issues and best practices in engineering consulting engagements.

## Project Scoping & Proposal Development

- **Problem**: Scope defined too narrowly by client, leading to change orders and disputes.
  - **Issue**: Clients often underestimate project complexity, especially for existing structures or site conditions.
  - **Reference**: NSPE guidelines recommend detailed scope definitions including assumptions, exclusions, and deliverables. [nspe.org](https://nspe.org)

- **Problem**: Fee estimation without adequate site investigation or document review.
  - **Issue**: Historical as-built drawings may not reflect current conditions; invasive investigation adds cost.
  - **Reference**: ASCE guidelines recommend site reconnaissance and document review as standard practice before fee proposals.

- **Problem**: Inadequate communication of deliverables and report formats.
  - **Issue**: Clients expect design documents while proposals specify analysis reports; mismatched expectations.
  - **Reference**: ACEC contract standards emphasize clear deliverable lists in proposal phase. [acec.org](https://acec.org)

## Client Intake & Conflict of Interest

- **Problem**: Starting work without a signed contract or executed agreement.
  - **Issue**: Verbal agreements lead to scope disputes; professional liability exposure begins with project start.
  - **Reference**: NSPE Code of Ethics requires written agreements before commencing professional services.

- **Problem**: Conflicts of interest not identified or disclosed.
  - **Issue**: Working for multiple parties on the same project (owner, contractor, A/E) without disclosure.
  - **Reference**: NSPE Ethics Reference includes conflict-of-interest guidelines. [nspe.org](https://nspe.org)

## Design Review & Quality Assurance

- **Problem**: Code compliance not verified against all applicable jurisdictions.
  - **Issue**: Projects spanning multiple municipalities or state/federal jurisdictions face conflicting requirements.
  - **Reference**: ICC provides jurisdiction-specific code adoption tracking; FHWA for federally-funded transportation projects.

- **Problem**: Calculations not independently checked by a second licensed engineer.
  - **Issue**: Internal QA reviews may not catch fundamental errors; peer review requirements vary by state.
  - **Reference**: Many state licensing boards require independent calculation review for specific project types.

- **Problem**: Specifications referencing outdated standards or withdrawn ASTM/AASHTO specifications.
  - **Issue**: Standard organizations update specifications periodically; project specifications must reference current editions.
  - **Reference**: ASTM and AASHTO publish new editions annually; specifications should include revision date.

## Coordination with Other Disciplines

- **Problem**: Structural, MEP, and architectural drawings not coordinated.
  - **Issue**: Clash detection failures lead to field changes; coordination reviews often skipped on small projects.
  - **Reference**: AIA and EJCDC provide coordination requirements; BIM workflows reduce coordination failures.

- **Problem**: Delegated design elements (e.g., metal decking, precast concrete) not fully coordinated with prime engineer.
  - **Issue**: Delegated designs approved without review of loads and interface conditions.
  - **Reference**: ASCE 7 and IBC Chapter 17 specify special inspection and delegated design requirements.

## Construction Phase Issues

- **Problem**: Requests for Information (RFIs) not answered within contractual timeframes.
  - **Issue**: Construction delays attributed to consultant; claims for delay damages.
  - **Reference**: EJCDC C-700 and AIA A201 define RFI response requirements.

- **Problem**: Submittal review beyond contractual timeframes.
  - **Issue**: Many contracts set 14-day review periods; consultants with high workloads miss deadlines.
  - **Reference**: Standard contract forms (EJCDC, AIA) specify review periods and default provisions.

- **Problem**: Site visits not documented or observations not communicated in writing.
  - **Issue**: Verbal site observations disputed during litigation; contractor actions not recorded.
  - **Reference**: Professional liability insurers recommend written site visit reports for every visit.

## Report & Deliverable Standards

- **Problem**: Reports missing limitations, assumptions, and basis of analysis.
  - **Issue**: Clients use reports for purposes beyond the original scope; liability for misuse.
  - **Reference**: NSPE and ASCE recommend explicit limitations sections in all professional reports.

- **Problem**: Sealed drawings not reviewed before stamping.
  - **Issue**: Stamping a drawing constitutes professional certification; errors on sealed documents are serious liability.
  - **Reference**: State licensing board rules require that seals attest to personal review and responsible charge.

## Project Closeout & Record Retention

- **Problem**: Final deliverables not organized or transmitted in required format.
  - **Issue**: Clients cannot locate documents; as-built conditions not captured.
  - **Reference**: Project closeout checklists from AIA and EJCDC provide deliverable checklists.

- **Problem**: Project records not retained for required period.
  - **Issue**: Professional liability claims can arise years after project completion; statutes of repose vary by state.
  - **Reference**: NSPE recommends 10+ year retention; some project types (structural, infrastructure) warrant longer periods.
