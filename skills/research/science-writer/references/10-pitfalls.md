# Common Pitfalls & Anti-Patterns

## 10.1 Anti-Patterns in Science Writing

| # | Anti-Pattern| Severity| Quick Fix|
|---|----------------------|-----------------|---------------------|
| 1 | **Using "breakthrough" without justification** | 🔴 High | Reserve for truly paradigm-shifting findings; use "new," "study finds" |
| 2 | **Implying causation from correlation** | 🔴 High | Say "linked to" or "associated with," not "causes" or "prevents" |
| 3 | **Overloading with jargon** | 🟡 Medium | One technical term per paragraph; define immediately |
| 4 | **Missing the human element** | 🟡 Medium | Include at least one researcher quote or human application |
| 5 | **Burying the lead** | 🟡 Medium | First paragraph should stand alone as complete story |

## 10.2 Problematic Phrases

| ❌ Avoid | ✅ Use Instead |
|----------|----------------|
| "This revolutionary breakthrough will cure cancer." | "A new treatment approach shows promise for some cancer patients" |
| "Coffee extends your life." | "Coffee consumption is associated with lower mortality risk" |
| "Scientists proved..." | "A study provides evidence..." |

## 10.3 Accuracy Mistakes

### Common Problems
- Citing correlation as causation
- Not noting study limitations
- Overstating implications
- Quoting preprints as established
- Missing conflicts of interest

### Fact-Check Checklist
- [ ] Verified all numbers
- [ ] Checked source methodology
- [ ] Noted limitations
- [ ] Included expert perspective
- [ ] Used appropriate hedging

## 10.4 Style Issues

### Common Mistakes
- Passive voice
- Long, complex sentences
- Technical jargon
- Missing context
- Weak headlines

### Best Practices
- Lead with the conclusion
- Use active voice
- Short paragraphs
- One idea per sentence
- Headline should stand alone
