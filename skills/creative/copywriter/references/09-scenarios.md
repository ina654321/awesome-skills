# Scenario Examples

## 9.1 Client Wants "More Professional" Copy

**User:** "My client says the copy needs to be 'more professional' but won't give specific feedback. How do I diagnose what they actually want?"

**Expert:**
> **Analysis:**
>
> "More professional" is a vague request. Diagnose the real issue:
>
> 1. **Ask for examples**: "Could you share 2-3 pieces of copy you consider professional?"
> 2. **Explore specific concerns**: Is it vocabulary level? Tone? Format? Structure?
> 3. **Consider alternatives**: Sometimes "professional" means "shorter" or "more concise"
> 4. **Present options**: Show 2-3 variations with different approaches
>
> Common interpretations:
> - "Professional" = Less conversational, more formal vocabulary
> - "Professional" = Industry jargon
> - "Professional" = Longer explanations
> - "Professional" = More structure (headers, bullets)
> - "Professional" = Less emotional, more logical

## 9.2 Product Has No Obvious Differentiator

**User:** "I'm writing copy for a commodity product that is essentially the same as competitors. How do I create compelling copy?"

**Expert:**
> **Comprehensive Solution:**
>
> Commoditized products require creative differentiation:
>
> 1. **Find the story**: Origin, founder passion, sourcing, process
> 2. **Service differentiation**: Guarantees, support, relationship
> 3. **Audience connection**: Speak to a specific persona competitors ignore
> 4. **Micro-positioning**: "The best for [specific use case]"
> 5. **Trust builders**: Social proof, credentials, case studies
> 6. **Emotional angle**: Commodities can still have emotional resonance
>
> Example: Bottled water (commodity) became differentiated through:
> - Fiji: " artesian water, untouched by civilization"
> - Smartwater: Celebrity endorsement, hydration science
> - Liquid Death: Punk rock anti-establishment humor

## 9.3 Target Audience Is Everyone

**User:** "The client says their product is for 'anyone who wants to lose weight' or 'every small business owner.' How do I write targeted copy?"

**Expert:**
> **Special Handling:**
>
> Universal audiences = no emotional connection:
>
> 1. **Challenge the assumption**: "Every successful campaign I've run has been built around a specific audience. Who are your BEST customers?"
> 2. **Create personas**: Help client visualize specific people
> 3. **Narrow to win**: "If you could only reach 1,000 people this year, who would they be?"
> 4. **Build out from core**: Write for primary persona, add secondary variations
>
> The narrower the targeting, the more compelling the copy. Everyone is not your audience. Who is?

## 9.4 Client Wants to Use All the Copy

**User:** "The client wants to use every word I've written instead of selecting the best options. How do I advise them?"

**Expert:**
> **Special Handling:**
>
> Length vs. effectiveness:
>
> 1. **Explain conversion principles**: More words = more friction
> 2. **A/B test concept**: "Let's test one short, punchy version against the longer one"
> 3. **Show data**: Landing page benchmarks, email engagement rates
> 4. **Offer compromise**: Landing page gets short version; email gets longer version
> 5. **Respect expertise**: "My job is to make this effective. Your job is to let me"
>
> If client insists on longer version: deliver what they want, note your recommendation in writing, let data decide.

## 9.5 Competitor Copy Is Simply Better

**User:** "I'm hired to write copy that's better than a competitor, but their copy is actually quite good. How do I approach this?"

**Expert:**
> **Special Handling:**
>
> Don't copy competitors; outmaneuver them:
>
> 1. **Analyze what makes their copy work**: Structure, tone, proof points
> 2. **Find their blind spots**: Who are they NOT speaking to?
> 3. **Different angles**: Take a contrarian position, different frame
> 4. **Test unconventional formats**: If they use lists, try narrative
> 5. **Prove claims differently**: Same claim, better evidence
>
> Good competitors validate the market. Your job is to win the specific audience segments they may be neglecting.
