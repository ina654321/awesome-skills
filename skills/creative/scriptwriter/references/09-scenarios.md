# Scenario Examples

## 9.1 Writer's Block on Act Two

**User:** "I have a great opening and the ending is clear, but I'm stuck in the middle. I have no idea what happens next. What do I do?"

**Expert:**
> **Analysis:**
>
> The "sagging middle" is the most common structural problem:
>
> 1. **Identify your midpoint**: The halfway point should have a major revelation or reversal
> 2. **List complications**: What could make the protagonist's goal harder?
> 3. **Raise stakes**: What happens if they fail? Who else is affected?
> 4. **Add a subplot**: Introduce a B-story that mirrors the main conflict
> 5. **Use the "worst thing possible" test**: What is the worst thing that could happen right now?
> 6. **Try the "random input" technique**: Open a book, point to a word, and incorporate it
>
> The middle is where characters are tested. If your protagonist isn't struggling, neither is the audience.

## 9.2 Client Wants Changes That Contradict the Story Logic

**User:** "A producer wants me to change the ending, but the change doesn't make story sense. How do I advocate for the story while keeping the client happy?"

**Expert:**
> **Comprehensive Solution:**
>
> Professional negotiation:
>
> 1. **Ask why**: "I'd love to understand what you're hoping the ending achieves"
> 2. **Explain the logic**: "The ending works because [specific story logic]. If we change it, we lose [specific element]"
> 3. **Offer alternatives**: "What if we adjusted the third act to deliver [their goal] while maintaining [your essential element]?"
> 4. **Know your leverage**: WGA has specific provisions about creative differences
> 5. **Pick your battles**: If it's not a core element, accommodate; if it breaks the story, push back
>
> Producers buy creative vision but also want commercial appeal. Help them get both.

## 9.3 Dialogue Sounds Flat or Unnatural

**User:** "The story works but the dialogue sounds stilted or unnatural when read. How do I fix this?"

**Expert:**
> **Special Handling:**
>
> Dialogue polish techniques:
>
> 1. **Read aloud**: If you stumble speaking it, rewrite it
> 2. **Remove filler**: Cut 30% of dialogue - most first draft dialogue is overwritten
> 3. **Add subtext**: Characters should mean more than they say
> 4. **Distinguish voices**: Give each character unique speech patterns
> 5. **Kill the expository dialogue**: Find other ways to convey information
> 6. **Use interruptions**: Real conversation rarely flows perfectly
> 7. **Listen to how people talk**: Record conversations (with permission), transcribe, study
>
> Natural dialogue is about what characters DON'T say as much as what they do.

## 9.4 Multiple Projects, Tight Deadlines

**User:** "I have three projects due in the same month. The deadlines were set assuming I'd work 16-hour days. How do I handle this?"

**Expert:**
> **Special Handling:**
>
> Overcommitment requires proactive management:
>
> 1. **Communicate immediately**: Don't wait until you miss deadlines
> 2. **Negotiate priorities**: "Project A is most urgent - which should I prioritize?"
> 3. **Negotiate timelines**: "I can deliver B and C in Week 2 instead of Week 1"
> 4. **Offer solutions**: "I can bring in a trusted colleague for one project"
> 5. **Reassess future commitments**: Set better boundaries going forward
>
> Never promise what you can't deliver. Your reputation is built on reliability.

## 9.5 Character Motivation Feels Thin

**User:** "My protagonist does things but I'm not sure WHY they do them. The motivation feels thin. How do I fix this?"

**Expert:**
> **Special Handling:**
>
> Character motivation depth:
>
> 1. **The wound**: What happened to this character before the story begins? (Backstory)
> 2. **The want**: What do they consciously pursue? (External goal)
> 3. **The need**: What do they unconsciously need to learn? (Internal journey)
> 4. **The flaw**: What personal weakness must they overcome? (Character arc)
> 5. **The lie**: What does the character believe that's wrong? (Thematic)
>
> When motivation is thin, go deeper into backstory. Characters act based on who they ARE, not who the plot needs them to be.
