# Scenario Examples

## 9.1 Client Wants Changes After Approval

**User:** "I delivered a recording and the client approved it. Now they want significant changes to the tone. What do I do?"

**Expert:**
> **Analysis:**
>
> Scope and approval expectations:
>
> 1. **Check your contract**: What were the revision terms?
> 2. **Distinguish scope from changes**: Is this "make the character sadder" (minor) or "rewrite the entire read" (major)?
> 3. **Professional courtesy**: Small adjustments should be accommodated
> 4. **Quote for significant changes**: "I'd be happy to re-record. Since this is a significant departure from the approved direction, I'd need to quote this as additional work"
> 5. **Document approval in writing**: Email confirmation protects both parties
>
> Most industry standard: 1-2 minor revision passes included. Major changes = additional session fee.

## 9.2 Client Claims You Missed Direction

**User:** "The client says the read doesn't match the direction they gave, but I followed the script notes. How do I handle this?"

**Expert:**
> **Comprehensive Solution:**
>
> Direction misalignment requires diplomacy:
>
> 1. **Listen carefully to their concern**: Are they right?
> 2. **Review your notes**: Did you miss something?
> 3. **Check communication history**: Was direction given in writing?
> 4. **Offer solution**: "I'm happy to do another take that leans more toward [their interpretation]"
> 5. **Document going forward**: Request written direction with specific examples
>
> Key considerations:
> - Reputation matters more than winning an argument
> - If you're clearly right, offer to demonstrate
> - If unclear, err on side of accommodating client

## 9.3 Microphone Issues Mid-Session

**User:** "During a paid recording session, my microphone starts producing crackling sounds. What do I do?"

**Expert:**
> **Special Handling:**
>
> Technical issues during paid sessions:
>
> 1. **Stop recording immediately**: Don't record bad audio
> 2. **Troubleshoot quickly**: Check cable connection, USB port, interface
> 3. **Offer alternatives**:
>    - Take 5 minutes to fix
>    - Use backup microphone if available
>    - Record the affected lines after resolving
> 4. **Communicate with client**: "I'm experiencing a technical issue and need a few minutes to resolve it"
> 5. **Prevent recurrence**: Test all equipment before every session
>
> Prevention: Arrive early, test everything, have backup equipment available.

## 9.4 Identity vs. Character Confusion

**User:** "A client keeps referring to me by my character's name during a long project. It's starting to feel uncomfortable. How do I address this?"

**Expert:**
> **Special Handling:**
>
> Professional boundaries in character work:
>
> 1. **Address it politely**: "I go by [Your Name] off-mic. Would you mind using that when we're not recording?"
> 2. **Understand their intent**: Sometimes it's immersion, not disrespect
> 3. **Be firm if needed**: "I appreciate your enthusiasm for the character, but I prefer we keep things professional"
> 4. **Escalate if needed**: Contact the client's manager or your agent
> 5. **Include preferences in future contracts**: "Off-mic, please address me by my given name"
>
> Your identity as a professional is separate from the characters you play.

## 9.5 Rates Below Industry Standard

**User:** "A potential client offers $50 for a project that would normally take 3 hours and should pay $600. How do I decline professionally?"

**Expert:**
> **Special Handling:**
>
> Undervalued offers require professional response:
>
> 1. **Don't burn the bridge**: "I appreciate you thinking of me for this project"
> 2. **State your rate professionally**: "My standard rate for this type of work is $X. I may be able to offer a small discount for an ongoing relationship"
> 3. **Explain value**: "This rate reflects the professional equipment, studio time, editing, and expertise required for quality output"
> 4. **Offer alternatives**: "I could offer a reduced scope version for $Y if budget is a concern"
> 5. **Know your minimum**: Never go below your break-even rate
>
> You set the standard for how clients value your work. Discounting significantly signals you're not confident in your value.
