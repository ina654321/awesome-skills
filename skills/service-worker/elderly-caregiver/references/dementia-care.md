# Dementia Care Reference Guide

## Understanding Dementia

### Types of Dementia

| Type | Percentage | Key Characteristics |
|------|-----------|---------------------|
| **Alzheimer's Disease** | 60-80% | Gradual memory loss, language difficulties, disorientation |
| **Vascular Dementia** | 10-20% | Stepwise decline, executive dysfunction, post-stroke |
| **Lewy Body Dementia** | 5-10% | Fluctuating cognition, visual hallucinations, parkinsonism |
| **Frontotemporal** | 5-10% | Personality/behavior changes, language impairment |
| **Mixed Dementia** | 10-20% | Combination of Alzheimer's + vascular pathology |

### Stages of Dementia

#### Early Stage (Mild)
**MMSE Score:** 20-26

**Characteristics:**
- Memory lapses (names, recent events, word-finding)
- Difficulty with complex tasks (finances, planning)
- Mood changes, anxiety about symptoms
- Still independent in most ADLs

**Care Approach:**
- Maintain routines and familiar environments
- Introduce memory aids (calendars, labels, alarms)
- Encourage continued activities and socialization
- Begin advance care planning conversations
- Monitor driving safety

**Activity Ideas:**
- Crossword puzzles, word games
- Reading, book clubs
- Walking, gentle exercise
- Card games, board games
- Music from their era
- Gardening, light household tasks

---

#### Middle Stage (Moderate)
**MMSE Score:** 10-19

**Characteristics:**
- Significant memory loss (may not recognize family)
- Communication difficulties
- Wandering, sundowning, sleep disturbances
- Personal care needs increase
- Behavioral symptoms (agitation, suspicion, repetition)

**Care Approach:**
- Structured daily routine essential
- Simplify tasks, break into steps
- Use visual cues and demonstrations
- Validation therapy for distress
- Safety modifications (wandering prevention)
- Family support and education critical

**Activity Ideas:**
- Simple crafts (painting, collage)
- Music therapy and sing-alongs
- Reminiscence activities (photo albums)
- Folding laundry, sorting items
- Simple cooking tasks
- Sensory stimulation (textures, scents)

**Behavior Management:**

| Behavior | Possible Cause | Intervention |
|----------|---------------|--------------|
| **Wandering** | Boredom, searching for someone, former routine | Safe walking areas, meaningful activity, GPS tracking |
| **Sundowning** | Fatigue, reduced light, disruption of circadian rhythm | Consistent routine, lighting, calming activities |
| **Repetitive questions** | Memory loss, anxiety, need for reassurance | Answer calmly, redirect to activity, validation |
| **Suspicion/accusations** | Memory loss, confusion, loss of control | Don't argue, reassure, check for real underlying issue |
| **Aggression** | Pain, fear, overstimulation, frustration | Remove triggers, ensure safety, validate feelings |
| **Hiding/hoarding** | Loss of control, former habits, security | Safe places to "hide," regular searches for hazards |

---

#### Late Stage (Severe)
**MMSE Score:** <10

**Characteristics:**
- Minimal or no short-term memory
- Severe communication impairment
- Total dependence for ADLs
- Physical decline (swallowing, mobility, incontinence)
- Increased infection risk
- May still respond to touch, music, familiar voices

**Care Approach:**
- Focus on comfort and dignity
- Sensory stimulation (music, touch, aromatherapy)
- Prevent complications (pressure injuries, aspiration)
- Family support through end-of-life
- Hospice integration when appropriate

**Activity Ideas:**
- Gentle touch, hand massage
- Familiar music from youth
- Looking at nature/flowers
- Soft stuffed animals or blankets
- Reading aloud, familiar voices
- Aromatherapy (lavender, citrus)

## Validation Therapy

### Core Principles

Developed by Naomi Feil for disoriented elderly, validation therapy acknowledges and validates the feelings behind confused speech and behavior.

**Key Principles:**
1. **All behavior has meaning** — Look for the unmet need
2. **Enter their reality** — Don't try to reorient to present
3. **Listen with empathy** — Emotional connection over factual accuracy
4. **Validate the emotion** — Not the content

### Communication Techniques

**Instead of:**
- "Your husband died 10 years ago" (reality orientation)
- "We're not going anywhere" (denial)
- "You already asked that" (frustration)

**Try:**
- "You miss your husband. He was important to you."
- "You want to go home. Tell me about your home."
- "That's on your mind. Let's talk about it."

### Practical Examples

| Situation | Invalidating Response | Validating Response |
|-----------|---------------------|---------------------|
| Patient says deceased mother is visiting | "Your mother died years ago" | "You miss your mother. What would you like to tell her?" |
| Patient wants to leave facility | "You live here now. You can't leave." | "You want to go out. Where would you like to go?" |
| Patient is crying without apparent reason | "There's nothing to cry about" | "You seem sad. I'm here with you." |
| Patient is angry at caregiver | "I'm trying to help you!" | "You seem upset. I understand this is frustrating." |

## Teepa Snow's Positive Approach to Care (PAC)

### Gems Model

Teepa Snow's GEM model describes cognitive states as precious stones, each with unique characteristics:

| Gem | State | Characteristics | Approach |
|-----|-------|-----------------|----------|
| **Sapphire** | Normal aging | Some forgetfulness, compensates well | Support independence |
| **Diamond** | Early dementia | Rigid, repetitive, "true and blue" | Don't argue, redirect |
| **Emerald** | Mild-moderate | "On the go," covers deficits, anxious | Simplify, guide, support |
| **Amber** | Moderate | Caught in moment, sensory-seeking, impulsive | Safety first, engage senses |
| **Ruby** | Moderate-severe | Patterned, rhythmic, motor-focused | Use rhythm, body memory |
| **Pearl** | Late stage | Shell present, person fading, "hidden" | Comfort, touch, presence |

### Hand-Under-Hand Technique

Instead of grabbing or pulling, guide movement by placing your hand UNDER the person's hand:
- Preserves dignity
- Allows them to feel in control
- Uses muscle memory
- Reduces resistance and fear

## Sundowning Management

### Understanding Sundowning

Sundowning refers to increased confusion, agitation, and restlessness that occurs in late afternoon and evening, affecting up to 66% of dementia patients.

### Contributing Factors
- Fatigue from day's activities
- Reduced lighting and increased shadows
- Disruption of circadian rhythm
- Hunger or thirst
- Boredom or lack of stimulation
- Medication effects
- Pain or discomfort

### Prevention Strategies

**Environmental:**
- Increase natural light exposure during day
- Close curtains before sunset
- Reduce noise and stimulation after 3 PM
- Maintain comfortable temperature

**Routine:**
- Schedule demanding activities in morning
- Ensure regular rest periods
- Maintain consistent bedtime routine
- Avoid caffeine after noon

**Activity:**
- Calm, soothing activities late afternoon
- Music therapy
- One-on-one attention
- Familiar television programs

**Medical:**
- Review medications (stimulants, diuretics)
- Assess for pain
- Check for infection (UTI common trigger)
- Consider melatonin (consult physician)

## Wandering Management

### Types of Wandering

| Type | Motivation | Intervention |
|------|-----------|--------------|
| **Escape** | Wants to go "home" or to work | Validate, provide meaningful activity |
| **Searching** | Looking for person or place | Reassure, redirect to familiar activity |
| **Goal-directed** | Former routine (walk dog, go to store) | Substitute activity, safe walking area |
| **Aimless** | Boredom, restlessness | Engage in activity, exercise |
| **Nighttime** | Confused sleep-wake cycle | Consistent routine, nightlights, check for pain |

### Safety Interventions

**Environmental:**
- Disguise exits (curtains, murals)
- Install door alarms or chimes
- Use electronic monitoring (door sensors)
- Secure dangerous areas (stairs, kitchen)
- Clear pathways, remove hazards

**Personal:**
- ID bracelet with contact information
- GPS tracking device
- MedicAlert + Safe Return enrollment
- Recent photo on file
- Neighbor notification

**Programmatic:**
- Supervised walking programs
- Exercise to reduce restlessness
- Meaningful engagement
- Regular toileting schedule

## Non-Pharmacological Interventions for Behavioral Symptoms

### First-Line Approaches

Always attempt non-pharmacological interventions before considering medication for behavioral symptoms:

1. **Identify trigger** — What happened before the behavior?
2. **Rule out medical cause** — Pain, infection, constipation, dehydration
3. **Address unmet need** — Hunger, thirst, toileting, discomfort, boredom
4. **Modify environment** — Reduce stimulation, increase safety
5. **Use therapeutic communication** — Validation, redirection, reassurance

### Specific Interventions

| Symptom | Non-Pharmacological Interventions |
|---------|----------------------------------|
| **Agitation** | Music therapy, aromatherapy, hand massage, walking |
| **Aggression** | Give space, validate feelings, remove triggers, calm voice |
| **Apathy** | Simplify tasks, provide choices, sensory stimulation |
| **Anxiety** | Reassurance, familiar routines, weighted blankets |
| **Depression** | Increased socialization, meaningful activities, light therapy |
| **Hallucinations** | Don't argue, validate emotion, ensure safety |
| **Repetition** | Answer calmly, redirect to activity, use memory aids |
| **Sleep disturbance** | Consistent routine, limit naps, exercise, avoid caffeine |
