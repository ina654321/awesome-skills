# Medication Management Reference Guide

## Scope of Practice

### What Caregivers CAN Do

| Activity | Requirements |
|----------|--------------|
| **Medication reminders** | No special certification required |
| **Opening containers** | No special certification required |
| **Handing medication to client** | No special certification required |
| **Documenting medication taken** | Training on documentation |
| **Observing for side effects** | Training on common side effects |
| **Reporting concerns** | Clear communication protocols |

### What Caregivers CANNOT Do (Without Certification)

| Activity | Who Can Perform |
|----------|-----------------|
| **Administering medications** | Licensed nurse or Medication Aide/CMA |
| **Crushing medications** | Licensed nurse (some restrictions) |
| **Injections** | Licensed nurse |
| **Rectal/vaginal medications** | Licensed nurse |
| **Changing medication doses** | Physician only |
| **Stopping medications** | Physician order required |

## The Five Rights of Medication Administration

### Core Principles

1. **Right Patient** — Verify identity (name, birth date)
2. **Right Drug** — Check label three times
3. **Right Dose** — Confirm amount matches order
4. **Right Route** — Oral, topical, sublingual, etc.
5. **Right Time** — As prescribed (with meals, bedtime, etc.)

### Additional Safety Checks

6. **Right Documentation** — Record immediately after administration
7. **Right Reason** — Understand purpose of medication
8. **Right Response** — Monitor for effectiveness and side effects
9. **Right to Refuse** — Client has right to refuse (document and report)

---

## Three Checks System

### Check 1: When Retrieving
- Read label carefully
- Check expiration date
- Verify against medication list
- Check for any "hold" orders

### Check 2: Before Preparation
- Confirm client identity
- Verify medication name and dose
- Check time of last dose
- Assess for contraindications

### Check 3: At Administration
- Final label check
- Confirm client is ready
- Verify route of administration
- Observe client take medication

## Common Medication Categories in Elderly Care

| Class | Examples | Common Side Effects | Monitoring |
|-------|----------|---------------------|------------|
| **ACE Inhibitors** | Lisinopril, Enalapril | Cough, dizziness, hyperkalemia | BP, potassium, creatinine |

## Polypharmacy in the Elderly

### Definition
- **Polypharmacy:** Taking 5 or more medications
- **Hyper-polypharmacy:** Taking 10 or more medications

### Statistics
- 50% of elderly take 5+ medications
- 10% take 10+ medications
- Increases risk of adverse drug events
- Associated with falls, hospitalizations, mortality

### High-Risk Medications in Elderly (Beers Criteria)

**Potentially Inappropriate:**
- Anticholinergics (diphenhydramine)
- Benzodiazepines (long-term use)
- NSAIDs (chronic use)
- Antipsychotics (for behavioral symptoms without psychosis)
- Hypoglycemics (glyburide)

**Red Flags for Deprescribing:**
- Medication no longer has indication
- Benefits no longer outweigh risks
- Drug-drug interaction
- Drug-disease interaction
- Adverse effect attributed to medication

## Medication Adherence Strategies

### Organizational Tools

| Tool | Best For | Considerations |
|------|----------|----------------|
| **Pill organizers** | Simple regimens, cognitively intact | Fill weekly, check daily |
| **Blister packs** | Multiple medications, from pharmacy | Pharmacy-prepared, labeled |
| **Automatic dispensers** | Complex regimens, memory issues | Locked, alarmed, expensive |
| **Medication apps** | Tech-savvy clients, reminders | Requires smartphone |
| **Medication charts** | Visual learners, reference | Post in visible location |

### Behavioral Strategies

1. **Link to routine** — Take with meals, brushing teeth, etc.
2. **Simplify regimen** — Work with prescriber on timing
3. **Education** — Ensure client understands purpose
4. **Address barriers** — Cost, side effects, complexity
5. **Involve family** — Support system engagement
6. **Regular review** — Medication reconciliation

### Documentation Requirements

**Medication Administration Record (MAR):**
- Date and time given
- Medication name and dose
- Signature of person administering
- Any observations or concerns
- Refusals (with reason)
- Missed doses (with reason)

## Recognizing Adverse Drug Events

### Warning Signs

| System | Symptoms | Common Causes |
|--------|----------|---------------|
| **CNS** | Confusion, dizziness, falls | Sedatives, anticholinergics |
| **GI** | Nausea, constipation, bleeding | NSAIDs, opioids, iron |
| **Cardiovascular** | Chest pain, palpitations, edema | Digoxin, calcium channel blockers |
| **Skin** | Rash, itching, bruising | Antibiotics, anticoagulants |
| **Renal** | Decreased urine, swelling | NSAIDs, diuretics |

### When to Report Immediately

**Call 911:**
- Difficulty breathing
- Severe allergic reaction (anaphylaxis)
- Chest pain
- Severe bleeding
- Loss of consciousness

**Notify Supervisor/Healthcare Provider:**
- New confusion or altered mental status
- Falls
- Significant blood pressure change
- Refusal to take critical medications
- Suspected medication error

## Medication Storage and Handling

### Storage Requirements

| Requirement | Guidelines |
|-------------|------------|
| **Temperature** | Room temperature (68-77°F) unless otherwise specified |
| **Light** | Protect from light if indicated on label |
| **Humidity** | Keep in dry place; avoid bathroom storage |
| **Security** | Locked storage for controlled substances |
| **Organization** | Original containers with labels intact |

### Disposal

**Safe Disposal Methods:**
1. Drug take-back programs (preferred)
2. FDA flush list (if no take-back available)
3. Household trash (mix with undesirable substance, seal in bag)

**Do NOT Flush:**
- Medications not on FDA flush list
- Controlled substances (unless instructed)

## Herbal Supplements and Drug Interactions

### Common Interactions

| Supplement | Interacts With | Effect |
|------------|---------------|--------|
| **St. John's Wort** | Antidepressants, warfarin, digoxin | Reduced efficacy |
| **Ginkgo Biloba** | Anticoagulants, antiplatelets | Increased bleeding risk |
| **Garlic** | Anticoagulants, antiplatelets | Increased bleeding risk |
| **Ginseng** | Warfarin, diabetes medications | Variable effects |
| **Cranberry** | Warfarin | May increase INR |

**Documentation:** Always ask about and document herbal supplements, vitamins, and over-the-counter medications.

## Medication Reconciliation

### Definition
The process of comparing a patient's medication orders to all medications the patient is taking to avoid errors.

### When to Perform
- Admission to new care setting
- Discharge from hospital
- Return from hospital
- Change in providers
- Quarterly review

### Process
1. Obtain comprehensive medication list
2. Compare to current orders
3. Identify discrepancies
4. Clarify with prescriber
5. Document reconciled list
6. Educate client/family
