# End-of-Life Care Reference Guide

## Understanding the Dying Process

### Physiological Changes

As the body begins to shut down, predictable physical changes occur:

| System | Changes | Timeframe |
|--------|---------|-----------|
| **Cardiovascular** | Decreased circulation, cool extremities, mottling | Days to hours |
| **Respiratory** | Irregular breathing, Cheyne-Stokes pattern | Hours to minutes |
| **Neurological** | Decreased consciousness, unresponsiveness | Days to hours |
| **Renal** | Decreased urine output, dark/concentrated urine | Days |
| **Gastrointestinal** | Decreased appetite, difficulty swallowing | Weeks to days |
| **Integumentary** | Cool, pale, mottled skin; decreased turgor | Days to hours |

### Signs of Approaching Death (Days to Hours)

**1-3 Months Before:**
- Withdrawal from world and people
- Speaking with deceased relatives
- Decreased food intake
- Increased sleep
- Changes in vital signs

**1-2 Weeks Before:**
- Further decrease in food/fluid intake
- Body temperature fluctuations
- Changes in breathing patterns
- Restlessness or agitation
- Changes in urination

**Days to Hours Before:**
- Surge of energy (rally)
- Decreased responsiveness
- Changes in breathing (Cheyne-Stokes)
- Noisy breathing (death rattle)
- Mottling of extremities
- Glassy, teary eyes

## Comfort Measures

### Pain Management

**Non-Pharmacological:**
- Repositioning every 2 hours
- Gentle massage
- Heat/cold therapy
- Distraction (music, reading)
- Relaxation techniques
- Therapeutic presence

**Pharmacological (Hospice/Physician Directed):**
- Morphine for pain and dyspnea
- Lorazepam for anxiety
- Haloperidol for terminal restlessness
- Atropine or scopolamine for excessive secretions
- Medications given sublingually, buccally, or rectally when swallowing difficult

### Respiratory Comfort

**Cheyne-Stokes Breathing:**
- Cyclical pattern of deep breathing followed by apnea
- Not distressing to the dying person
- Reassure family this is normal

**Death Rattle (Terminal Secretions):**
- Caused by accumulation of secretions in airways
- More distressing to family than patient
- Position on side to promote drainage
- Medications (atropine, scopolamine, glycopyrrolate) may help
- Suctioning usually not recommended (causes more distress)

### Mouth Care

**Importance:**
- Prevents dry mouth and discomfort
- Maintains dignity
- Should be provided every 1-2 hours

**Technique:**
1. Use soft sponge swabs or gauze
2. Apply small amounts of water or mouth moisturizer
3. Gently swab inside cheeks, tongue, lips
4. Apply lip balm
5. Remove/clean dentures if present

### Positioning

**Goals:**
- Comfort and safety
- Prevent pressure injuries
- Promote breathing
- Facilitate drainage

**Positions:**
- Side-lying (promotes drainage)
- Semi-Fowler's (helps breathing)
- Prone with support (pressure relief)
- Change position every 2 hours or as tolerated

### Skin Care

**Preventing Pressure Injuries:**
- Reposition every 2 hours
- Use pressure-relieving mattress/pads
- Keep skin clean and dry
- Apply barrier cream to bony prominences
- Use draw sheets to move patient

**Incontinence Care:**
- Use absorbent pads/briefs
- Change promptly when soiled
- Gentle cleansing with warm water
- Apply barrier cream to protect skin

## Emotional and Spiritual Support

### For the Dying Person

**Communication:**
- Assume they can hear until death
- Speak softly, clearly
- Touch if appropriate
- Play favorite music
- Read aloud
- Share memories
- Give permission to let go

**Statements that Help:**
- "I love you"
- "I'll be OK"
- "You can let go when you're ready"
- "Thank you for everything"
- "Goodbye"

**Avoid:**
- Loud noises
- Forcing food/fluids
- Correcting confusion
- Rushing
- Leaving alone if possible

### For the Family

**Anticipatory Grief Support:**
- Normalize feelings
- Provide information about what to expect
- Encourage reminiscing
- Suggest life review activities
- Facilitate resolution of conflicts
- Support decision-making

**During Active Dying:**
- Explain physical changes
- Encourage presence
- Support physical touch
- Allow private time
- Provide breaks
- Ensure comfort (food, rest, bathroom)

**After Death:**
- Allow time with body
- Follow cultural/religious practices
- Provide information about next steps
- Offer grief resources
- Document final moments if family desires

### Cultural Considerations

| Culture/Religion | Beliefs/Practices |
|-----------------|-------------------|
| **Christianity** | Last rites, prayer, sacraments; family presence important |
| **Judaism** | Chevra Kadisha (sacred society), prompt burial, sitting shiva |
| **Islam** | Face toward Mecca, prompt burial, washing of body by same gender |
| **Buddhism** | Peaceful environment, chanting, mind consciousness continues 3-4 days |
| **Hinduism** | Sacred water (Ganges), family present, prompt cremation |
| **Catholicism** | Anointing of the sick, Eucharist, family may want priest present |

**Always Ask:**
- "What is important to you/your family at this time?"
- "Are there cultural or religious practices we should observe?"
- "Would you like spiritual support?"

## Hospice and Palliative Care

### Hospice Eligibility

**General Criteria:**
- Life expectancy of 6 months or less (if disease follows natural course)
- Curative treatment is no longer chosen
- Goals shift to comfort and quality of life

**Common Hospice Diagnoses:**
- Dementia (FAST scale 7c or beyond)
- Cancer
- Heart failure (NYHA Class IV)
- COPD (severe, frequent exacerbations)
- Renal failure (declining dialysis)
- Stroke/coma
- Failure to thrive

### Palliative Care vs. Hospice

| Aspect | Palliative Care | Hospice |
|--------|-----------------|---------|
| **Timing** | Any stage of serious illness | End of life (6 months prognosis) |
| **Goal** | Improve quality of life, manage symptoms | Comfort at end of life |
| **Treatment** | Concurrent with curative treatment | Comfort care only |
| **Location** | Hospital, clinic, home | Home, facility, hospice house |
| **Team** | Doctors, nurses, social workers, chaplains | Interdisciplinary team |

### Hospice Team Members

- **Physician** — Medical oversight, symptom management
- **Nurse** — Clinical care, education, coordination
- **Social Worker** — Emotional support, resources, planning
- **Chaplain** — Spiritual care
- **Home Health Aide** — Personal care
- **Volunteer** — Companionship, respite
- **Bereavement Counselor** — Grief support for family

## Advance Care Planning

### Key Documents

**Advance Directive:**
- Written statement of healthcare wishes
- May include living will and healthcare proxy
- Legally binding

**Living Will:**
- Specific instructions for end-of-life care
- Addresses artificial nutrition, hydration, ventilation
- Activated when unable to make own decisions

**Healthcare Proxy (Durable Power of Attorney for Healthcare):**
- Designates decision-maker
- Becomes active when incompetent
- Should be someone who knows values and wishes

**POLST (Physician Orders for Life-Sustaining Treatment):**
- Medical orders for specific treatments
- Signed by physician
- Followed by EMS and healthcare providers

**DNR/DNAR (Do Not Attempt Resuscitation):**
- Specific order not to perform CPR
- Does not mean "do not treat"
- Comfort measures continue

### Conversation Starters

- "Have you thought about what kind of care you would want if you couldn't speak for yourself?"
- "What matters most to you at this stage of your life?"
- "Are there treatments you would want to avoid?"
- "Who would you want to make decisions for you?"

## Signs of Imminent Death

### Final Hours

**Physical Signs:**
- Decreased responsiveness
- Irregular, shallow breathing (Cheyne-Stokes)
- Cold, mottled extremities
- Weak, slow pulse
- Decreased urine output
- Glassy eyes, half-open
- Jaw relaxed, mouth open

**Not Distressing to Patient:**
- Death rattle (terminal secretions)
- Irregular breathing
- Color changes

**Family Education:**
- "These changes are expected and normal"
- "Your loved one is not suffering"
- "Hearing may be the last sense to go"
- "Continue to speak softly and offer comfort"

### At the Moment of Death

**What to Do:**
1. Confirm death (no breathing, no pulse)
2. Notify hospice/facility per protocol
3. Allow family time with body
4. Prepare for body changes (relaxed jaw, evacuation)
5. Contact funeral home when family ready
6. Provide death certificate information

**Body Care After Death:**
- Close eyes gently
- Support jaw if needed
- Remove tubes if appropriate
- Cleanse body as desired
- Position with dignity
- Cover with clean sheet

## Grief and Bereavement

### Types of Grief

**Anticipatory Grief:**
- Grieving before death occurs
- Normal and common
- Allows for preparation
- Does not replace grief after death

**Complicated Grief:**
- Persistent, debilitating grief
- Interferes with daily functioning
- May require professional support
- Risk factors: sudden death, dependent relationship, lack of support

**Disenfranchised Grief:**
- Loss not socially recognized
- Caregivers may experience this
- Important to acknowledge

### Supporting Grieving Families

**Do:**
- Listen without judgment
- Use the deceased's name
- Acknowledge the loss
- Offer practical help
- Be present
- Remember anniversaries

**Don't:**
- Minimize the loss ("At least they're not suffering")
- Rush the process
- Avoid the topic
- Offer platitudes ("They're in a better place")
- Compare losses

### Caregiver Grief

Caregivers often experience significant grief:
- Loss of the person they knew
- Loss of relationship
- Loss of purpose/role
- Physical exhaustion
- Financial impact

**Self-Care:**
- Allow time to grieve
- Seek support groups
- Consider counseling
- Maintain social connections
- Honor the memory
- Consider new purpose/volunteering
