# Scenario Examples

## 9.1 Suspected Elder Abuse

**User:** "I noticed bruises on my elderly client's arms and legs. She says she fell, but the bruises look like fingerprints. What do I do?"

**Expert:**
> **Elder Abuse Assessment:**
>
> | Red Flag | Significance |
> |----------|-------------|
> | **Bruises in unusual patterns** | Fingerprints, belt marks |
> | **Explanation doesn't match injuries** | "I fell" doesn't explain the mark |
> | **Caregiver change of story** | Inconsistent explanations |
> | **Elder flinches at touch** | Possible physical abuse |
> | **Withdrawal, fearfulness** | Emotional abuse indicators |
> | **Missing money, unpaid bills** | Financial exploitation |
>
> **Response:**
> 1. **Document** — Write down exactly what you observed, when, where
> 2. **Don't confront** — Don't accuse the caregiver or family member
> 3. **Ask gently** — "I noticed these marks. Can you tell me how they happened?"
> 4. **Report** — Call Adult Protective Services (APS): 1-800-677-1116
> 5. **Follow up** — Document your report
> 6. **Continue care** — Don't abandon the client
>
> **Legal Requirement:**
> - Most states mandate reporting of suspected elder abuse
> - Failure to report can result in professional consequences
> - Good faith reports are protected from liability
> - You don't need proof — just reasonable suspicion

## 9.2 Refusing Care

**User:** "My elderly client refuses to bathe. She hasn't bathed in a week. Her family is pushing for more frequent bathing. What do I do?"

**Expert:**
> **Refusal of Care Protocol:**
>
> | Principle | Application |
> |-----------|-------------|
> | **Autonomy** | Competent adults can refuse care |
> | **Dignity** | Forcing care violates dignity |
> | **Safety** | But if unsafe, intervention may be needed |
>
> **Approaches:**
> 1. **Understand why** — "I notice you seem hesitant today. What's going on?"
> 2. **Offer choices** — Sponge bath vs. shower, timing, temperature
> 3. **Involve family** — Respect her voice in her care
> 4. **Meet in the middle** — Every other day with a warm compress
> 5. **Explain consequences** — "Your family is worried about your comfort"
>
> **When to Escalate:**
> - Skin breakdown or infection risk
> - Wound care needed
> - Client becoming unsafe due to hygiene
> - Family conflict requiring mediation
>
> **Document:** The refusal, your attempts, and the plan

## 9.3 Wandering

**User:** "My client with dementia wandered out of the house while I was in the kitchen. I found her in the yard but I'm terrified. What can I do to prevent this?"

**Expert:**
> **Dementia Wandering Prevention:**
>
> | Prevention Strategy | Details |
> |-------------------|--------|
> | **Door alarms** | Pressure sensors, chime when opened |
> | **Camouflage doors** | Curtain, poster to match wall |
> | **Safe outside space** | Fenced yard for safe wandering |
> | **GPS tracking** | Wearable device or shoe insert |
> | **Routine** | Predictable schedule reduces wandering impulse |
> | **Redirect** | "Let's make lunch together" |
>
> **If It Happens:**
> 1. **Stay calm** — Don't chase (causes more fear)
> 2. **Call family/emergency contacts** — Immediately
> 3. **Search safe areas first** — Neighbors, nearby
> 4. **Call 911** — If not found within 10-15 minutes
> 5. **Provide photo and description** — Know this in advance
> 6. **After found** — Don't punish — redirect, check for injury
>
> **Care Plan Update:**
> - Tell family this happened
> - Get medical evaluation (new wandering = possible progression)
> - Implement prevention measures immediately
> - Document incident
