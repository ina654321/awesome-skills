# Scenario Examples

## 9.1 Postpartum Depression Signs

**User:** "The new mom seems unusually tearful and overwhelmed. She hasn't slept in two days. She says she feels like a failure. Is this normal baby blues or something more?"

**Expert:**
> **Postpartum Mental Health Assessment:**
>
> | Baby Blues (Normal) | Postpartum Depression (PPD) |
> |-------------------|------------------------------|
> | Lasts up to 2 weeks | Persists beyond 2 weeks |
> | Tearfulness, mood swings | Persistent sadness, emptiness |
> | Fatigue from newborn care | Severe fatigue, inability to sleep |
> | Anxiety about new role | Excessive anxiety, panic attacks |
>
> **What to Watch For:**
> - Crying uncontrollably
> - Not eating or eating too much
> - Withdrawing from baby, family, friends
> - Feelings of worthlessness or guilt
> - Thoughts of harming self or baby
> - Intrusive, scary thoughts
>
> **Response:**
> 1. **Listen without judgment** — "I'm here for you"
> 2. **Don't minimize** — "This sounds really hard"
> 3. **Encourage professional help** — Connect to PPD resources
> 4. **Monitor safety** — If thoughts of harm, call Postpartum Support International: 1-800-944-4773
> 5. **Don't isolate her** — Facilitate support
> 6. **Document and report** — Tell employer/partner if safety concern
>
> **This is NOT your role to fix** — Connect to mental health professionals immediately

## 9.2 Newborn Feeding Difficulty

**User:** "The baby is only 3 days old. She won't latch properly and the mom is exhausted and frustrated. The mom is considering giving up breastfeeding. What do I do?"

**Expert:**
> **Breastfeeding Support Protocol:**
>
> | Issue | Solution |
> |-------|----------|
> | **Poor latch** | Skin-to-skin, reclined feeding, check tongue tie |
> | **Sore nipples** | Check latch, apply nipple cream, air dry |
> | **Engorgement** | Warm compress before, cold after; frequent feeding |
> | **Low milk supply concern** | Count wet diapers (6+ per day normal) |
> | **Baby won't wake to feed** | Undress baby, skin-to-skin |
>
> **Support Steps:**
> 1. **Normalize** — "Day 3 is the hardest. This is common."
> 2. **Offer practical help** — Bring the baby to her, bring water/snacks
> 3. **Check basics** — Is baby positioned well? Is latch deep?
> 4. **Suggest lactation consultant** — Refer to IBCLC
> 5. **If formula needed temporarily** — Support without judgment
> 6. **Protect her rest** — Take over other tasks so she can focus on feeding
>
> **Know When to Refer:**
> - Mastitis (red, painful breast, fever): Call doctor
> - Baby not gaining weight: Call pediatrician
> - Persistent pain: Lactation consultant
> - Mom expressing distress about "failure": Mental health referral

## 9.3 Grandparents Overstepping

**User:** "The new grandparents want to visit but they're ignoring the new mom's boundaries. They're giving the baby a bottle without asking, trying to take the baby to another room, and making comments about the mom's parenting. The mom is too exhausted to confront them."

**Expert:**
> **Family Dynamic Management:**
>
> | Situation | Recommended Response |
> |-----------|---------------------|
> | **Giving bottle without asking** | "Mom is working hard on breastfeeding — let's give her the baby for the next feeding" |
> | **Taking baby to another room** | "Baby is due for a feeding — let me bring her back" |
> | **Critical comments** | "Mom is doing a great job — every baby is different" |
> | **Ignoring mom's instructions** | Privately support mom's position, facilitate conversation |
>
> **Your Role:**
> - You are the mom's advocate
> - Support her decisions without judgment
> - Redirect grandparents firmly but kindly
> - "Mom has asked that we..."
> - Don't escalate — stay professional
> - If unsafe situations (sleeping with baby, etc.), intervene directly
>
> **If It Becomes Unmanageable:**
> - Discuss with the mom about her preferences
> - She may need to set boundaries directly
> - You can facilitate the conversation
> - Your job is to protect the mother's rest and bonding
