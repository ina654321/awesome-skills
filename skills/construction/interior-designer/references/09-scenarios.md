# Interior Design — Glossary

## Space Planning & Layout

**FF&E (Furniture, Fixtures, and Equipment)** — Movable and fixed items in an interior project: furniture, lighting fixtures, plumbing fixtures, and equipment. The FF&E budget is separate from construction costs.

**Floor Plan** — A scaled, two-dimensional drawing showing the arrangement of spaces, walls, doors, windows, and furniture from a top-down view.

**Elevation** — A two-dimensional drawing showing a wall face with dimensions, heights, and finishes. Interior elevations are drawn at 1/4" = 1' scale.

**Section** — A cut-through drawing showing vertical relationships between floors, ceilings, and built-in elements. Used to verify clearances and proportions.

**Clearance** — The minimum required open space between furniture pieces, between furniture and walls, or at circulation paths. Defined by building codes and accessibility standards.

**Egress** — The path of travel from any point in a building to an exit. Must meet minimum width, headroom, and door clearance requirements per IBC.

**Riser / Tread** — The vertical (riser) and horizontal (tread) components of a stair. Building codes specify maximum riser height (typically 7") and minimum tread depth (typically 11").

**ADAAG (ADA Accessibility Guidelines)** — The accessibility standards published by the US Access Board. Covers reach ranges, maneuvering clearances, accessible routes, and fixture mounting heights.

**Turnkey** — A project delivery where the contractor provides everything needed for occupancy. The client receives a finished, functional space.

## Materials & Finishes

**VOC (Volatile Organic Compound)** — Chemicals that evaporate from paints, adhesives, sealants, and finishes. Regulated due to indoor air quality impacts. Zero-VOC and low-VOC products are preferred for green building projects.

**Sheen** — The degree of glossiness in paint. Types: Flat (0-5%), Eggshell (3-10%), Satin (25-35%), Semi-Gloss (35-70%), High-Gloss (70-85%). Higher sheen = more washable but shows wall imperfections.

**Vinyl Composition Tile (VCT)** — Durable floor tile made from vinyl, limestone, and pigments. Common in commercial applications. Requires regular maintenance and periodic stripping and waxing.

**Luxury Vinyl Plank (LVP)** — Rigid core vinyl flooring that mimics wood or stone. Waterproof, scratch-resistant, and relatively easy to install. Click-lock or glue-down installation.

**Porcelain Tile** — Densest type of ceramic tile (water absorption <0.5%). Suitable for floors, walls, and exterior applications. Defined by PEI rating for abrasion resistance.

**Quartz / Engineered Stone** — Composite of crushed quartz and resin binder. Non-porous, durable, and consistent in color/pattern. Does not require sealing (unlike natural stone).

**Solid Surface** — Acrylic or polyester resin mixed with minerals. Seamless installations, repairable, and available in countless colors. Used for countertops, shower walls, and millwork.

**Solid Wood vs. Veneer** — Solid wood is solid lumber throughout. Veneer is thin wood layer glued to substrate (MDF, plywood). Veneer is more dimensionally stable but cannot be refinished as many times.

**Hardwood vs. Engineered Hardwood** — Hardwood is solid wood throughout, can be sanded 4-8 times. Engineered has plywood or HDF core with hardwood veneer top; more dimensionally stable but fewer refinish cycles.

**Terrazzo** — Composite material of marble, quartz, granite, or glass chips in cement or epoxy binder. Ground and polished smooth. Seamless and extremely durable.

**Stucco** — Portland cement plaster applied to exterior or interior walls. Can be smooth, float, or textured. Interior stucco requires proper moisture management.

**Wainscoting** — Paneling installed on the lower portion of interior walls. Traditionally wood; modern versions use MDF, PVC, or composite materials.

**Millwork** — Custom woodwork including cabinetry, built-in shelving, trim, and decorative elements. Typically fabricated in a shop and installed on-site.

**Casegood** — Ready-made furniture including desks, tables, and storage units. Distinguishes from built-in millwork.

## Lighting

**Foot-Candle (FC)** — Imperial unit of illuminance. 1 FC = 1 lumen per square foot. The standard unit for lighting specifications in the US.

**Lux** — Metric unit of illuminance. 1 lux = 1 lumen per square meter. 1 lux ≈ 0.0929 FC.

**Color Rendering Index (CRI)** — Scale 0-100 measuring how accurately a light source reveals object colors compared to natural light. CRI 90+ is excellent; CRI 80 is standard for general use.

**Correlated Color Temperature (CCT)** — Measured in Kelvin (K). Describes the warmth or coolness of light. 2700K = warm white; 3000K = neutral white; 4000K = cool white; 5000K+ = daylight.

**Ambient Lighting** — The general, overall illumination in a space. Provided by ceiling fixtures, recessed cans, or cove lighting.

**Task Lighting** — Focused lighting for specific work areas (desk, kitchen counter, vanity). Higher illuminance than ambient.

**Accent Lighting** — Lighting that highlights specific features: artwork, architectural elements, merchandise displays. Typically 3-5x the ambient level.

**Lumens** — The total quantity of visible light emitted by a source. Higher lumens = brighter light. Replacing watts (which measure energy consumption).

**Dimming Control** — System to adjust light output. Types: 0-10V, DALI, DMX, Lutron Caseta, Lutron RadioRA. LED fixtures require dimmer compatibility verification.

**IC-Rated (Insulation Contact)** — Recessed lighting rated for direct contact with insulation. Required in most ceiling installations for fire safety.

## Design Styles & Movements

**Modern** — Design style emphasizing clean lines, minimal ornament, industrial materials (steel, glass, concrete), and functional form. 1920s-1960s influence.

**Contemporary** — The design style of the current moment. Fluid, mixing styles, curved forms, and often blending modern with traditional elements.

**Transitional** — Blend of traditional and contemporary. Clean lines but warm; structured but comfortable. Most popular style in residential design.

**Mid-Century Modern** — Design from 1945-1969. Characterized by clean lines, organic curves, mixed materials, and emphasis on function. Think Eames, Knoll, Herman Miller.

**Traditional** — Classic European and American design with rich colors, ornate details, heavy fabrics, and formal arrangements. Inspired by 18th and 19th-century design.

**Industrial** — Raw, unfinished aesthetic inspired by warehouses and factories. Exposed brick, concrete, metal pipes, reclaimed wood, and Edison bulbs.

**Scandinavian / Nordic** — Minimal, functional design from Denmark, Sweden, Norway. Light woods, muted colors, clean lines, and emphasis on craftsmanship.

**Japandi** — Fusion of Japanese and Scandinavian aesthetics. Clean lines, natural materials, muted colors, and intentional minimalism.

**Biophilic Design** — Design approach incorporating natural elements, materials, forms, and patterns to connect occupants with nature. Living walls, natural light, organic materials.

**Feng Shui** — Traditional Chinese practice of arranging spaces to promote positive energy flow (chi). Considers spatial layout, orientation, and elemental balance.

## Furniture & Casework

**Custom Millwork** — Bespoke woodwork built specifically for the project. Higher cost than stock but exact fit and unlimited design options.

**Semi-Custom Millwork** — Manufacturer's standard sizes with limited customization options (door styles, finishes, hardware). Middle ground of cost and customization.

**Modular Furniture** — Prefabricated furniture components that can be reconfigured. Common in open plan offices. Knoll, Herman Miller, Steelcase.

**Open Plan / Benchging** — Workstation arrangement with no or low partitions. Maximizes density but reduces privacy and focus.

**Sit-Stand Desk** — Adjustable-height workstation allowing both seated and standing postures. Electric height adjustment is standard.

**Task Chair** — Ergonomic office chair with adjustable seat height, back angle, armrests, and lumbar support. BIFMA-certified.

**Reception Desk / Counter** — First point of contact in commercial spaces. Must meet ADA reach range requirements and provide clear floor space.

**Banquette** — Built-in upholstered seating, typically along a wall. Maximizes seating in tight spaces and creates intimate atmosphere.

**Murphy Bed** — Wall bed that folds up into a cabinet when not in use. Space-saving solution for studios and multi-use rooms.

## Project Phases

**Programming** — Phase 1 of interior design. Documenting client requirements, collecting existing conditions, and establishing project scope, budget, and timeline.

**Schematic Design (SD)** — Conceptual design development. Space planning diagrams, preliminary finish palettes, and rough budgets.

**Design Development (DD)** — Refining the schematic design. Detailed floor plans, finish schedules, furniture selections, lighting specifications, and detailed cost estimate.

**Construction Documents (CDs)** — Detailed drawings and specifications for permitting and construction. Coordinate with architects, engineers, and contractors.

**Furniture Procurement / FF&E Phase** — Sourcing, ordering, and managing delivery of furniture, fixtures, and equipment. Typically overlaps with construction completion.

**Installation / Punch List** — Placing furniture, installing artwork, and finalizing accessories. Creating punch list of items needing correction.

**Project Closeout** — Final walk-through, documenting as-built conditions, providing care/maintenance documentation to client.

## Budget & Contracts

**Design Fee Structure** — Interior design fees can be: hourly, fixed fee, percentage of construction cost (typical: 10-25% residential, 5-15% commercial), or per phase.

**Change Order** — Written authorization for additional work beyond the original contract scope. Required before proceeding with changes.

**Contingency** — Budget reserve for unforeseen conditions. Construction contingency: typically 10-15%. FF&E contingency: typically 5-10%.

**Allowance** — A budget placeholder for items not fully specified. e.g., "Allowance: $500/ln ft for countertop" — actual cost may be more or less.

**Reimbursables** — Expenses the client reimburses the designer for: printing, shipping, sample purchases, mileage, consultant coordination.

**Purchase Order (PO)** — Formal authorization to purchase goods or services. Documents what was ordered, at what price, and for which project.

## Color Theory

**Color Wheel** — Primary (red, yellow, blue), secondary (orange, green, purple), and tertiary colors arranged in a circle. The basis for color harmony relationships.

**Complementary Colors** — Colors opposite each other on the color wheel (e.g., blue and orange). High contrast when paired.

**Analogous Colors** — Colors adjacent on the color wheel (e.g., blue, blue-green, green). Creates harmonious, cohesive palettes.

**Warm Colors** — Red, orange, yellow, and their variations. Create energy, excitement, and the perception of warmth.

**Cool Colors** — Blue, green, purple, and their variations. Create calm, relaxation, and the perception of coolness.

**Neutral Colors** — White, black, gray, and off-whites. Also includes warm neutrals (beige, tan, greige) and cool neutrals (blue-gray, green-gray).

**Color Progression** — A range of one hue from light to dark (e.g., pale blue → medium blue → navy blue).

**Monochromatic** — Design using variations of a single color.

## Tools & Software

**AutoCAD** — Industry-standard 2D drafting software. Used for construction documents and detailed plans.

**Revit** — BIM (Building Information Modeling) software. Creates 3D models with data-rich components. Used in larger commercial projects.

**SketchUp** — 3D modeling software for conceptual visualization. Intuitive interface for quick massing studies and presentation models.

**Enscape** — Real-time 3D rendering plugin for Revit, SketchUp, and Rhino. Provides walkthrough renderings directly from design models.

**Lumion** — 3D rendering software producing cinematic-quality visualization. Imports models from most CAD software.

**Adobe Creative Suite** — Includes InDesign (presentation layouts), Photoshop (image editing), and Illustrator (vector graphics). Standard for design presentations.

**Material Bank** — Online platform for sourcing, sampling, and specifying materials from hundreds of manufacturers. Integrates with design software.

**Studio Designer** — Project management and accounting software for interior designers. Tracks client billing, vendor purchases, and project budgets.

## Sustainability & Green Building

**LEED (Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design)** — Green building rating system from USGBC. Interior projects can achieve LEED ID+C (Interior Design & Construction) certification.

**Living Building Challenge** — Advanced green building standard requiring net-zero energy, water independence, and non-toxic materials. Most stringent sustainability standard.

**WELL Building Standard** — Focuses on human health and wellness in buildings. Addresses air, water, nourishment, light, fitness, comfort, and mind.

**Carbon Footprint** — Total greenhouse gas emissions caused by a project. Measured in CO2 equivalent (CO2e). Lower is better.

**Embodied Carbon** — Carbon emissions associated with manufacturing, transportation, and installation of building materials. A growing focus in sustainable design.

**Biophilic Materials** — Natural materials that support biophilic design: reclaimed wood, natural stone, living plants, natural textiles.

**Daylighting** — Design strategy maximizing natural light. Includes window placement, light shelves, skylights, and light tubes.
