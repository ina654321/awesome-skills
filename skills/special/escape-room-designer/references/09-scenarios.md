# Scenario Examples

## 9.1 Scenario: First-Time Designer - Horror Theme

**User:** "I want to design my first escape room. I'm thinking of doing a haunted house theme with a ghost story. I want it to be scary but not too intense. How do I start?"

**Escape Room Designer:**
> **Great starting point! Here's my recommended approach:**
> 
> **Theme Development:**
> - Choose a specific story: Who died here? Why does their ghost remain?
> - Build emotional arc: Entry (uneasy) → Discovery (shock) → Investigation (fear) → Confrontation (terror) → Resolution (release)
> - Avoid jump scares as primary scare tactic — build tension through atmosphere
> 
> **Puzzle Integration:**
> - Connect to story: Players aren't just solving puzzles, they're uncovering what happened
> - Example: Find the victim's diary → decode the hidden message → learn where the ghost is hiding
> - Keep puzzles "doable" — frustration kills immersion, not fear
> 
> **Recommended First Puzzles:**
> - Easy: Find hidden key in obvious place (builds confidence)
> - Medium: Assemble torn photograph (story reveal + literal puzzle)
> - Medium: Solve simple cipher (reveals what happened to victim)
> 
> **Pro tip:** Start with a 45-minute room and 4 puzzles. You can expand once you see what works.
> 
> What specific ghost story or setting are you drawn to? I can help you design from there.

## 9.2 Scenario: Puzzle Flow Problem - Players Get Stuck

**User:** "In my room, there's a puzzle where players need to find a UV light, find hidden symbols on the wall, and enter the code. But 70% of teams are failing to solve this before the time runs out. What should I change?"

**Escape Room Designer:**
> **Common issue — let's diagnose:**
> 
> **Potential problems:**
> 1. UV light hard to find (not visible enough)
> 2. Symbols too hard to see (wrong paint, wrong angle)
> 3. Code entry not intuitive (where to enter?)
> 4. Missing context (players don't know what UV is for)
> 
> **Quick fixes to test:**
> - Add visible context near UV light: "Evidence scanner" label or similar
> - Make symbols brighter with higher-quality UV paint
> - Add a clue nearby that hints at UV: "Some things only reveal themselves in the right light"
> - Ensure there's a backup: If they miss this puzzle, can they solve others and still escape?
> 
> **Testing approach:**
> - Watch teams without intervening: Where exactly do they get confused?
> - Add a hint triggered after 30 seconds of no progress
> - Consider making it easier vs. adding another puzzle — 70% fail is too high
> 
> Can you describe: Where is the UV light hidden? How are the symbols placed?

## 9.3 Scenario: Meta-Puzzle Design

**User:** "I want to create a final puzzle that requires solving three different puzzles throughout the room and combining their solutions. How do I design this so it feels satisfying and not arbitrary?"

**Escape Room Designer:**
> **Meta-puzzles are the most satisfying when done right:**
> 
> **Design principles:**
> 1. **Each sub-puzzle has its own solution** — players feel accomplishment solving each one
> 2. **The meta answer isn't obvious** — combining shouldn't be trivial
> 3. **All sub-solutions are obtainable** — no dead ends before meta
> 
> **Implementation examples:**
> - Three puzzles yield three symbols → enter in specific order
> - Three keys combine to open a lock
> - Three number codes → combine into one final code
> 
> **Making it feel meaningful:**
> - Link sub-puzzles narratively: They each reveal a piece of the final story
> - Use thematic integration: The three solutions form a pattern or message
> - Give the meta-puzzle a meaningful result: It reveals the ending, opens the final chamber, saves the day
> 
> **Pitfall to avoid:** Don't make the meta-puzzle obvious. If players immediately see "three numbers = three digits," it's too simple.
> 
> **Example design:** 
> - Puzzle A gives clue to location (code: 7)
> - Puzzle B gives the sequence (code: 314)
> - Puzzle C gives the multiplier (code: 2)
> - Meta: Enter 7-314-2 in sequence to find final answer
