# Standard Workflow

## 8.1 Wine Tasting Workflow

### Step 1: Preparation
- Ensure proper glassware (ISO tasting glass)
- Verify temperature (whites: 45-50°F; reds: 55-65°F)
- Open wine 30-60 min before for reds; not needed for most whites
- Have water and neutral crackers for palate cleansing
- Note: If wine is corked, oxidized, or flawed, note it immediately

### Step 2: Appearance Evaluation
- Hold glass against white background
- Assess color: intensity (pale to deep), hue (purple → ruby → garnet for reds)
- Note viscosity (legs/tears indicate body and alcohol)
- Check clarity (should be clear; haziness indicates fault)

### Step 3: Nose Evaluation (Two Assessments)
**First nose (on pour)**: Initial impressions, volatile compounds
- Swirl gently to release aromatics
- Note intensity and quality

**Second nose (after swirling)**: Deeper analysis
- Identify specific aromas by category
- Look for primary fruit, secondary (oak, fermentation), tertiary (aging)
- Assess complexity and balance

### Step 4: Palate Evaluation
- Take small sip, let it coat entire mouth
- Assess key structural elements:
  - Sweetness (dry to sweet)
  - Acidity (mouthwatering quality)
  - Tannin (structure, grip, quality)
  - Body (light, medium, full)
  - Alcohol (low to high)
- Identify flavor compounds matching nose
- Note length of finish (seconds after swallowing)
- Assess balance: all elements in harmony

### Step 5: Conclusion
- Overall quality assessment (fault, average, very good, excellent)
- Ageability potential
- Value assessment (quality relative to price)
- Food pairing recommendations

## 8.2 Tea Tasting Workflow

### Step 1: Preparation
- Use gongfu cha or standard tasting method based on tea type
- Water temperature: 175-185°F for greens; 200-212°F for black and puerh
- Tea-to-water ratio: 5-8g per 100ml for gongfu; 2-3g per 200ml for western
- Pre-warm vessels

### Step 2: Dry Leaf Assessment
- Observe before water contact: shape, color, uniformity
- Note processing characteristics (rolled, twisted, compressed)
- Smell dry leaf for initial aromas

### Step 3: First Infusion (15-30 seconds)
- Pour hot water, steep briefly
- Examine wet leaf: opened structure, color change
- Smell wet leaf
- Taste liquor: initial impressions
- Note: Discard first infusion for aged teas (washing)

### Step 4: Subsequent Infusions
- Increase steep time with each infusion (30s, 45s, 1min, etc.)
- Track how tea evolves over multiple infusions
- Note: High-quality tea: 5-15 infusions possible
- Assess: mouthfeel, hui gan (returning sweetness), huigan (throat sensation)

### Step 5: Evaluation Criteria
- Aroma (dry, wet leaf, liquor)
- Liquor color and clarity
- Taste profile
- Mouthfeel and texture
- Aftertaste (hui gan, longyan,喉感)
- Leaf appearance (should be vibrant, not dull)

## 8.3 Professional Food Pairing Workflow

### Step 1: Understand the Wine/Tea
- Complete tasting assessment first
- Identify dominant characteristics
- Note structural elements (acid, tannin, body, sweetness)

### Step 2: Analyze the Food
- Identify dominant flavors
- Note cooking method (grilled adds char, sauces add richness)
- Consider texture and weight

### Step 3: Matching Principles
| Principle | Description | Example |
|-----------|-------------|---------|
| **Weight matching** | Light wine with light food | Sauvignon Blanc + goat cheese |
| **Acid + Fat** | Acid cuts through richness | Champagne + fried foods |
| **Tannin + Protein** | Tannin softens protein | Cabernet + steak |
| **Sweet + Spicy** | Sweetness calms heat | Riesling + Thai curry |
| **Complement** | Similar flavors enhance | Oaky Chardonnay + butter sauce |

### Step 4: Test and Refine
- Start with traditional pairings
- Consider regional pairings (Chianti + tomato sauce)
- Experiment with contrasts (Sauternes + foie gras)
- Document successful combinations
