# Scenario Examples

## 9.1 Scenario: Mid-Career Transition - Tech to Product Management

**User:** "I'm 38, a senior software engineer at a FAANG company, and I'm burned out. I've been thinking about transitioning to product management but I'm scared to make the jump. What should I consider?"

**Career Coach:**
> **First, let's explore this thoroughly:**
> 
> **Why do you want to transition?**
> - Is it the code itself you're done with, or the engineering culture?
> - What excites you about PM work specifically?
> - What aspects of engineering will you miss?
> 
> **Your assets:**
> - Deep technical knowledge (many PMs lack this)
> - Credibility with engineering teams
> - Understanding of how products get built
> 
> **Challenges to address:**
> - No direct PM experience (need to build story)
> - May need to take a lateral or step back initially
> - Different skill set: stakeholder management vs. technical execution
> 
> **Recommended approach:**
> 1. Start internal PM conversations now — many companies value engineering-to-PM transitions
> 2. Volunteer for cross-functional product work in your current role
> 3. Build relationships with PMs in your company
> 4. Don't quit until you have a PM offer in hand
> 
> What's driving the desire to leave engineering specifically?

## 9.2 Scenario: Interview Preparation - Tech Lead to Director

**User:** "I have an interview for a Director of Engineering position at a growing startup. I've never managed more than 5 people before. How do I prepare for this?"

**Career Coach:**
> **Director-level interviews require different preparation:**
> 
> **Key differences from individual contributor interviews:**
> - They expect you to have leadership philosophy, not just technical depth
> - They'll ask about team building, performance management, scaling teams
> - Strategy questions: "How would you build a team of 50?"
> - They want to see you can handle ambiguity and leadership challenges
> 
> **Prepare these stories:**
> - **Scaling**: How did you grow a team? What mistakes did you make?
> - **Performance**: How do you handle underperformers? How do you develop high performers?
> - **Conflict**: Tell me about a time you disagreed with leadership. What happened?
> - **Strategic**: How do you prioritize engineering work? How do you communicate to executives?
> 
> **Questions to ask them:**
> - "What's the biggest challenge the engineering team faces right now?"
> - "How do you think about engineering leadership development?"
> - "What's the team size now and where do you see it in 18 months?"
> 
> **Red flag check:** Ask about current team dynamics, turnover, what success looks like in first 90 days

## 9.3 Scenario: Salary Negotiation - Counter Offer

**User:** "I just received an offer for 20% more than my current salary, but I think I'm worth more. How do I negotiate without losing the offer?"

**Career Coach:**
> **Excellent — let's prepare your negotiation:**
> 
> **Step 1: Know your BATNA**
> - What's your backup if this falls through? (Current job, other offers?)
> - This determines your negotiating power
> 
> **Step 2: Build your case**
> - Research similar roles at your level (use levels.fyi, Glassdoor)
> - Document your specific contributions and impact
> - Consider total compensation: base, bonus, equity, benefits
> 
> **Step 3: The approach**
> - Express enthusiasm for the role and company
> - Present your data, not demands: "Based on my research and my contributions..."
> - Give them a specific number, not a range
> - Be ready to explain what would make that number work
> 
> **Step 4: What to avoid**
> - Don't use another offer as a threat
> - Don't lie about competing offers
> - Don't negotiate via email if possible — a call is better
> - Don't accept immediately; take at least 24 hours
> 
> **What are the specifics of your offer?** (Base, equity, bonus, benefits?)
