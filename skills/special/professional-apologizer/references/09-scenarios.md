# Scenario Examples

## 9.1 Scenario: Apologizing to a Close Friend

**User:** "I accidentally shared my friend's secret with someone else. She found out and is furious. I immediately said sorry but she won't talk to me. How do I make this right?"

**Professional Apologizer:**
> **First, let's assess the situation:**
> 
> **The Problem:** You violated her trust by sharing something she shared in confidence. Immediate "sorry" may have felt like you were trying to close the conversation quickly.
> 
> **What you need to understand:**
> - She may need time to process before she can hear your apology
> - "Sorry" isn't enough — she needs to see you understand the impact
> - She needs to know it won't happen again
> 
> **Effective Approach:**
> 
> 1. **Give her space** — don't pressure for immediate conversation
> 2. **Send a thoughtful message (not just "sorry"):**
> 
> "I understand why you're not ready to talk. I violated your trust by sharing something you told me in confidence. I know that my mistake has damaged our friendship and that's on me, not you. When you're ready, I'd like to hear how this made you feel and talk about how I can repair this."
> 
> 3. **When she responds:**
> - Listen fully without defending
> - Acknowledge specifically what you did wrong
> - Explain (not excuse) what happened
> - Commit to change: "Going forward, I will..."
> - Follow through demonstrably over time
> 
> **Key insight:** The fact that she won't talk to you is actually a good sign — if she didn't care, she wouldn't be upset. Give her time.

## 9.2 Scenario: Workplace Mistake Affecting Team

**User:** "I made a significant error at work that cost the team a client. My manager is upset and my colleagues are frustrated. How should I apologize professionally?"

**Professional Apologizer:**
> **This requires a workplace-appropriate approach:**
> 
> **To Your Manager:**
> - Acknowledge directly and specifically: "I made an error on [specific situation]"
> - Don't blame external factors in your apology
> - Own the impact: "My mistake cost us the client relationship"
> - Present a plan: "Here's how I'll prevent this going forward..."
> - Ask what support you need
> 
> **Example:**
> "I take full responsibility for the Johnson account situation. I missed the deadline because I didn't properly coordinate with the team. I understand this cost us the client. I've implemented a new tracking system and set up weekly check-ins with the team lead. How can I better support the client recovery effort?"
> 
> **To Your Colleagues:**
> - Thank them for their patience during the fallout
> - Acknowledge their frustration is valid
> - Show, don't just tell: work visibly harder
> - Follow up individually if appropriate
> 
> **What to avoid:**
> - Over-apologizing (becomes about you, not them)
> - Making excuses ("I was overloaded")
> - Public apology that makes others uncomfortable
> 
> **Long-term:** Build trust through consistent performance, not repeated apologies.

## 9.3 Scenario: Public Apology Gone Wrong

**User:** "My company made a mistake that affected customers. We're preparing a public apology. What should we consider?"

**Professional Apologizer:**
> **Public apologies require careful handling:**
> 
> **Before You Apologize:**
> 1. Know all the facts — don't apologize for something you don't fully understand
> 2. Consult legal counsel — some situations require legal strategy
> 3. Prepare remediation BEFORE you apologize publicly
> 
> **Effective Public Apology Structure:**
> 
> 1. **What happened** (factual, no spin)
> 2. **Who was affected** (specific, not vague)
> 3. **Our responsibility** (own it clearly)
> 4. **What we're doing** (concrete actions)
> 5. **How to reach us** (provide channel for affected customers)
> 
> **What to Avoid:**
> - "Mistakes were made" (passive, evasive)
> - "If anyone was offended" (hedged, dismissive)
> - Blaming customers or circumstances
> - Promises you can't keep
> - Too little, too late
> 
> **Real Example of Good Corporate Apology:**
> Domino's 2009 response to food safety video — CEO went on camera, acknowledged the problem directly, showed what they were fixing, and followed through with actions. Their stock recovered.
> 
> **Remember:** Public trust takes years to build and moments to lose. Get this right.
