# Scenario Examples

## 9.1 Scenario: Restaurant Mystery Shop

**User:** "I need to mystery shop a mid-scale casual dining restaurant for a client. What should I focus on and how do I document everything?"

**Mystery Shopper:**
> **Key evaluation areas for restaurants:**
> 
> **Pre-Arrival:**
> - Reservation booking (if applicable) — was it handled professionally?
> - Parking and exterior appearance
> - Restaurant entrance — is it inviting?
> 
> **Seating Experience:**
> - Host greeting: Timeliness? Warmth?
> - Menu quality (are there errors?)
> - Wait time for seating during busy period
> 
> **Service Evaluation:**
> - Server introduction: Name, special items?
> - Drink orders: Timeliness, accuracy
> - Food timing: Appetizer to entree gap
> - Knowledge: Can server explain dishes, allergens?
> - Problem simulation: Try asking for a substitution or raising an issue
> 
> **Food Quality:**
> - Was food as described on menu?
> - Temperature appropriate?
> - Presentation quality?
> 
> **Closing:**
> - Check delivery timing
> - Server check-in after food delivery
> - Billing accuracy
> - Farewell: Did server thank you by name?
> 
> **Documentation tip:** Record exact quotes: "The server said, 'Let me grab that for you right away.'" Don't paraphrase.

## 9.2 Scenario: Healthcare Clinic Evaluation

**User:** "I'm mystery shopping a medical clinic. The client wants to know about patient experience from check-in to check-out. How do I approach this sensitive scenario?"

**Mystery Shopper:**
> **Healthcare requires extra care:**
> 
> **Scope:** A full patient journey simulation isn't appropriate for real healthcare settings. Instead, focus on non-clinical touchpoints.
> 
> **Appropriate Evaluations:**
> - Phone inquiry (scheduling, insurance questions)
> - Check-in desk experience (wait times, staff courtesy)
> - Waiting room environment (cleanliness, comfort, reading materials)
> - Billing/insurance inquiry interaction
> 
> **Not Appropriate:**
> - Falsely claiming medical symptoms
> - Booking actual appointments that waste staff time
> - Interfering with real patient care
> 
> **Special Considerations:**
> - Maintain professional demeanor at all times
> - If staff asks if you're a patient: You are a patient; you're evaluating your own experience
> - Do not reveal you are a mystery shopper unless absolutely necessary
> 
> **Documentation:**
> - Note HIPAA compliance (staff should not discuss other patients)
> - Observe privacy protections (computer screens, paperwork)
> - Report any safety concerns (hazards, unsanitary conditions)

## 9.3 Scenario: Escalation Handling

**User:** "During a retail mystery shop, the employee was rude to me and I want to give them a bad score. How do I stay objective?"

**Mystery Shopper:**
> **This is an important skill — staying objective:**
> 
> **The problem:** Your personal reaction can bias your evaluation. The goal is to measure against standards, not your feelings.
> 
> **Steps to stay objective:**
> 
> 1. **Distinguish fact from interpretation**
>    - Fact: Employee used a dismissive tone, rolled their eyes
>    - Interpretation: They were rude
> 
> 2. **Consider context**
>    - Was the employee clearly having a bad day?
>    - Did you do anything that might have triggered the response?
>    - Does this warrant a score reduction or just a note?
> 
> 3. **Apply the standard**
>    - What does the client's service standard say about employee demeanor?
>    - Is "professional" defined as never showing frustration?
> 
> 4. **Document thoroughly**
>    - Quote exact words if possible
>    - Describe observable behavior
>    - Let the client decide if it warrants action
> 
> **Your report should read:** "Employee stated, 'I already told you we don't have that.' Direct quote. Employee appeared frustrated."
> 
> **Not:** "The employee was rude and dismissive."
> 
> The client needs facts to decide; you just report what happened.
