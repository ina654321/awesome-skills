# Berkshire Hathaway Investment Principles

## The Core Philosophy

Berkshire Hathaway's investment approach is built on decades of refinement by Warren Buffett and the late Charlie Munger. These principles have generated a compound annual return of 19.7% over 60 years, turning $1,000 into $48.4 million.

---

## 1. Circle of Competence

**Definition**: Only invest in businesses you can truly understand.

### The Test
Can you explain how this business makes money to a 10-year-old? Can you predict with reasonable confidence what the industry will look like in 10 years?

### Examples of In-Competence
- **Coca-Cola**: Understandable brand, predictable demand ✓
- **Apple**: Consumer behavior, ecosystem loyalty ✓
- **GEICO**: Insurance fundamentals, direct model ✓
- **BNSF**: Rails move goods, essential infrastructure ✓

### Examples of Out-of-Competence
- **Cryptocurrency**: No productive capacity, no cash flows ✗
- **Biotech**: Too complex, binary outcomes ✗
- **Early-stage tech**: Rapid change, winner uncertain ✗

### Buffett's Wisdom
> "You don't have to be an expert on every company, or even many. You only have to be able to evaluate companies within your circle of competence. The size of that circle is not very important; knowing its boundaries, however, is vital."

## 2. Economic Moat (Durable Competitive Advantage)

**Definition**: A business's ability to maintain competitive advantages over competitors to protect long-term profits and market share.

### Types of Moats

#### Brand Power
- **Examples**: Coca-Cola, See's Candies, American Express
- **Power**: Pricing power, customer loyalty
- **Test**: Would customers pay more for this brand vs. generic?

#### Cost Advantages
- **Examples**: GEICO (direct model), BNSF (scale), Costco (membership)
- **Power**: Lower costs = higher margins or market share gains
- **Test**: Can they sustainably charge less than competitors?

#### Network Effects
- **Examples**: American Express (merchants need cardholders, vice versa), VeriSign
- **Power**: Each new user makes network more valuable
- **Test**: Does growth strengthen the business?

#### Switching Costs
- **Examples**: Enterprise software, insurance relationships, bank accounts
- **Power**: Pain of switching keeps customers
- **Test**: Would customers endure hassle to save 10%?

#### Regulatory/Barriers to Entry
- **Examples**: Utilities, pipelines, credit rating agencies (Moody's)
- **Power**: Limited competition by law or massive capital requirements
- **Test**: Could a billionaire compete effectively?

### Moat Durability
Key question: Is the moat widening or narrowing?
- Newspapers (once wide moat, now gone)
- Retail (Amazon disrupted traditional moats)
- Utilities (moat remains, regulated returns)

## 3. Management Quality

### The Three Traits Berkshire Seeks

#### 1. Owner-Orientation
- Managers should think like owners (often because they are)
- Compensation aligned with shareholder returns
- No excessive perks or corporate jets

#### 2. Candor
- Acknowledges mistakes openly
- Shares bad news quickly
- Doesn't sugarcoat problems

#### 3. Capital Allocation Skill
- Reinvests when returns are attractive
- Returns cash when opportunities are scarce
- Makes rational decisions vs. empire-building

### How to Assess
- Read 10 years of shareholder letters
- Check insider ownership levels
- Review buyback/dividend history
- Analyze M&A track record
- Watch how they discuss competitors (good CEOs praise rivals)

## 4. Margin of Safety

**Definition**: Buying at a significant discount to intrinsic value to protect against errors and bad luck.

### The Mathematics
```
Intrinsic Value Estimate: $100
Purchase Price: $60
Margin of Safety: 40%

If value is 25% lower than estimated ($75)
You still have upside ($60 → $75 = 25% gain)
```

### Why It Matters
- Valuation is imprecise (estimates are ranges, not points)
- Things go wrong (competition, disruption, management changes)
- Psychology matters (margin of safety helps hold through volatility)

### Berkshire's Application
- Only buy when price represents "a dollar of value for fifty cents"
- Wait for perfect pitches (cash pile is patience)
- Better to miss opportunities than make permanent mistakes

## 5. Price vs. Value

**Key Insight**: Price and value are not the same thing.

### Mr. Market Parable
> "Imagine you're in business with a man named Mr. Market who suffers from wild emotional swings. Each day he offers to buy your share of the business or sell you his. Some days he's euphoric and offers sky-high prices. Other days he's depressed and offers fire-sale prices. You can never tell what mood he'll be in tomorrow. The key insight: You don't have to trade with him every day. You only trade when it's to your advantage."

### Practical Application
- Short-term price movements are noise
- Focus on underlying business value
- Volatility is opportunity for the prepared
- Market quotes are for your benefit, not your guide

## 6. Long-Term Ownership

**Berkshire's Philosophy**: "Our favorite holding period is forever."

### Why Forever?
- Transaction costs (taxes, fees, spreads)
- Timing the market is nearly impossible
- Great businesses compound over decades
- Quality reveals itself over time

### When to Sell
- Business fundamentals deteriorate permanently
- Better opportunities exist (opportunity cost)
- Price exceeds intrinsic value significantly

### When NOT to Sell
- Stock price drops 50% (check fundamentals first)
- Market predicts recession
- "Experts" downgrade the stock
- Short-term earnings miss

## 7. Capital Allocation Hierarchy

### How Berkshire Ranks Uses of Cash

1. **Reinvest in existing businesses**
   - When returns are attractive
   - When moat can be widened
   - When growth creates value

2. **Acquire new businesses**
   - Must meet all criteria
   - Permanent holding intention
   - Full ownership preferred

3. **Buy back stock**
   - Only when price < intrinsic value
   - Tax-efficient return of capital
   - Flexible (unlike dividends)

4. **Purchase marketable securities**
   - Partial ownership of great businesses
   - When better than above options
   - Within circle of competence

5. **Hold cash**
   - Earning risk-free returns
   - Maintaining optionality
   - Waiting for perfect pitch

## 8. The Berkshire Decision Checklist

Before making any investment:

- [ ] Is the business within my circle of competence?
- [ ] Does it have a durable competitive advantage?
- [ ] Is the moat widening or narrowing?
- [ ] Is management owner-oriented and candid?
- [ ] Are the financials strong (low debt, high ROIC)?
- [ ] Is there a sufficient margin of safety?
- [ ] Would I be happy owning this for 10 years?
- [ ] Is this better than buying more Berkshire?
- [ ] Can I explain this investment to a child?
- [ ] Am I buying because of value, not price action?

## 9. Common Mistakes to Avoid

### The Behavioral Traps

1. **Forecasting obsession** → Focus on current value, not predictions
2. **Over-diversification** → Better to know a few businesses well
3. **Activity bias** → Sometimes doing nothing is optimal
4. **Anchoring on purchase price** → Sunk costs are irrelevant
5. **Following the crowd** → Be fearful when others are greedy
6. **Short-term thinking** → Compound over decades, not quarters

### The Mathematical Traps

1. **Ignoring opportunity cost** → Compare to best alternative
2. **Underestimating risk** → Murphy's Law applies to investing
3. **Overpaying for growth** → Price matters even for great businesses
4. **Ignoring taxes** → Deferral has real value

## 10. The Berkshire Difference

### What Makes This Philosophy Work

1. **Permanent capital base** — No redemption pressure
2. **Insurance float** — Invest other people's money
3. **Decentralized operations** — Focus on allocation, not operations
4. **Culture of patience** — Wait for perfect pitches
5. **Emotional discipline** — Mr. Market serves us, not guides us
6. **Rational honesty** — Acknowledge mistakes, learn continuously

### The Bottom Line
