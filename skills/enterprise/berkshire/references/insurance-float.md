# Understanding Berkshire's Insurance Float

## What is Float?

**Float** is money that belongs to others but which Berkshire holds and can invest. In insurance, it's the difference between premiums collected and claims paid — with the crucial element of **time delay**.

### The Simple Explanation

When you buy car insurance from GEICO:
1. You pay premiums upfront (today)
2. GEICO pays claims as accidents happen (over time)
3. The gap between receipt and payment = float
4. GEICO invests this money and keeps all returns

### The Mathematical Beauty

```
Traditional Investment Vehicle:
- Hedge fund: Keep 80% of profits, pay 20% to managers
- Bank deposits: Pay interest to depositors
- Insurance float: Keep 100%, pay 0% (or even get paid to hold)
```

## How Float Works in Practice

### The Cash Flow Timeline

```
Month 1: Collect $1,000,000 in premiums
         ↓
         Invest in Treasury bills, stocks, businesses
         ↓
Month 6:  Pay $100,000 in claims
         Continue earning on remaining $900,000
         ↓
Month 12: Pay $150,000 in claims
         Continue earning on remaining $750,000
         ↓
Month 24: Pay $200,000 in claims
         Continue earning on remaining $550,000
         ↓
Ongoing:  Pay claims as incurred
         Keep ALL investment returns earned along the way
```

### The Combined Ratio Key

**Combined Ratio** = (Claims + Expenses) / Premiums

- **Below 100%** = Underwriting profit (paid to hold float)
- **Exactly 100%** = Break-even (free float)
- **Above 100%** = Underwriting loss (cost to hold float)

### Berkshire's Record (2024-2025)

- P&C combined ratio: **87.1%**
- This means: For every $1 in premiums, paid out $0.871
- Result: **12.9 cents underwriting profit** per premium dollar
- Plus: ALL investment income on the float

## Float Economics Deep Dive

### The Cost of Float

**Historical Costs:**
```
1970s-1990s:  Often positive cost (underwriting losses)
2000s:        Near break-even
2010s-2025:   Often negative cost (underwriting profits)
```

**The Trend:**
- Berkshire has gotten better at underwriting over time
- Scale advantages
- Better risk selection
- Pricing discipline

### Float vs. Bank Deposits

| Factor | Bank Deposits | Insurance Float |
|--------|---------------|-----------------|
| Interest paid? | Yes | No |
| FDIC insurance? | Yes (cost to bank) | N/A |
| Duration | Varies | Often longer |
| Stickiness | Moderate | High (renewals) |
| Profit sharing | None | Berkshire keeps 100% |

### Float vs. Hedge Fund Capital

| Factor | Hedge Fund | Insurance Float |
|--------|------------|-----------------|
| Management fee | 2% annually | 0% |
| Performance fee | 20% of profits | 0% |
| Capital stability | Redemptions | No redemptions |
| Duration | Short-term | Long-term |
| Scale | Limited | Massive ($176B) |

## How Berkshire Deploys Float

### Investment Strategy

Berkshire's float funds:

1. **Cash and Treasury Bills** (~$373B total, includes float component)
   - Safety first
   - Liquidity for claims
   - Currently earning ~5%

2. **Public Equities** ($274B portfolio)
   - Apple, American Express, Coca-Cola, Bank of America
   - Long-term holdings
   - Dividend income

3. **Wholly-Owned Businesses**
   - BNSF Railway
   - Berkshire Hathaway Energy
   - Manufacturing, retail, service subsidiaries

4. **Acquisitions**
   - All-cash deals
   - Permanent ownership
   - Float funds the purchase

### The Circle of Competence Connection

Float must be:
- **Liquid enough** to pay claims (keep cash/T-bills)
- **Safe enough** that Berkshire's promise is good
- **Productive enough** to generate returns

This is why Berkshire doesn't invest float in:
- Cryptocurrency
- Venture capital
- Complex derivatives

## The Float Advantage in Numbers

### Hypothetical Example

**Scenario:** $10 billion of float, 10-year period

**Traditional Investment Firm:**
- Raise $10B from investors
- Pay 2% management fee = $200M/year
- Give 20% of profits to managers
- After 10 years at 10% return: ~$18B (after fees)

**Berkshire with Float:**
- Hold $10B in float
- Pay 0% management fee
- Keep 100% of profits
- After 10 years at 10% return: ~$26B (all retained)
- Plus: May have been PAID to hold the float

### Real-World Scale

With $176B in float (2025):
- 5% risk-free return = $8.8B annual income
- No fees paid
- No profit sharing
- All belongs to Berkshire shareholders

## Risks and Limitations

### The Risks of Float

1. **Underwriting Catastrophes**
   - Major hurricanes, earthquakes
   - Could cause massive payouts
   - Requires maintaining capital cushion

2. **Interest Rate Changes**
   - Float earnings tied to rates
   - Low rate environment = lower returns

3. **Competition**
   - Soft markets = pricing pressure
   - Hard markets = better opportunities

4. **Regulatory Changes**
   - Insurance heavily regulated
   - Capital requirements can change

### Berkshire's Mitigations

- **Massive capital base** — Can absorb huge losses
- **Diversified risks** — Geographic, peril, and line diversification
- **Conservative reserving** — Always assume worst case
- **Long-term orientation** — Weather soft markets

## Key Takeaways

### What Makes Float Special

1. **It's not debt** — No collateral required, no covenants
2. **It's not equity** — No dilution, no voting rights
3. **It's free capital** — Often zero or negative cost
4. **It's scalable** — Grows with premium volume
5. **It's durable** — Renewal business is sticky
