# Mayo Clinic Nobel Laureates

## 1950 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine

### Recipients
- **Dr. Edward Calvin Kendall** (1886-1972) — Biochemist at Mayo Clinic
- **Dr. Philip Showalter Hench** (1896-1965) — Rheumatologist at Mayo Clinic
- **Dr. Tadeus Reichstein** (1897-1996) — Swiss biochemist (independent research)

### Award Citation
> "For their discoveries relating to the hormones of the adrenal cortex, their structure and biological effects."

---

## Dr. Edward Calvin Kendall

### Background
- Born: March 8, 1886, South Norwalk, Connecticut
- PhD: Columbia University (1910)
- Joined Mayo Clinic: 1914
- Retired: 1951 (continued as visiting professor at Princeton)
- Died: May 4, 1972

### Scientific Contributions

#### Thyroid Research (1910s-1920s)
- Isolated **L-thyroxine** — the active hormone of the thyroid gland
- First crystallization of thyroid hormone
- Enabled treatment of hypothyroidism and goiter

#### Adrenal Cortex Research (1930s-1940s)
- Isolated six hormones from adrenal glands (Compounds A through F)
- **Compound E** later became known as **cortisone**
- Collaborated with Dr. Philip Hench on clinical applications

### Key Publication
"Chemical Studies on the Thyroid Hormone" (1919) — Landmark work on thyroid biochemistry

---

## Dr. Philip Showalter Hench

### Background
- Born: February 28, 1896, Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania
- MD: University of Pittsburgh (1920)
- Joined Mayo Clinic: 1926
- Specialty: Rheumatology (Mayo Clinic's first rheumatologist)
- Died: March 30, 1965 (while vacationing in Jamaica)

### Scientific Contributions

#### Observations Leading to Discovery
- Noted patients with rheumatoid arthritis improved under certain conditions:
  - Pregnancy
  - Jaundice
  - Surgery
- Hypothesized existence of "**Substance X**" — natural anti-rheumatic compound

#### Cortisone Clinical Trials
| Date | Milestone |
|------|-----------|
| 1948 | First clinical trial of cortisone for rheumatoid arthritis |
| Sept 1948 | Requested Compound E from Merck for testing |
| 1948-1949 | Dramatic improvements observed in arthritic patients |
| 1950 | Nobel Prize awarded |

#### The Cortisone Team
- **Dr. Charles H. Slocumb** — Clinical collaborator
- **Dr. Howard F. Polley** — Clinical collaborator
- **Dr. Edward C. Kendall** — Biochemical isolation

### Key Insight
Hench hypothesized that "Substance X" was likely an adrenal hormone — leading to the collaboration with Kendall on Compound E (cortisone).

---

## Impact of the Discovery

### Medical Revolution
The cortisone discovery revolutionized treatment for:
- Rheumatoid arthritis
- Lupus (systemic lupus erythematosus)
- Inflammatory bowel disease
- Asthma
- Allergic conditions
- Addison's disease
- Countless inflammatory and autoimmune conditions

### Pharmaceutical Development
- Led to development of entire class of **corticosteroid drugs**
- Prednisone, prednisolone, hydrocortisone, and derivatives
- Still among the most prescribed medications worldwide

### Research Corporation Patent
- Kendall assigned cortisone patent administration to Research Corporation
- **Kendall-Hench Fund** established with $100,000 grant from Merck
- Funded endocrinology research

## Legacy Today

### Continued Research Excellence
- Mayo Clinic researchers hold **250+ NIH R01 grants**
- Annual research budget: **$1.27 billion** (2024)
- Second-greatest recipient of NIH funding in Minnesota
- 5,000+ employees involved in research

### Corticosteroid Evolution
70+ years after the Nobel Prize:
- Corticosteroids remain first-line therapy for many conditions
- Research continues on minimizing side effects
- New delivery methods (inhaled, topical, localized)
- Combination therapies reducing steroid dependence

### Mayo Clinic's Research Culture
The Nobel-winning collaboration exemplifies:
- **Translational research** — Laboratory to clinic
- **Multidisciplinary teams** — Biochemist + clinician collaboration
- **Patient-driven inquiry** — Clinical observations driving research
- **Long-term persistence** — Decades of research leading to breakthrough
