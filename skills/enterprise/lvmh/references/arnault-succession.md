# The Arnault Succession: LVMH Family Governance

## Overview

Bernard Arnault (born March 5, 1949) has led LVMH as Chairman and CEO since 1989. At 76 years old, succession planning is the most closely watched aspect of LVMH governance. Unlike typical corporate succession, LVMH operates as a family-controlled empire with five heirs positioned across key Maisons.

### Decision-Making Protocol

| Decision Type | Authority |
|---------------|-----------|
| **Strategic** (M&A, major investments) | Board + Family holding |
| **Operational** (Maison P&L) | Maison CEO + Group MD |
| **Creative** (design, collections) | Creative Director (autonomous) |
| **Succession** (CEO appointments) | Bernard + Executive Committee |

### Post-Bernard Scenario

Per 2022 restructuring:
- Agache Commandite SAS will control Agache SCA
- Five children each own 20% with 30-year lock-up
- Majority of 3 children required for decisions
- LVMH Board independence maintained

## Succession Scenarios & Analysis

### Scenario 1: Bernard Continues (Current)
- Age 76; tenure extended to 85 (2034)
- Stéphane Bianchi as Group Managing Director (operational chief)
- Five children in operating roles; learning/proving

### Scenario 2: Family Office Model
- No single successor; collective governance
- External professional CEO (non-family)
- Family holds board control via Agache

### Scenario 3: Single Heir Emergence
- Most likely: Delphine (seniority, Dior CEO) or Antoine (governance role)
- Other children remain in operating roles
- External Group Managing Director supports

### Scenario 4: External Leadership
- Professional CEO from outside (Stéphane Bianchi or other)
- Family retains ownership control
- Similar to Richemont model (Johann Rupert + professional CEO)

## Implications for LVMH Brand Directors

### Decision-Making Filters
1. **All major initiatives** viewed through succession lens
2. **Cross-Maison coordination** requires understanding which heir is involved
3. **Long-term investments** (10+ years) must survive governance transition
4. **Board presentations** should address multi-heir implications
