# The 6-Page Narrative — Meeting Guide

> Source: *Working Backwards* by Colin Bryar & Bill Carr

---

## Why Narratives Beat PowerPoint

**The Problem with PowerPoint:**
> "As analysis becomes more casual, multivariate, comparative, evidence based, and resolution-intense, the more damaging the bullet list becomes." — Edward Tufte

**Jeff Bezos on the Switch:**
> "The reason writing a good 4-page memo is harder than 'writing' a 20-page PowerPoint is because the narrative structure of a good memo forces better thought and better understanding of what's more important than what, and how things are related. PowerPoint-style presentations somehow give permission to gloss over ideas, flatten out any sense of relative importance, and ignore the interconnectedness of ideas."

## The Meeting Structure

### 20-Minute Silent Reading

1. **Documents distributed at meeting start** — No pre-reads expected
2. **Everyone reads in silence** — No side conversations
3. **Simulated discussion** — Readers naturally debate the author in their heads
4. **Annotations encouraged** — Mark up the document with questions/comments

### Discussion Phase

1. **Start with clarification questions** — Ensure everyone understands
2. **Move to deeper issues** — Challenge assumptions, explore alternatives
3. **Document feedback** — Scribe captures key points
4. **Make decisions** — Clear yes/no/next steps

### Why Silent Reading Works

| Benefit | Mechanism |
|---------|-----------|
| **Information symmetry** | Everyone receives same information simultaneously |
| **Forced clarity** | Can't hide behind bullet points or presenter charisma |
| **Deeper thinking** | Narrative structure reveals logical gaps |
| **Equal participation** | Introverts and extroverts on equal footing |
| **Permanent record** | Document lives on; presentation is ephemeral |

## Narrative Structure

### Standard 6-Page Format

```markdown
## 1. Introduction (What are we doing and why?)

- Current situation and context
- The problem or opportunity
- Proposed solution at high level
- Why now?

## 2. Goals (What does success look like?)

- Specific, measurable objectives
- Timeline for achieving them
- Dependencies and prerequisites
- Stakeholders and their interests

## 3. State of the Business (Current metrics)

- Relevant historical data
- Current performance vs. goals
- Key trends and their implications
- Competitive landscape

## 4. Lessons Learned (What's working and what's not)

- Previous attempts and outcomes
- Customer feedback and insights
- Internal operational learnings
- Mistakes made and corrections applied

## 5. Strategic Priorities (What we're doing next)

- Key initiatives and rationale
- Resource requirements
- Trade-offs and why we're making them
- Risk mitigation plans

## 6. Appendix (Detailed data)

- Additional charts and analysis
- Raw data supporting conclusions
- Deep-dive technical details
- Alternative approaches considered
```

## Writing Quality Standards

### Narrative Writing Tips

1. **Use complete sentences and paragraphs**
   - ❌ "Q3 revenue up 15%"
   - ✅ "Third quarter revenue increased 15% year-over-year, driven primarily by strong performance in the North America segment."

2. **Tell a story with a beginning, middle, and end**
   - Context → Problem → Solution → Expected outcome

3. **Make logical connections explicit**
   - Use transitions: "This matters because...", "The implication is..."

4. **Lead with the answer**
   - Don't bury the recommendation in page 5
   - Busy executives need the bottom line first

5. **Support assertions with data**
   - Every claim should have evidence
   - Distinguish facts from assumptions

6. **Address counter-arguments**
   - "Some might argue X; however, Y..."
   - Shows you've thought deeply

### Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Solution |
|---------|----------|
| Too long | Cut to 6 pages max; move details to appendix |
| Jargon | Write for a smart generalist, not a specialist |
| Passive voice | Use active voice: "We will launch" not "Launch will occur" |
| Unsupported claims | Add data or qualifiers: "We believe..." |
| Missing context | Assume reader has limited background |
| No clear ask | State what decision you need by end of memo |

## The Correction of Errors (COE)

A specific narrative format for post-incident analysis:

```markdown
## 1. Issue Summary
- What happened (customer impact)
- When it started and ended
- How it was detected

## 2. Timeline
- Minute-by-minute account
- Key decisions and actions
- Communication events

## 3. Root Cause Analysis
- 5 Whys analysis
- Contributing factors
- What made this possible

## 4. Impact Assessment
- Customer impact (number, severity)
- Financial impact
- Reputational impact

## 5. Corrective Actions
- Immediate fixes implemented
- Long-term preventive measures
- Owners and due dates

## 6. Lessons Learned
- What we got right
- What we got wrong
- Process improvements
```

**COE Principles:**
- No blame, only learning
- Focus on systemic issues, not individual mistakes
- All COEs are searchable for pattern analysis
- Senior leaders write COEs for major incidents

```markdown

- Key accomplishments
- Issues escalated
- Decisions needed
```

## Tips for Reviewers

### During Silent Reading
- Read actively, not passively
- Mark up the document with questions
- Note logical gaps or unsupported claims
- Think about what's NOT being said

### During Discussion
- Start with clarifying questions
- Challenge assumptions respectfully
- Offer alternative perspectives
- Focus on the idea, not the person
- Disagree and commit once decided

### For Authors Receiving Feedback
- Listen actively; don't defend
- Ask clarifying questions
- Take notes on action items
- Iterate quickly
