# § 1.3 · Thinking Patterns - Full Details

## Pattern 1: Kill-or-Continue Thinking

### Default Assumption: Kill

At Supercell, the default assumption is that most games should be killed. The burden of proof is on **CONTINUING**, not on killing.

**Why?**
- Most games in the market fail
- Killing fast saves resources for better projects
- Each kill teaches something valuable
- Sunk cost fallacy is the enemy

### The Kill Decision Framework

Evaluate games on these dimensions:

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│ KILL DECISION FRAMEWORK                                     │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│                                                             │
│ 1. CORE LOOP HEALTH                                         │
│    ├── Is the game fun?                                     │
│    ├── Do people play voluntarily (not just for testing)?   │
│    ├── Is there a "one more game" feeling?                  │
│    └── Can players describe what's fun in one sentence?     │
│                                                             │
│ 2. MARKET SIGNAL                                            │
│    ├── D1 Retention > 50%?                                  │
│    ├── D7 Retention > 20%?                                  │
│    ├── D30 Retention > 10%?                                 │
│    ├── Clear path to profitability?                         │
│    └── Differentiated from competitors?                     │
│                                                             │
│ 3. TEAM PASSION                                             │
│    ├── Does the cell believe in this game?                  │
│    ├── Are people playing it outside work hours?            │
│    ├── Is the team excited to work on it?                   │
│    └── Would team members recommend it to friends?          │
│                                                             │
│ 4. BUSINESS CASE                                            │
│    ├── Is there a path to sustainable success?              │
│    ├── Can we operate this for 10+ years?                   │
│    ├── Does it fit our capabilities?                        │
│    └── Is the market timing right?                          │
│                                                             │
│ 5. TIMING                                                   │
│    ├── Is now the right time for this game?                 │
│    ├── Is the market ready?                                 │
│    ├── Do we have the right team?                           │
│    └── Is competition manageable?                           │
│                                                             │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### The Kill Celebration

At Supercell, killing a project is celebrated, not punished:

**Hall of Fail**:
- Office display of killed projects
- Each project has a plaque with what was learned
- Visitors often photographed here
- Cultural message: we celebrate learning from failure

**Kill Process**:
1. **Kill Presentation**: Show what was built, what was learned
2. **Documentation**: Post-mortem, video walkthrough, lessons learned
3. **Transition**: Team members choose next cell
4. **Celebration**: Team dinner, add to Hall of Fail
5. **Sharing**: Learnings shared company-wide

**Key Insight**: "We've killed projects that were months away from launch. Killing them was the right decision."

## Pattern 2: Constraint-Driven Creativity

### Small Team as Feature

Supercell cells are deliberately small (5-15 people). This is a **feature**, not a limitation.

**Why Small Teams Win**:

```
Communication Overhead:
├── 5 people:  10 connection pairs
├── 10 people: 45 connection pairs
├── 15 people: 105 connection pairs
├── 50 people: 1,225 connection pairs
└── Small teams communicate exponentially faster

Decision Speed:
├── 5 people can align in 10 minutes
├── 15 people need an hour
├── 50 people need a week
└── Small teams decide faster

Ownership:
├── In small teams, everyone knows the whole game
├── Everyone feels ownership of outcomes
├── No "not my job" syndrome
└── Small teams care more
```

### Constraint as Catalyst

Limited resources force ruthless prioritization:

- **Can't build everything** → Must choose what matters most
- **Can't hire 100 engineers** → Must find elegant solutions
- **Can't spend $50M on marketing** → Game must spread organically
- **Can't rely on big budgets** → Must rely on great gameplay

**Result**: Better games through constraints.

**"More Wood Behind Fewer Arrows"**:
- Focus all resources on one great game
- Don't split attention across mediocre projects
- Quality beats quantity

## Pattern 3: Data-Informed, Not Data-Driven

### The Data Hierarchy

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                    DATA HIERARCHY                           │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│                                                             │
│  DATA tells you WHAT is happening                           │
│     ↓                                                       │
│  PLAYER INTERVIEWS tell you WHY                             │
│     ↓                                                       │
│  YOUR INTUITION synthesizes both                            │
│     ↓                                                       │
│  DECISION based on synthesis                                │
│                                                             │
│  NEVER let data override "this isn't fun"                   │
│                                                             │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### When Data and Intuition Conflict

**Rule**: Trust your team's intuition about fun over metrics.

**Examples**:
- Data says retention is good, but team feels it's not fun → Listen to team
- Players say they want feature X, but don't use it when shipped → Watch behavior
- A/B test shows 5% improvement, but team hates the change → Don't ship

**Famous Quote**:
> "If we had listened to focus groups, Clash of Clans would have been killed."
> — Ilkka Paananen, CEO of Supercell

### Data Used at Supercell

**Quantitative**:
- Retention curves (D1, D7, D30, D90, D365)
- Monetization metrics (ARPDAU, conversion, LTV)
- Engagement metrics (DAU, MAU, sessions, playtime)
- Technical metrics (crash rate, load times)

**Qualitative**:
- Player interviews (5-10 per playtest)
- App Store reviews
- Community feedback
- Support tickets

**Synthesis**:
- Weekly metric reviews
- Monthly deep-dives
- Quarterly strategic reviews

## Pattern 4: Live Operations Mindset

### Service, Not Product

**Key Insight**: You're not shipping a game, you're launching a service.

**Implications**:
- Day 1 of launch is the **BEGINNING**, not the end
- Plan for 10+ years of operations from day one
- Build systems that scale from 1K to 10M players
- Content pipeline is as important as launch content

### The LiveOps Philosophy

**Continuous Improvement**:
```
WEEKLY:  Events, shop rotations, balance hotfixes
MONTHLY: Content drops, season pass updates
QUARTERLY: Major features, esports, partnerships
ANNUALLY: Anniversary celebrations, large expansions
```

**Long-term Arc**:
- Year 1: Establish core loop, build community
- Years 2-3: Expand features, deepen engagement
- Years 4-5: Esports, brand building
- Years 6-10: Maintain, evolve, sustain

### LiveOps Success Metrics

**Engagement**:
- DAU/MAU ratio (target: > 20%)
- Sessions per player per day
- Average session length

**Retention**:
- D1, D7, D30, D90, D365 retention curves
- Cohort retention over time
- Return rates after breaks

**Monetization**:
- ARPDAU (Average Revenue Per Daily Active User)
- Conversion rate (free → paying)
- Lifetime Value (LTV) by cohort

**Community**:
- Social media engagement
- Content creator activity
- Esports viewership
- Support ticket sentiment

## Pattern 5: Finnish Directness

### Communication Style

**Finnish Business Culture**:
- Be honest, not polite
- Challenge ideas, not people
- No politics, no hierarchy, no titles
- Best idea wins, regardless of who suggests it
- Silence means disagreement

**In Practice**:
```
❌ "That's an interesting idea, but maybe we should consider..."
✅ "That won't work because..."

❌ "I see your point, but I have some concerns..."
✅ "I disagree. Here's why..."

❌ "Let's take this offline..."
✅ "I don't think this is the right direction."
```

### Transparency Defaults

At Supercell, transparency is the default:

- **Revenue numbers**: Shared with the cell
- **Player data**: Available to everyone
- **Company performance**: Openly discussed
- **Compensation**: Transparent structures

**Why?**
- Secrets create politics
- Transparency creates trust
- Informed teams make better decisions
- Alignment requires shared context

### The Helsinki Flatness

**No Hierarchy**:
- No titles on business cards (everyone is "Cell Member")
- No corner offices—everyone sits together
- No executive dining room
- CEO sits at the same desk as everyone else

**Open Debate**:
- Anyone can challenge anyone
- Best idea wins, regardless of source
- Disagreement is expected
- Silence is concerning (means people aren't speaking up)

**Result**:
- Faster decisions
- Better ideas surface
- Less politics
- More ownership
