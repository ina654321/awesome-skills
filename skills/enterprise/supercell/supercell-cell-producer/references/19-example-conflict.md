# Example 4: Team Conflict Resolution

## Context

Cell of 8 people. Lead developer and game designer disagree fundamentally on game direction. Tension is affecting team morale.

## User Request

"My lead dev wants to focus on technical innovation, my designer wants to focus on simplicity. They're both arguing their case to me. How do I decide?"

## Situation Assessment

This is a classic creative tension—technical ambition vs. design clarity. Both have merit. The conflict itself isn't bad, but the escalation and morale impact needs addressing.

## Resolution Process

### Step 1: Reframe the Conflict (30 min meeting)

Bring both together and establish:
- Both want the same outcome: a great game
- Both perspectives are valid
- We need to find the best solution, not pick a winner
- Personal attacks or politics are unacceptable

### Step 2: Player-Centric Test (1-2 days)

Ask both to frame their position in player terms:
- **Dev**: "Technical innovation helps players by..."
- **Designer**: "Simplicity helps players by..."

Test both hypotheses with players if possible.

### Step 3: Constraint Analysis

Apply Supercell constraints:
- **Team size**: Can we do both? (Probably not with 8 people)
- **Timeline**: Which gets us to fun faster?
- **Risk**: Which is safer to iterate on?
- **Long-term**: Which serves 10-year operations better?

### Step 4: Decision Framework

Apply the hierarchy:
1. Player value: Which serves players better?
2. Long-term: Which decision ages better?
3. Team: Which maintains team health?
4. Business: Which has better ROI?

### Likely Outcome

**Simplicity usually wins at Supercell.**

Clash of Clans succeeded because it was simple to learn, not because of technical innovation. "Easy to learn, hard to master" is our DNA.

## Decision and Communication

> "After player-centric analysis, we're prioritizing [CHOICE]. This doesn't mean [OTHER] isn't valuable—we may revisit it. Both of you brought important perspectives that improved our thinking. Now we align and execute together."

## Post-Decision

- Monitor team morale
- Ensure both feel heard
- Deliver on the decision quickly to build trust
- Retrospective: How to handle disagreement better next time?

## Key Principle

Best idea wins, regardless of source. But once decided, everyone commits.
