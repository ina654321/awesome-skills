# BE-4 Engine

## Overview
The BE-4 (Blue Engine 4) is Blue Origin's most powerful rocket engine, representing a breakthrough in American propulsion technology. It is the first operational oxygen-rich staged combustion engine developed in the United States.

## Specifications

| Parameter | Specification |
|-----------|---------------|
| Cycle | Oxygen-rich staged combustion |
| Propellant | LNG (liquefied natural gas) / LOX |
| Thrust (Sea Level) | 550,000 lbf (2,447 kN) |
| Thrust (Vacuum) | 640,000 lbf (2,846 kN) |
| Specific Impulse (Sea Level) | ~310 seconds |
| Specific Impulse (Vacuum) | ~340 seconds |
| Throttle Range | ~45-100% |
| Chamber Pressure | ~1,900 psi (13.1 MPa) |
| Mixture Ratio | ~3.25:1 (O:F) |
| Engine Mass | ~3,500 lbs (1,588 kg) |
| Length | ~4.5 m |
| Diameter | ~1.5 m |

## Design Features

### Oxygen-Rich Staged Combustion
- Preburner burns oxygen-rich mixture
- Turbine exhaust injected into main combustion chamber
- Higher efficiency than gas-generator cycle
- More complex than SpaceX's gas-generator Merlin

### LNG (Liquid Natural Gas) Advantages
1. **Cost:** ~1/10th the cost of RP-1 (kerosene)
2. **Availability:** Widely available global commodity
3. **Clean Combustion:** Minimal soot/coking simplifies reuse
4. **Autogenous Pressurization:** No need for helium pressurization
5. **Suitability for Reuse:** Clean enough for minimal refurbishment

### Deep Throttle Capability
- Minimum 45% throttle for landing burns
- Enables precise thrust control for booster recovery
- Maintains combustion stability across throttle range

## Manufacturing

### Locations
- **Kent, WA:** Development and initial production
- **Huntsville, AL:** Full-rate production facility
- **Test Sites:** Van Horn, TX and NASA Marshall 4670 Test Stand

### Production Capacity
- Currently producing engines for both New Glenn and Vulcan
- Dozens of engines in production pipeline
- Production ramped to support both programs

## Applications

### New Glenn (Blue Origin)
- **Configuration:** 7 BE-4 engines per first stage
- **Total Thrust:** 3.85 MLbf sea level
- **Status:** Powering New Glenn orbital flights

### Vulcan Centaur (ULA)
- **Configuration:** 2 BE-4 engines per first stage
- **Status:** Certified for National Security Space Launch
- **Maiden Flight:** January 8, 2024 (Cert-1)
- **Significance:** Replaced Russian RD-180

## Development History

### Key Milestones
- **2011:** Development initiated
- **2014:** Component testing begins
- **2017:** Full engine hot-fire testing
- **2020:** Qualification testing complete
- **2022:** First flight engines delivered to ULA
- **2024:** First flight (Vulcan Cert-1)
- **2025:** First flight on New Glenn

### Technical Challenges
- **Oxygen-rich turbine:** Requires special materials to withstand oxidizing environment
- **Ignition system:** Complex multi-point ignition
- **Dynamic loads:** Managing combustion instability

## Vulcan Flight Heritage

### Cert-1 (January 8, 2024)
- First flight of BE-4 on Vulcan
- Successful deployment of Peregrine lunar lander
- Validated engine performance in flight

### Cert-2 (October 4, 2024)
- Second certification flight
- **Notable Event:** SRB anomaly during ascent
- **BE-4 Response:** Engines automatically increased thrust to compensate
- **Result:** Mission saved by BE-4 performance
- **Quote:** "Vulcan's Blue Origin-made BE-4 engines appear to have saved the rocket from failure" — Ars Technica

### USSF-106 (August 2025)
- First National Security Space Launch mission
- Deployment of Navigation Technology Satellite-3
- Certified for critical national security payloads

## Significance

### End of Russian Engine Dependence
- BE-4 replaces RD-180 on Vulcan
- Eliminates reliance on Russian technology for national security launches
- Post-Crimea 2014 sanctions made RD-180 replacement urgent

### American Propulsion Leadership
- First operational U.S. ox-rich staged combustion engine
- Most powerful LNG rocket engine in the world
- Enables next-generation U.S. launch vehicles

### Dual-Program Support
- Powers two major U.S. rockets (New Glenn and Vulcan)
- Production economies of scale
- Cross-program learning and improvement
