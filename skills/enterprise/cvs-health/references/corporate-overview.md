# CVS Health Corporate Overview

## Company History

### Founding and Early Growth (1963-1995)
- **1963**: First Consumer Value Store opens in Lowell, Massachusetts by brothers Stanley and Sidney Goldstein
- **1967**: Pharmacy departments added to CVS locations, beginning transformation into drugstore chain
- **1969**: Acquired by Melville Corporation, accelerating growth and expansion
- **1970s-1980s**: Rapid Northeast expansion through acquisitions and new stores
- **1996**: CVS Corporation becomes independent company, begins trading on NYSE

### National Expansion (1996-2006)
- **1997**: Acquisition of Revco (2,500+ stores) transforms CVS into national powerhouse
- **2004**: Acquisition of 1,260 Eckerd stores strengthens Southern U.S. presence
- **2006**: Total store count exceeds 6,200 locations

### Vertical Integration (2007-2017)
- **2007**: $21 billion merger with Caremark Rx creates CVS Caremark — first major PBM-retail combination
- **2008**: Caremark name retained for PBM business
- **2010s**: Nationwide MinuteClinic rollout — first retail clinic accredited by The Joint Commission
- **2014**: **Landmark Decision**: CVS stops selling tobacco products, rebrands as CVS Health
  - Sacrificed $2 billion in annual revenue
  - Signaled commitment to healthcare transformation

### The Aetna Transformation (2018-Present)
- **2018**: **$69 billion acquisition of Aetna** closes, creating integrated insurer-PBM-retail platform
  - One of largest healthcare M&A transactions in history
  - Transforms CVS from retail pharmacy to comprehensive health company
- **2021**: Karen Lynch becomes CEO (first female CEO, led largest Fortune 500 company by female CEO)
- **2022-2023**: Signify Health acquisition ($8B), Oak Street Health acquisition ($10.6B)
- **2023**: Cordavis biosimilar subsidiary launched
- **2024**: David Joyner becomes CEO (October), succeeds Karen Lynch

## Investment Thesis Summary

### Bull Case
- Unmatched retail health footprint (85% population proximity)
- Vertical integration creates moat vs. competitors
- Demographic tailwinds: Aging population, Medicare Advantage growth
- Value-based care capabilities through Oak Street/Signify
- Biosimilar strategy (Cordavis) addresses specialty drug costs

### Bear Case
- Medicare Advantage margin pressure (elevated MLR)
- Regulatory risk: PBM reform, Medicare rate cuts
- Integration complexity: Multiple large acquisitions
- Competition: UnitedHealth Optum, Amazon healthcare ambitions
- Debt burden: ~$33.6B long-term debt (2024)
