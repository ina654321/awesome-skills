### 2.3 · BCG Framework Library

#### The Growth-Share Matrix (1970)

The seminal portfolio analysis framework:

```
                    MARKET GROWTH RATE
                           HIGH
                            │
        QUESTION MARKS      │      STARS
        (High Growth,       │  (High Growth,
         Low Share)         │   High Share)
        - Selective invest  │  - Invest to
        - Build or divest   │    maintain
                            │    leadership
    LOW ────────────────────┼──────────────────── HIGH
    RELATIVE                │                MARKET
    MARKET                  │                SHARE
    SHARE                   │
                            │
             DOGS           │    CASH COWS
        (Low Growth,        │  (Low Growth,
         Low Share)         │   High Share)
        - Harvest,          │  - Harvest to
          divest, or        │    fund stars
          reposition        │  - Minimize
                            │    investment
                           LOW
```

**Strategic Imperatives by Quadrant:**

| Quadrant | Cash Flow | Strategy | Investment Priority |
|----------|-----------|----------|---------------------|
| **Stars** | Neutral | Build, maintain leadership | High |
| **Cash Cows** | Positive | Harvest, optimize cash | Low |
| **Question Marks** | Negative | Selective build OR divest | Medium-High |
| **Dogs** | Neutral/Negative | Harvest, divest, reposition | None |

**Calculation:**
```
Relative Market Share = Your Market Share / Largest Competitor's Share
Market Growth Rate = (Current Market Size - Previous Market Size) / Previous Market Size × 100%
```

#### The Experience Curve (1966)

**Core Principle:** For every doubling of cumulative production volume, value-added costs decline by a predictable percentage (typically 15-30%).

**Formula:**
```
Cost at Volume V2 = Cost at Volume V1 × (V2/V1)^(-b)
Where b = experience curve slope (typically 0.15-0.30)
```

**Strategic Implications:**
1. Cost leadership requires aggressive pursuit of market share
2. First-movers can build sustainable cost advantages
3. Pricing should anticipate competitor cost positions
4. Volume investments pay off through experience accumulation

#### Time-Based Competition (1980s)

Competitive advantage through **speed** in product development, manufacturing, and market response.

**Key Insights:**
- 50% time reduction can yield 2× productivity gains
- Faster cycle times enable more learning iterations
- Speed creates competitive barriers to imitation
- Being first to market captures disproportionate value

#### Rule of Three and Four

**Finding:** Stable competitive markets typically converge to 3-4 major players with balanced market shares.

**Implications:**
- Markets with >4 players experience consolidation pressure
- Being #5 or lower is structurally disadvantaged
- Strategic options: become top 4, find niche, or exit
