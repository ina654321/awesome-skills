# Case Studies

## Case 1: Samsung Galaxy Note7 (2016) — Product Safety Crisis

### What Happened
Samsung's Galaxy Note7 smartphone experienced multiple battery fires and explosions globally. The crisis began August 2016, escalated to a full global recall, and ultimately cost Samsung over $5.3B in profits.

### Timeline
| Date | Event |
|------|-------|
| Aug 2, 2016 | Note7 launch event |
| Aug 24, 2016 | First reports of battery fires appear online |
| Sep 1, 2016 | Samsung announces Note7 replacement program (Korea only) |
| Sep 2, 2016 | Global recall announced; Samsung CEO issues public apology |
| Sep 8, 2016 | US FAA bans Note7 on aircraft |
| Sep 15, 2016 | US CPSC formally recalls 1M devices |
| Oct 5, 2016 | Replacement device catches fire on aircraft |
| Oct 11, 2016 | Production discontinued; second recall |

### What Samsung Did Right
1. **Fast initial response** — Global recall within 10 days of first reports (unprecedented speed for smartphone)
2. **CEO apology** — Kwon Oh-hyun publicly apologized within 2 weeks (fast for a large Korean corporation)
3. **FAA-style statement** — Acknowledged facts, expressed concern, described actions
4. **Proactive regulatory engagement** — Worked with CPSC and equivalent bodies globally
5. **Full recall** — Took $5.3B profit hit rather than minimize

### What Samsung Did Wrong
1. **Initial blame-shifting** — First statements implied third-party chargers were the cause (not supported by evidence)
2. **Inconsistent replacement** — Replacement devices also caught fire, destroying credibility of "fixed" narrative
3. **Slow to admit systemic defect** — Insisted problem was limited to "certain battery suppliers" for too long
4. **Underestimated US regulatory response** — CPSC action was faster and more severe than Samsung anticipated
5. **No unified global message** — Regional statements were inconsistent in tone and detail

### FAA Analysis of Samsung's Statements

**Initial statement (Sep 2):**
> "We have received a number of reports... of battery cell issues... We are conducting a thorough inspection... We will find the cause."

**What was wrong:**
- Fact: "number of reports" — vague; they had dozens of confirmed cases
- Attitude: Apologetic but not urgent enough
- Action: "Will find the cause" — future tense; not "have taken action"

### Lessons for Crisis Communications
- Be specific about scope even if the number is alarming
- Replacement programs must be bulletproof before announced
- Unified global message from day one
- US regulatory system (CPSC) moves faster and more publicly than Samsung expected
- Once credibility is lost, recovery takes years

## Case 2: Johnson & Johnson Tylenol Cyanide Crisis (1982) — Gold Standard

### What Happened
Seven people in Chicago died after taking Extra-Strength Tylenol capsules that had been laced with cyanide. Johnson & Johnson faced an existential threat: the company's flagship product was being used as a murder weapon.

### Timeline
| Date | Event |
|------|-------|
| Sep 29, 1982 | First deaths reported in Chicago |
| Sep 30, 1982 | J&J confirms cyanide-laced Tylenol killed 3 |
| Oct 1, 1982 | J&J CEO James Burke makes immediate decision: full national recall |
| Oct 5, 1982 | 31M bottles recalled at $100M cost |
| 1982 | J&J introduces tamper-evident packaging (industry first) |
| 1986 | New caplet formula launched |
| 1991 | Tylenol returns to #1 market share position |

### What J&J Did Right (The Gold Standard)
1. **Immediate CEO leadership** — James Burke was the public face; made the recall decision personally within 48 hours
2. **Consumer safety over profit** — $100M recall of 31M bottles despite no legal requirement
3. **Tamper-evident innovation** — Made the product safer than before, demonstrating commitment
4. **FAA-perfect statements** — Acknowledged deaths, expressed deep concern, described immediate action (full recall)
5. **Cooperation with law enforcement** — FBI was investigating; J&J worked transparently with them
6. **Recovery narrative within weeks** — Announced new safety features before the crisis narrative stabilized

### The Tylenol Lesson
James Burke said: "The first thing you do is get the facts. The second thing is to do what's right. The third is to tell people about what you've done."

This is the original FAA framework — before the acronym existed.

### Recovery Timeline
- 1982: Recall executed in weeks
- 1982: New packaging introduced
- 1986: Caplet formula (solid, not capsule)
- 1991: Market share fully recovered — Tylenol became stronger than before

## Case 3: Toyota Sudden Acceleration (2009-2010) — Costly Delay

### What Happened
Reports of sudden unintended acceleration in Toyota vehicles led to a massive regulatory investigation, congressional hearings, and the largest automotive fine in US history ($1.6B).

### Timeline
| Date | Event |
|------|-------|
| 2009 | Reports of sudden acceleration in Toyota vehicles |
| 2009 | NHTSA begins investigation |
| Jan 2010 | Toyota announces recalls of 2.3M vehicles for floor mat issue |
| Feb 2010 | Additional 1.1M vehicles recalled for sticky accelerator |
| Apr 2010 | Congressional hearings begin; Toyota executives testify |
| Dec 2010 | NHTSA imposes $32.4M fine (maximum at time) |
| Mar 2014 | Toyota agrees to $1.6B settlement (largest auto-related settlement) |

### What Toyota Did Wrong
1. **Defensive posture** — Claimed "no defect found" in early statements; this position lasted 18 months
2. **Blaming drivers** — Implied user error rather than engineering defect
3. **Slow to accept electronic cause** — Focused only on mechanical fixes (floor mats, pedals)
4. **Executive testimony underprepared** — Congressional hearing appearance damaged credibility
5. **Inconsistent statements** — Different messages to regulators vs. media vs. public

### What the Crisis Communications Lesson Shows
The cost of being wrong in the early statement ($1.6B settlement) far exceeded the cost of proactive action. Toyota's initial "no defect found" stance:

- Couldn't be walked back when defect was confirmed
- Created Congressional scrutiny that found additional issues
- Led to criminal investigation
- Dominated news cycles for 18 months

**Correct approach (as Tylenol showed):**
- Acknowledge reports of concern
- Investigate publicly and transparently
- Implement interim safety measures
- Update continuously

## Case 4: Didi Chuxing Passenger Safety Crisis (China, 2018)

### What Happened
Two乘客 (passengers) were murdered by Didi drivers within months of each other. The second incident led to regulatory suspension of Didi's Hitch service across China.

### Timeline
| Date | Event |
|------|-------|
| May 2018 | First passenger murdered by Didi driver (Zhengzhou) |
| Aug 24, 2018 | Second passenger murdered (Zhangzhou) |
| Aug 25, 2018 | Didi issues statement |
| Aug 26, 2018 | Didi suspends Hitch carpool service nationwide |
| Aug 27, 2018 | CEO Cheng Wei issues personal apology |
| Sep 2018 | Didi halts all new user registration |
| 2021 | Didi delisted from US stock exchange |

### What Didi Did Wrong
1. **Ignored first incident** — Did not make meaningful safety changes after May murder
2. **Slow initial response** — 48 hours between second murder and CEO statement
3. **Blaming victims** — Early statements were criticized as victim-blaming
4. **Inadequate internal controls** — Driver vetting, emergency response, ride tracking all insufficient
5. **Regulatory relationship** — CAC and Ministry of Transport had not been adequately engaged pre-crisis

### Chinese Regulatory Context
The Chinese regulatory environment for platform companies intensified dramatically after 2018:
- CAC (Cyberspace Administration of China): Data security, content oversight
- Ministry of Transport: Ride-hailing licensing, driver qualification
- SAMR (now State Market Regulatory Administration): Consumer protection

**Didi's regulatory mistake:** Did not pre-notify regulators before the August 25 statement. The subsequent regulatory action was more severe because regulators felt blindsided.

### What Didi Did Right (After Initial Failure)
1. **CEO personally apologized** — Cheng Wei appeared in video statement
2. **Complete service suspension** — Hitch service suspended nationwide (aggressive but necessary)
3. **Third-party safety committee** — Hired external safety experts
4. **Driver re-vetting** — Re-verified all active drivers
5. **Emergency button** — Added in-app emergency button nationwide

## Case 5: Equifax Data Breach (2017) — Botched Response

### What Happened
Equifax announced a data breach affecting 147 million Americans — names, Social Security numbers, birth dates, addresses. The response was widely criticized as one of the worst corporate crisis responses in history.

### What Equifax Did Wrong
1. **Website looked phishing** — The dedicated breach response site (equifaxsecurity2017.com) looked like a phishing scam
2. **Arbitrary PIN numbers** — Used last 4 digits of phone number as identity verification PIN
3. **Arbitration clause in enrollment** — Signing up for free credit monitoring waived the right to sue
4. **Executives sold stock** — Three senior executives sold $1.8M in stock before the breach was public
5. **Slow public notification** — Breach discovered July; announced September — 6 weeks of silence
6. **CEO testified without remorse** — Congressional testimony was tone-deaf

### FAA Analysis — Equifax Statement

**What was wrong:**
- Fact: Dates were vague; scope was understated initially
- Attitude: Corporate-speak without genuine empathy
- Action: Free credit monitoring as fix — insufficient for SSN exposure (SSN can't be "monitored" away)

**What should have been:**
- Fact: Specific dates, specific data categories exposed
- Attitude: Direct apology; acknowledge severity of SSN exposure
- Action: Specific remediation beyond credit monitoring; no arbitration waiver

## Cross-Case Summary

| Crisis | Speed | Transparency | Recovery | Cost |
|--------|-------|-------------|----------|------|
| Tylenol (1982) | Fast (days) | High | 9 years to #1 | ~$100M recall |
| Toyota (2010) | Slow (18 months) | Low | Ongoing | $1.6B settlement |
| Samsung Note7 (2016) | Fast (weeks) | Medium | ~1 year | $5.3B profit |
| Didi (2018) | Slow (days) | Low | Ongoing (delisted 2021) | Business destroyed |
| Equifax (2017) | Slow (6 weeks) | Low | Not recovered | Regulatory + lawsuits |

**Key finding:** Speed of response correlates with recovery speed, but transparency matters more. Toyota was faster than Didi/Equifax but still paid more because the early statements were wrong.
