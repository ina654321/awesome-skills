# Phil Knight & The Nike Founding Story

> **Source:** "Shoe Dog: A Memoir by the Creator of Nike" by Phil Knight (2016)

---

## The Founder

**Phil Knight**
- Born: February 24, 1938, Portland, Oregon
- Education: University of Oregon (BS Accounting, 1959), Stanford MBA (1962)
- Nike Role: Founder (1964), CEO (1968-2004), Chairman (2004-2016)
- Current: Chairman Emeritus, Philanthropist
- Net Worth: ~$40+ billion

---

## The Origin Story

### The "Crazy Idea"

**1962:** As a Stanford MBA student, Knight wrote a research paper titled:

> "Can Japanese Sports Shoes Do to German Sports Shoes What Japanese Cameras Did to German Cameras?"

The paper argued that Japanese manufacturers could produce high-quality, low-cost athletic shoes that would disrupt the German-dominated (Adidas, Puma) market.

**The Insight:**
- Japanese cameras (Canon, Nikon) were displacing German Leica
- Japanese manufacturing quality was improving rapidly
- American runners lacked affordable, quality footwear

### The World Trip

After Stanford, Knight embarked on a global trip:
1. **Hawaii**: Worked briefly, then continued
2. **Japan**: The crucial stop—met with Onitsuka Tiger executives
3. **Asia, Europe, Africa**: Backpacking, thinking, planning

### The Onitsuka Meeting

**Kobe, Japan, 1962:**

Knight walked into Onitsuka Co. (Tiger shoe manufacturer) without an appointment. He claimed to represent "Blue Ribbon Sports"—a company that didn't exist yet.

**The Pitch:**
- Representing American sporting goods distributors
- Interested in exclusive distribution rights for western U.S.
- Wanted samples to show "retail partners"

**The Result:**
- Onitsuka agreed to exclusive western U.S. distribution
- Knight ordered 300 pairs of Tiger shoes
- Took 18 months to deliver
- Cost: $50 borrowed from his father

## Blue Ribbon Sports: The Early Years

### 1963-1964: The Hustle

**The First Year:**
- Knight sold shoes from the trunk of his 1964 Plymouth Valiant
- Targeted track meets, high school coaches, running clubs
- Grossed $8,000 in revenue
- Worked as an accountant at Price Waterhouse to survive

**The First Office:**
- Parents' basement in Portland
- Cracked windows, freezing in winter
- "Office" was a converted space under the stairs

### Bill Bowerman Enters

**1964:** Knight sent a pair of Tiger shoes to his former track coach at University of Oregon.

**Bill Bowerman:**
- Legendary Oregon track coach
- Obsessed with athlete performance
- Constantly tinkering with shoes, tracks, equipment
- Coached Steve Prefontaine and many Olympians

**The Partnership:**
- Bowerman offered to buy 50% of Blue Ribbon Sports
- Knight accepted immediately
- Bowerman would handle product design and innovation
- Knight would handle sales and business

**Bowerman's Impact:**
- Lent credibility to the enterprise
- Began modifying Tiger shoes for better performance
- The foundation of Nike's innovation culture

### The First Employees

Knight recruited a ragtag team:

**Jeff Johnson:**
- First full-time employee (1965)
- Customer #1 (bought first pair from Knight's car)
- Created the name "Nike" (from a dream about Greek goddess of victory)
- Built the first mail-order system

**Penny Parks:**
- Met as Knight's accounting student at Portland State
- Became bookkeeper, then wife
- Married within a year of meeting

**The "Buttfaces":**
- Knight's term for his early team
- A band of "swoosh-crazed brothers"
- Shared obsession with shoes and athletics

## The Birth of Nike

### Naming the Brand

**1971:** Knight needed a new name (Onitsuka relationship deteriorating)

**Candidates:**
- Knight wanted: "Dimension Six"
- Others wanted: "Falcon," "Bengal"
- Jeff Johnson dreamed of "Nike" (Greek goddess of victory)
- Chosen in desperation as production deadline loomed

**The Swoosh:**
- Designed by Carolyn Davidson, Portland State art student
- Cost: $35 (later given stock worth millions)
- Knight's reaction: "I don't love it, but it'll grow on me"
- Inspired by wings, speed, movement

### The Waffle Sole

**1971:** Bowerman's legendary innovation

**The Story:**
- Breakfast with wife Barbara in Bowerman's kitchen
- Looking at the waffle iron
- Thought: "What if the pattern created better traction?"
- Poured rubber into the waffle iron
- Destroyed the iron but created the future

**The Impact:**
- Revolutionary traction pattern
- Lighter than traditional soles
- Launched with Nike's first shoe: Moon Shoe (1972)
- Named for footprint-like pattern

### The Break with Onitsuka

**1971-1972:**

The relationship with Onitsuka deteriorated:
- Onitsuka wanted to terminate the distribution agreement
- Onitsuka planned to distribute directly in the U.S.
- Lawsuits followed
- Knight had to build manufacturing capability quickly

**The Outcome:**
- Nike (formerly Blue Ribbon) began manufacturing its own shoes
- Japan Nippon Factory became first manufacturing partner
- Bowerman's designs became proprietary
- The break forced Nike's independence

## The Early Struggles

### Constant Cash Crisis

**The Pattern:**
- Double revenue every year
- Run out of cash every year
- Beg banks for credit lines
- Repeat

**Knight's Philosophy:**
> "Any dollar that wasn't nailed down I was plowing directly back into the business."

**Bank Relationships:**
- First National Bank of Oregon: Initial partner
- Multiple banks over the years
- Constant negotiations for letters of credit
- Near-bankruptcy multiple times

### The $25 Million Customs Fine

**1970s:**
- U.S. Customs accused Nike of misclassifying imports
- Assessed $25 million fine (equivalent to ~$150M today)
- Would have bankrupted the company
- Fought for years, settled for much less

### The Lawsuits

**Onitsuka:**
- Breach of contract claims
- Trademark disputes
- Eventually settled

**Competitors:**
- Adidas and Puma constantly threatened litigation
- Nike's growth attracted legal attention
- Knight learned to "lawyer up"

## Growth and Going Public

### Revenue Milestones

| Year | Revenue | Key Event |
|------|---------|-----------|
| 1963 | $8,000 | First year |
| 1969 | $300,000 | Growing but still tiny |
| 1972 | $2M | Nike-branded shoes launch |
| 1976 | $14M | Waffle Trainer success |
| 1980 | $270M | IPO year |
| 1986 | $1B | First billion |
| 1996 | $6.5B | Global dominance |
| 2004 | $12B | Knight steps down as CEO |
| 2015 | $30B | Global #1 |
| 2024 | $51.4B | Peak revenue |

### The IPO

**December 2, 1980:**
- Nike went public
- Stock price: $22 per share
- Raised capital for growth
- Knight and team finally had financial stability
- "16 years of chaos" (Knight's words) began to stabilize

### Michael Jordan

**1984:** The deal that changed everything

**The Context:**
- Jordan was rookie, unproven
- Wanted Adidas, settled for Nike
- Nike projected $3M in Year 4 sales
- Actual Year 1: $126 million

**The Deal:**
- 5 years, $2.5 million
- Signature shoe line
- Royalties on sales
- Created the athlete-brand template

**The Result:**
- Jordan Brand: $7.3B business today
- Michael Jordan earned $300M+ annually from Nike
- Highest-paid athlete of all time

## Knight's Leadership Philosophy

### The Anti-Micromanager

Knight famously avoided micromanagement:
- Hired smart, passionate people
- Got out of their way
- Let them make mistakes
- Trusted the "swoosh-crazed" culture

**Quote:**
> "The art of competing, I learned from track. The art of business, I learned as I went. But the art of leadership, I learned from not leading—by letting others lead."

### The Chaos Embrace

Knight never sought stability:
- Constant growth over profit
- Reinvestment over dividends
- Risk over caution
- "The messy, perilous, chaotic journey"

**Quote:**
> "The path to business success is messy, perilous, and chaotic, riddled with mistakes, endless struggles, and sacrifice."

### The Competitiveness

Knight's drive came from:
- Track competition (ran for Oregon)
- Hatred of losing
- Fear of being ordinary
- Obsession with growth

**Quote:**
> "Life is growth. You grow or you die."

## Shoe Dog: The Memoir

### Publication

**2016:** "Shoe Dog: A Memoir by the Creator of Nike"

**Reception:**
- New York Times Bestseller
- Bill Gates' favorite book of 2016
- Praised for candor and storytelling
- Rare insight from notoriously private founder

### Key Themes

**1. The Power of Crazy Ideas**
- Knight's Japanese shoe thesis seemed absurd
- Persistence over conventional wisdom
- Vision over immediate validation

**2. The Importance of Teams**
- "Swoosh-crazed brothers" made Nike
- Bowerman, Johnson, Strasser, et al.
- Shared obsession > individual genius

**3. The Reality of Entrepreneurship**
- Not glamorous
- Constant fear and stress
- Near-death experiences (financially)
- Worth it for the impact

**4. Sports as Transformative Force**
- Running changed Knight's life
- Shoes enable human potential
- "If you have a body, you are an athlete"

### Notable Quotes from Shoe Dog

On starting:
> "I wanted to build something all my own, something new, dynamic, different."

On fear:
> "I was afraid of failure, sure. But I was more afraid of never trying."

On the team:
> "We were a band of swoosh-crazed brothers, misfits and savants who believed in something."

On competition:
> "The cowards never started, and the weak died along the way. That leaves us."

On success:
> "The end result wasn't the point. The journey was the point. The struggle was the point."

## Post-Nike Philanthropy

### Phil and Penny Knight

After stepping down as CEO (2004) and Chairman (2016), Knight focused on philanthropy:

**Major Donations:**
- **University of Oregon**: $1B+ (largest donor in history)
- **Stanford University**: $400M+ (Knight-Hennessy Scholars)
- **Oregon Health & Science University**: $500M+ (cancer research)

**Total Giving:**
- Estimated $2+ billion
- Among largest donors in U.S. history

### Legacy

**Nike:**
- World's largest athletic brand
- $46+ billion annual revenue
- 83,000+ employees
- Global cultural icon

**Beyond Business:**
- Changed how athletes are marketed
- Created the athlete-brand partnership model
- Democratized athletic performance
- "Just Do It" as cultural ethos

## The Nike DNA

Knight's influence persists in Nike's culture:

**The Swoosh-Crazed Ethos:**
- Obsession with product and athletes
- Innovation over imitation
- Growth over stability
- "Just Do It" fearlessness

**The Chaotic Journey:**
- Nike's current "Win Now" turnaround embodies Knight's willingness to change course
- Elliott Hill's wholesale focus mirrors Knight's retail hustle
- Product innovation remains the core

**The Quote That Defines Nike:**
> "If you have a body, you are an athlete."
> — Bill Bowerman (adopted as Nike's ethos)

## Key Takeaways

1. **Start with a crazy idea** — Knight's Japanese shoe thesis seemed absurd but proved visionary

2. **Find great partners** — Bowerman, Johnson, and the "buttfaces" made Nike possible

3. **Embrace chaos** — The road to success is messy; accept the struggle

4. **Bet on yourself** — Knight plowed every dollar back into growth

5. **Hire people who care** — The "swoosh-crazed" culture outperforms mercenaries

6. **Never stop growing** — "You grow or you die"

7. **Leave a legacy** — From $50 borrowed to $40B+ donated
