# Scenario Examples

## 9.1 Common Scenario: Buyer's Agent Compensation After NAR Settlement

**User:** A buyer calls and wants to see homes but says they won't sign a buyer representation agreement and expect me to be paid by the seller. What do I do?

**Expert:**
> **Analysis:**
> Post-August 2024 NAR settlement rules require:
>
> **Required steps:**
> 1. **Do not show homes without a signed agreement** in most states now — this protects the agent and ensures compensation clarity
> 2. **Explain the agreement:** It protects the buyer by ensuring you represent their interests exclusively
> 3. **Negotiate compensation transparently:** It can be hourly, flat fee, or percentage — but must be agreed upon before service
>
> **Compensation options to offer:**
> 1. **Buyer-conceded in offer:** The buyer's offer to the seller can include agent compensation as a seller concession — this is the most common post-settlement structure
> 2. **Direct buyer payment:** Buyer pays you directly from their own funds
> 3. **Hybrid:** Part from seller concession, part from buyer
>
> **What to say to the buyer:**
> "I am required by law to have a signed agreement before providing buyer representation services. This agreement outlines what I do for you and how I am compensated. We can structure compensation so the seller pays my fee as part of your offer — most buyers do this. Would you like me to explain the agreement terms?"
>
> **Important:** You cannot require the buyer to pay you out of pocket if seller concession is standard in your market. Compensation must be negotiated in good faith.

## 9.2 Complex Scenario: Client Wants to Discriminate Against Section 8 Tenants

**User:** A landlord client says he doesn't want to accept Section 8 vouchers. Can I list his property this way? One of my buyer clients has a voucher.

**Expert:**
> **Comprehensive Solution:**
> This is a Fair Housing violation waiting to happen. Here's the analysis:
>
> **Fair Housing Act analysis:**
> Source of income is protected in some states and municipalities (not federally). However, blanket refusal of Section 8 creates disparate impact liability:
> - Section 8 holders are disproportionately minority, disabled, or elderly
> - Blanket exclusion = disparate impact discrimination
> - HUD has pursued cases against landlords who refuse Section 8
>
> **What you should do:**
> 1. **Do NOT list the property as "no Section 8"** — this exposes you and the seller to liability
> 2. **Inform the seller of fair housing risks** — document the conversation
> 3. **Offer alternatives:** If the landlord has legitimate concerns (payment timing, inspection requirements), help them understand how Section 8 works
> 4. **If seller insists on refusing:** Document refusal; recommend they consult a fair housing attorney
>
> **For your buyer client with a voucher:**
> 1. Continue providing full service — do not treat them differently
> 2. Find properties that accept Section 8 (many do)
> 3. Do not disclose the voucher status unless necessary
>
> **NAR Code of Ethics Article 10:** REALTORS must not deny equal professional services based on protected class.

## 9.3 Edge Case: Agent Discovers Active meth lab in Listed Property

**User:** During a listing appointment, you smell chemical odors and see suspicious stains. You suspect a former meth lab. What do you do?

**Expert:**
> **Special Handling:**
>
> Meth lab disclosure is a critical safety and legal issue.
>
> **Immediate steps:**
> 1. **Do NOT enter the property alone** — methamphetamine production involves toxic chemicals
> 2. **Document observations:** Take photos (from outside), note dates
> 3. **Disclose to seller:** Must disclose material facts — meth contamination is definitely material
> 4. **Recommend professional testing:** Meth screen before any buyer visits
> 5. **Consult state law:** Many states require specific meth lab disclosure forms
>
> **Disclosure obligations:**
> - **Seller's duty:** Sellers must disclose known meth lab history in most states
> - **Agent's duty:** If the agent becomes aware of undisclosed meth contamination, they must disclose to prospective buyers
> - **Failure to disclose:** Both seller and agent can face liability for non-disclosure
>
> **What buyers need to know:**
> - Remediation costs: $5,000–$50,000 depending on severity
> - Testing: methamphetamine residue testing at < 0.1 µg/100cm² (CDPHE standard)
> - Clearance required before occupancy
>
> **Recommended seller actions:**
> 1. Hire certified meth remediation company for testing
> 2. Remediate if positive
> 3. Provide buyer with full remediation report
> 4. Adjust asking price if contamination found and remediated
>
> **If contamination is active/unsafe:** Do not show the property until cleared by certified professionals.
