# Scenario Examples

## 9.1 Common Scenario: Tenant Requests Reasonable Accommodation for Emotional Support Animal

**User:** A prospective tenant has no pets but wants to move in with an emotional support dog. The animal has no specific training. The lease has a "no pets" policy. What do we do?

**Expert:**
> **Analysis:**
> Under the Fair Housing Act, emotional support animals (ESAs) are not considered "pets" — they are accommodations for persons with disabilities.
>
> **Required response:**
> 1. **Do not automatically deny** — ESA requests must be evaluated through the reasonable accommodation process
> 2. **Request documentation:** Letter from a licensed mental health professional (not internet certification) stating the tenant has a disability and the ESA is necessary
> 3. **Evaluate the request:** Is the disability-documented? Is the ESA directly related to the disability?
> 4. **Do not ask for detailed medical records** — just the verification letter
> 5. **Cannot charge pet rent or deposit** for an ESA (federal law)
>
> **When to deny:**
> - The animal poses a direct threat (unprovoked aggression, documented attacks)
> - The animal is not commonly accepted as a domestic animal
> - The accommodation would cause fundamental alteration to the property
>
> **Required process:**
> 1. Acknowledge the request in writing within 10 days
> 2. Request the documentation within 14 days
> 3. Respond with approval or denial within 30 days of receiving documentation
> 4. If denying, provide written explanation and offer to engage in interactive process
>
> **Important:** Breed restrictions and weight limits generally do NOT apply to ESAs.

## 9.2 Complex Scenario: Security Deposit Dispute at Move-Out

**User:** Tenant moved out; deposit is $1,500. We have $800 in damages beyond normal wear. Tenant disputes $600 of the charges. What is the correct process?

**Expert:**
> **Comprehensive Solution:**
> Most states have strict timelines and procedural requirements for security deposits:
>
> **Step-by-step process:**
> 1. **Move-out inspection:** Complete within 3 days of move-out (varies by state)
> 2. **Itemized statement:** List all deductions with estimated cost or contractor invoice
> 3. **Send within deadline:** Most states require 14–30 days to return deposit
> 4. **Send to last known address:** Use certified mail with return receipt
>
> **Typical deductible items:**
> - Beyond normal wear: carpet stains, wall holes, appliance damage
> - Unpaid rent (if any)
> - Early termination fees (if lease allows)
> - Cleaning costs (if unit left dirtier than move-in condition)
>
> **Non-deductible items:**
> - Normal wear and tear: faded paint, worn carpet in high-traffic areas, minor scuffs
> - Ordinary cleaning: normal post-tenant cleaning
> - Pre-existing damage (documented at move-in)
>
> **If tenant disputes:**
> 1. Respond in writing with itemized receipts and photos
> 2. If unresolved, tenant may sue in small claims court (typically < $5,000–$10,000)
> 3. If PM holds deposit on owner's behalf, the PM can be sued directly
> 4. Landlord/PM can be liable for 2–3× deposit + attorney's fees if wrongfully withheld
>
> **Prevention:** Always do thorough move-in documentation with photos and video.

## 9.3 Edge Case: HOA Fines Property Manager for Violation — Who Pays?

**User:** The HOA fined our management company $500 for a lease violation (unapproved exterior modification by tenant). The owner says it's our fault. Who is responsible?

**Expert:**
> **Special Handling:**
>
> This depends on the management agreement and the nature of the violation:
>
> **Analysis framework:**
> 1. **Scope of management services:** Did the PM have authority to approve/disapprove exterior modifications?
> 2. **Lease review:** Did the PM include HOA compliance clause in the lease?
> 3. **Tenant approval process:** Did PM approve the modification without HOA consent?
> 4. **Management agreement indemnification:** Does the agreement specify responsibility for HOA violations?
>
> **Likely responsibility allocation:**
> - **PM responsible:** If PM approved modification without verifying HOA approval
> - **Owner responsible:** If modification was by tenant choice, not PM action
> - **Tenant responsible:** If tenant made modification without any authorization
>
> **Resolution steps:**
> 1. Review management agreement for indemnification and responsibility clauses
> 2. Determine whether PM or owner signed the HOA acknowledgment
> 3. In most cases: owner is ultimately responsible to HOA for property compliance
> 4. PM should reimburse owner only if PM negligence caused the violation
>
> **Prevention:** Management agreements should explicitly state:
> - PM is responsible for lease compliance review
> - Tenant exterior modifications require owner + HOA approval
> - PM shall not approve modifications without owner authorization
> - Indemnification clause for HOA violations caused by PM negligence
