# Scenario Examples

## 9.1 Common Scenario: Builder Wants to Charge 50% Markup on Upgrades

**User:** The builder is charging $8,000 for an upgrade package that costs them $5,000. The builder says that's the standard markup. How do I advise my buyer client?

**Expert:**
> **Analysis:**
> Builder upgrade markups typically range from 20–50%. Here is how to evaluate:
>
> **Cost analysis approach:**
> 1. Request itemized cost breakdown from builder — they are required to provide this in many states
> 2. Compare builder prices to retail: e.g., flooring at $8/sqft builder vs. $4/sqft retail = 100% markup
> 3. Negotiate from builder cost + 15–20% service fee rather than accepting full markup
>
> **Negotiation strategy:**
> 1. Offer to pay cash for upgrades (no lender involvement) — builders often discount for cash
> 2. Bundle upgrades to negotiate volume discount
> 3. Request base price reduction equivalent to builder cost on priority items
> 4. Ask for closing cost credits equivalent to builder markup on must-have upgrades
>
> **Key tip:** Energy-efficient upgrades (high-SEER HVAC, better insulation) often qualify for lender credits or lower utility costs that offset markup. Some builders offer "design center allowance" as an alternative.
>
> **Bottom line:** If the $8,000 upgrade saves $2,000/yr in utilities over the 30-year mortgage, it's worth it. If it's cosmetic (upgraded faucet handles), negotiate hard.

## 9.2 Complex Scenario: HOA Has Underfunded Reserves and Special Assessment Looming

**User:** During HOA document review, I see the HOA reserves are at 23% funded and a major roof replacement is due in 3 years. What should I advise my buyer?

**Expert:**
> **Comprehensive Solution:**
> A reserve fund below 70% funded (per ASTM E-2279 standard) is a red flag. Here's the analysis:
>
> **Risk assessment:**
> 1. Calculate estimated special assessment: roof replacement × number of units ÷ number of owners
> 2. Review HOA meeting minutes: any mention of special assessment discussions?
> 3. Check HOA management company recommendation letter
> 4. Evaluate HOA board composition: are owners or professional management?
>
> **Disclosure requirements:**
> - Most states require HOA disclosure of pending special assessments
> - California Civil Code 5300 requires full financial disclosure before sale
> - Review state-specific HOA seller disclosure laws
>
> **Options for buyer:**
> 1. Walk away: reserve-funded ratio below 30% is high risk
> 2. Negotiate price reduction: 5–15% discount to cover anticipated special assessment
> 3. Require HOA to complete reserve study and fund appropriately before closing
> 4. Request seller credit at closing for buyer's share of anticipated assessment
> 5. Make purchase contingent on HOA adopting a properly-funded reserve plan
>
> **Critical:** The HOA's right to assess special levies is typically unlimited — even a small per-unit assessment of $5,000 can impact the buyer's finances significantly.

## 9.3 Edge Case: Builder Misses Closing Date by 6 Months

**User:** The builder has delayed closing by 6 months due to supply chain issues. The buyer has already sold their current home and is renting. What can we do?

**Expert:**
> **Special Handling:**
>
> Extended delays can expose the buyer to significant financial and legal risk.
>
> **Immediate actions:**
> 1. Review purchase agreement for delay clauses: most builders have "force majeure" and "time is not of the essence" language
> 2. Send written notice of delay to builder — document the delay with timestamps
> 3. Identify contract remedies: liquidated damages, rent credits, or termination right
>
> **Financial protection:**
> 1. Request rent credits from builder for every month beyond the contract date
> 2. Request price lock guarantee — lumber/material costs may decline
> 3. Verify interest rate lock extension (work with lender to extend)
> 4. Consider temporary housing arrangements — negotiate builder-paid temporary housing
>
> **Contract renegotiation opportunity:**
> 1. If market has shifted: request price reduction
> 2. Request additional upgrades as compensation for delay
> 3. Request extended warranty period as remedy
>
> **Termination right:** If contract contains a "time is of the essence" clause and the builder has exceeded the deadline by more than the contractual grace period (typically 30–90 days), the buyer may have grounds to terminate and reclaim earnest money. Consult real estate attorney.
>
> **Prevention for future:** Always negotiate a delay penalty clause (e.g., $100/day credit after 30-day grace period) into new construction contracts.
