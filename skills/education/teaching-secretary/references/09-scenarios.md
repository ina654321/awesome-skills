# Scenario Examples

## 9.1 Common Scenario: Angry Parent Demanding to See Their Child's Records

**User:** "A parent called demanding to see their child's full academic file immediately. They were rude and said we have no right to withhold it. What do I say?"

**Expert:**
> **FERPA Compliance Response:**
>
> 1. **Stay calm**: Don't match the emotional tone. Professionalism is your shield.
> 2. **Acknowledge their right**: "You're absolutely entitled to your child's educational records. FERPA protects that right."
> 3. **Explain the process**: "Here's how you can request them: you can submit a written request to the school office. We have 45 days to fulfill the request, though we typically process them much faster."
> 4. **Note the parent's behavior**: Document the call — date, time, what was said. This protects you if the situation escalates.
> 5. **Offer immediate help**: "Would you like me to provide the request form right now? Or would you prefer to submit it in writing and we can have the records ready in [X] days?"
> 6. **Clarify what "full file" means**: Some parents think "full file" includes teacher personal notes. "The file includes grades, attendance, and disciplinary records. Certain documents like teacher personal notes and recommendation letters are not part of the educational record."
> 7. **Escalate if needed**: If the parent continues to be aggressive, politely state: "I'm happy to help you, but I need you to speak respectfully. Let me transfer you to [administrator] if you'd like to discuss this further."
> 8. **Do not argue**: You are not the decision-maker here — FERPA sets the rules. Be a helpful guide through the process.

## 9.2 Complex Scenario: Unauthorized Person Claims to Be Authorized Pickup

**User:** "A person arrived to pick up a student. They're listed as a contact but the parent did NOT indicate they are an authorized pickup. They have no legal documentation. What do I do?"

**Expert:**
> **Student Safety Protocol:**
>
> 1. **Do not release the student**: Without explicit written parental authorization, you cannot release a student to someone not on the approved list.
> 2. **Explain the policy calmly**: "I'm sorry, but for student safety, we can only release students to people specifically authorized by the parent. Was this arranged in writing?"
> 3. **Call the parent**: Contact the parent immediately to verify. "Someone is here claiming to pick up [student]. Are they authorized to do so?" Document the parent's response.
> 4. **If parent says yes**: Get written or verbal authorization (document verbal clearly). Release student but update the authorized pickup list for future reference.
> 5. **If parent says no or can't be reached**: The student stays at school. "I'm not able to release [student] without your authorization. Your child will wait in the office until we can reach you."
> 6. **Do not give reasons or details**: Don't explain why the person can't pick up (e.g., "the parent said not to let this person"). Simply: "I'm not authorized to release the student."
> 7. **If the person becomes aggressive**: Activate silent alarm or alert administrator. Do not physically block — prioritize your safety.
> 8. **Document everything**: Record the person's name, description, time, what was said, and parent's response.

## 9.3 Edge Case: Student Arrives High or Intoxicated

**User:** "A student came to the front office looking disoriented and smelling of alcohol. What do I do?"

**Expert:**
> **Student Welfare Emergency Protocol:**
>
> 1. **Don't send them to class**: A student who may be under the influence should not be unsupervised. Keep them in the office.
> 2. **Notify the school nurse immediately**: Have the nurse assess the student's condition. Do not attempt to assess medical status yourself.
> 3. **Notify administrator**: Report immediately to principal or assistant principal.
> 4. **Document observations**: Write down exactly what you observed — time, physical appearance, statements made. Use exact quotes if possible.
> 5. **Do not confront or accuse**: "You seem unwell. Please sit here until the nurse comes." Do not say "are you drinking?"
> 6. **Parent notification**: Once administrator confirms concern, parent must be contacted. This is a welfare and safety issue.
> 7. **Possible next steps**: Student may need to be sent home with parent; administrator handles discipline per school policy; counselor referral; drug/alcohol policy applies.
> 8. **Self-care**: Such situations are stressful. Speak with your supervisor or use employee support resources afterward.
> 9. **Know your limits**: You are not a medical professional or counselor. Your role is to observe, document, and escalate appropriately.
