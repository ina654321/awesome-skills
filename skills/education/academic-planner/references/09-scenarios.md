# Scenario Examples

## 9.1 Student Has Two Required Courses at the Same Time

**User:** "A junior needs both AP English and AP History for college applications, but both are scheduled at the same period. The student has a strong GPA and we're at max capacity in the lower-period sections."

**Expert:**
> **Resolution Path:**
>
> 1. **Examine capacity**: Are both courses truly maxed? Check if any sections have 1-2 open seats that could accommodate a section transfer
> 2. **Alternate period options**: Can any of the two courses be moved to an open period for this student? (Check other grade-level sections)
> 3. **Distance learning option**: Many districts have agreements for online course completion for conflicting AP courses
> 4. **Summer school**: Can one course be completed in summer school and pre-credited?
> 5. **Course request priority**: Check when requests were submitted — did the student request early enough?
> 6. **Counselor-level solution**: If truly irreconcilable, the counselor may need to adjust another course on the student's schedule
> 7. **Document the decision**: Whatever the resolution, document it and the rationale
>
> **Key consideration**: AP courses are often weighted differently — check district policy on course conflicts with weighted classes.

## 9.2 Low-Enrollment Course Facing Cancellation

**User:** "A popular elective has only 12 students enrolled, below the 15-student minimum threshold. The teacher is beloved and cancellation would lose the program."

**Expert:**
> **Decision Framework:**
>
> 1. **Review minimum enrollment policy**: What does the district contract say about section minimums? Is there a waiver process?
> 2. **Cost-benefit analysis**: Calculate cost-per-student vs. educational value and program sustainability
> 3. **Alternative delivery models**: Could the class run with 12 if the period length changes? Could it be combined with another section?
> 4. **Recruitment window**: Is there time to recruit additional students before the decision deadline?
> 5. **Multi-year program impact**: Will cancelling this year affect future enrollment (word spreads)?
> 6. **Teacher communication**: Discuss with the teacher and explore options together
> 7. **Escalation if needed**: Bring data to administration with a recommendation — continue with waiver or cancel
>
> **Document everything**: Every conversation, decision, and rationale must be documented.

## 9.3 Schedule Change Request on Day 10 of Semester

**User:** "A parent is demanding their child's schedule be changed because the teacher 'doesn't teach well.' It's already day 10 of the semester."

**Expert:**
> **Protocol:**
>
> 1. **Acknowledge and listen**: Validate the parent's concern; schedule a meeting to discuss specifics
> 2. **Gather data**: Review gradebook, teacher observation notes, and student performance to date
> 3. **Clarify timeline**: Explain the schedule change window has passed; changes at this point disrupt learning
> 4. **Explore alternatives**: If there are legitimate issues, can tutoring, conference with teacher, or peer support help?
> 5. **Intervention process**: If teaching quality is genuinely a concern, route through the proper administrative channels
> 6. **Formal complaint pathway**: Provide the parent with the district-level complaint procedure if they are unsatisfied
> 7. **Do not make promises**: Do not commit to schedule changes that are outside policy
>
> **Key consideration**: Schedule changes after the add/drop period should be rare and require administrative approval.
