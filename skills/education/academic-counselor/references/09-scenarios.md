# Scenario Examples

## 9.1 Student Struggling with Test Anxiety

**User:** "A high-achieving junior consistently performs 15-20% lower on standardized tests than their class grades suggest. Their parents are pushing for accommodations, but the student doesn't want to be 'labeled.'"

**Expert:**
> **Assessment:**
>
> 1. **Distinguish anxiety from LD**: Arrange psychoeducational evaluation to rule out learning disabilities; anxiety alone doesn't automatically qualify for accommodations but can be documented under Section 504
> 2. **Evidence building**: Collect test scores across multiple administrations; document discrepancy between class performance and standardized tests
> 3. **Student-centered conversation**: Validate their concern about labeling; frame accommodations as tools, not labels; explain that colleges do not see 504 plans
> 4. **Skill building**: Teach test-anxiety management techniques — deep breathing, progressive muscle relaxation, guided visualization
> 5. **Practice testing**: Simulate testing conditions to build familiarity and reduce novelty-anxiety
> 6. **Parent communication**: Help parents understand the process and timelines; manage expectations
>
> **Key consideration**: Document all evidence and conversations. An accommodations request must be supported by qualifying documentation reviewed by the 504/IEP team.

## 9.2 First-Generation College Student

**User:** "A first-generation student wants to apply to a prestigious university but their parents are skeptical about college and won't help with applications. The student is talented but lacks guidance on the process."

**Expert:**
> **Intervention:**
>
> 1. **Validate the student**: Acknowledge the unique challenge of navigating systems their family hasn't encountered
> 2. **Identify adult allies**: Connect with teachers, mentors, coaches who can write recommendation letters
> 3. **Financial aid education**: Demystify FAFSA, CSS Profile, merit vs. need-based aid; address the "cost" misconception
> 4. **Application fee waivers**: SAT/ACT fee waivers available via College Board; application fee waivers via individual colleges
> 5. **Pipeline programs**: Connect with pre-college summer programs, AVID, TRIO, GEAR UP
> 6. **Guidance on family dynamics**: Coach student on how to explain their choices to parents without alienating them
> 7. **Realistic match**: Balance aspiration with match; apply to a range of schools including in-state and community college options
>
> **Key resource**: The National College Attainment Network (ncan.org) has first-gen specific toolkits.

## 9.3 Student Considering Dropping Out

**User:** "A 10th-grader says they want to drop out because the material is 'too hard' and they don't see the point. Their grades have dropped from A/B to D/F this semester."

**Expert:**
> **Immediate Response:**
>
> 1. **Safety first**: Check for disengagement signals — withdrawal from friends, talk of hopelessness; connect with school psychologist if any concerning signs
> 2. **Root cause exploration**: Schedule individual session; dig beneath the "too hard" — Is it academic frustration? Social problems? Mental health? Home situation?
> 3. **Re-engagement strategies**: Identify a subject or teacher they connect with; explore alternative pathways (CTE tracks, credit recovery, online options)
> 4. **Motivational interviewing**: Use open questions, reflections, affirmations, summaries to help student articulate their own goals
> 5. **Parent conference**: Bring in support, not punishment; align home and school messaging
> 6. **Short-term plan**: Reduce course load if needed; focus on core subjects; connect with tutoring
> 7. **Long-term vision**: Help student see how education connects to their stated goals, even if the goal changes
>
> **Document everything**: Document the session, the plan, and all follow-up communications per school policy.
