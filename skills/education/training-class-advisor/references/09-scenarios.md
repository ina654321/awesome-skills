# Scenario Examples

## 9.1 Common Scenario: Student Who Wants to Drop a Class Last Minute

**User:** "A student wants to drop a class 2 weeks before finals. They say they're failing and it's 'ruining their GPA.' What do I do?"

**Expert:**
> **Drop/Withdrawal Advising:**
>
> 1. **Understand the timeline**: Most schools have a deadline for "W" (withdrawal) grade — if past that, withdrawal may not be possible. Check the academic calendar.
> 2. **Assess the actual impact**: Calculate what grade is needed to pass. Sometimes the situation is less dire than the student thinks.
> 3. **Explore alternatives before dropping**: Is there extra credit? Can the grade be replaced by a retake? What's the financial aid impact of dropping?
> 4. **Discuss the consequences**: Dropping affects: (a) financial aid Satisfactory Academic Progress (SAP), (b) tuition (may not be refundable), (c) graduation timeline, (d) NCAA eligibility if athlete
> 5. **If drop is warranted**: Submit the drop form; help student register for the course in a future term if needed
> 6. **Don't judge**: Some students genuinely need to drop. Your role is to ensure they understand consequences, not to discourage or encourage.
> 7. **Documentation**: Note the advising conversation; if student is on academic probation, this drop counts toward their GPA calculation even with a W.
> 8. **Follow-up**: If they drop, schedule a check-in for next semester to prevent recurrence.

## 9.2 Complex Scenario: First-Generation Student Overwhelmed by Campus Resources

**User:** "A first-generation college student is confused and overwhelmed. They don't know what advising, tutoring, or career services even mean. How do I help them navigate?"

**Expert:**
> **First-Generation Student Navigation Support:**
>
> 1. **Validate their experience**: "You're doing great by showing up. This campus has a lot of support — let me help you understand what each one does."
> 2. **Translate the system**: Explain each office in concrete terms:
>    - "Academic advising = I help you pick classes and stay on track to graduate"
>    - "Tutoring = Free peer help when you're stuck on homework"
>    - "Career services = Help you find internships and jobs"
> 3. **Accompany them if possible**: Walk them to their first tutoring session or career services appointment. Familiarity reduces anxiety.
> 4. **Create a visual map**: Provide a one-page guide with office locations, hours, and a one-sentence description. Email it to them and their parent (with student's permission).
> 5. **Peer mentorship referral**: If the school has a first-gen student support program, connect them immediately.
> 6. **Parent education**: With student's permission, educate parents so they can support (not direct) the student's journey.
> 7. **Simplify next steps**: Give them ONE thing to do this week — not five. Master one resource before adding the next.
> 8. **Check in weekly initially**: First-gen students benefit from more frequent touchpoints in their first semester.

## 9.3 Edge Case: Student Caught Plagiarizing — Now What?

**User:** "A student came to me crying after being caught plagiarizing. They said they didn't know it was wrong because 'everyone does it' and their parents are 'going to kill them.' How do I respond?"

**Expert:**
> **Post-Academic Misconduct Advising:**
>
> 1. **Listen first**: Let them express their emotions. This is a distressing moment for a student. Validate the feeling without validating the behavior.
> 2. **Explain the gravity clearly**: "Plagiarism is a serious academic integrity violation. I need to explain what happens next, but first I want to make sure you understand why this happened."
> 3. **Address the "everyone does it" myth**: "Many students underestimate what plagiarism is. But intentional or not, the consequences are the same. The academic penalty is real."
> 4. **Explain the process**: "The professor has reported this. There will be an academic integrity hearing. You'll receive notice of the date. You have the right to present your side."
> 5. **Encourage honesty at the hearing**: "The best thing you can do is take responsibility and explain what you didn't understand. Committees respond better to honesty than excuses."
> 6. **Address family concerns**: "Your parents will likely be contacted by the school. I can't control what they say to you, but I can help you think through how to explain it."
> 7. **Academic support**: Connect with writing center, citation workshops, and tutoring — to prevent recurrence.
> 8. **Mental health referral**: This is traumatic. Suggest counseling services.
> 9. **Long-term impact**: If this is a first offense and student takes responsibility, most schools apply educational sanctions (plagiarism workshop, rewriting assignment) rather than expulsion.
