# Scenario Examples

## 9.1 Student's Bread Dough Didn't Rise

**User:** "A student's sourdough levain was active and bubbles formed, but after 3 hours the dough barely increased in volume. The student followed the recipe exactly. What went wrong?"

**Expert:**
> **Troubleshooting Analysis:**
>
> 1. **Temperature check**: Was the dough temperature measured? Ideal fermentation is 75-80°F. Cold dough stalls fermentation
> 2. **Yeast viability**: Was the commercial yeast within date and properly bloomed? (For sourdough: was the levain ripe and active?)
> 3. **Hydration ratio**: Was all the water absorbed? Dry flour in the mixing bowl can make it seem like the recipe was followed
> 4. **Salt interference**: Did the student add salt directly to yeast without dissolving? Salt can kill yeast if direct contact
> 5. **Ambient conditions**: Was the proofing area warm enough? Cold kitchens in winter can stall proofing
> 6. **Remediation**: If time allows, punch down, reshape, and proof again in a warmer area; or use a warm water bath
> 7. **Teaching moment**: This is a perfect learning opportunity — have the student troubleshoot with you, not just be told the answer
>
> **Key consideration**: Fermentation is science. Walk through the chemistry every time something goes wrong.

## 9.2 Student with Celiac Disease in Baking Class

**User:** "A student with celiac disease wants to participate in baking class, but most recipes use wheat flour. How should we handle cross-contact and instruction?"

**Expert:**
> **Inclusive Approach:**
>
> 1. **Health and safety first**: Celiac disease requires strict gluten-free protocols. This is a medical accommodation issue
> 2. **Designate a GF prep station**: Separate mise en place, cutting boards, utensils, and storage
> 3. **Adapt recipes**: Use validated gluten-free flour blends (not just rice flour — those don't work structurally). King Arthur's GF blend is a reliable starting point
> 4. **Xanthan gum addition**: GF baked goods need xanthan or guar gum for structure — add 1/2 tsp per cup of GF flour
> 5. **Cross-contact protocol**: Glove changes, separate towel use, cleaning surfaces with soap and water (not just sanitizer)
> 6. **Learning equity**: The student should learn the same techniques — laminated doughs, enriched breads — adapted for GF
> 7. **Communicate with parents/healthcare provider**: Ensure the school has the student's allergy action plan on file
>
> **Key consideration**: This is an opportunity to teach universal cross-contact precautions that benefit all students.

## 9.3 Pantry Item Running Low Mid-Demonstration

**User:** "During a live chocolate tempering demonstration, the instructor realizes they don't have enough quality dark chocolate for all students to practice. Only 3 of 10 students can participate."

**Expert:**
> **Crisis Response:**
>
> 1. **Pivot to observation**: Have the remaining students observe closely and take detailed notes; rotate them through the practice station in groups of 3
> 2. **Supplemental practice**: Provide modeling chocolate or compound coating as a secondary practice option — it demonstrates the hand movements without requiring tempered chocolate
> 3. **Lab adjustment**: If the school has a kitchen store nearby, send a TA to purchase additional chocolate (advance planning should prevent this)
> 4. **Alternative technique**: Seed method vs. tabling method — if only one method was planned, having students try both extends practice time
> 5. **Future planning**: Always have 20% more expensive ingredients than needed; maintain a supply closet with backup items
> 6. **Make it a learning moment**: Discuss ingredient inventory management as a professional baker's responsibility
>
> **Key consideration**: Every class should have backup plans for ingredient shortages.
