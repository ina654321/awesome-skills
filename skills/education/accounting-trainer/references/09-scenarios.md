# Scenario Examples

## 9.1 Student Struggling with Debit/Credit Framework

**User:** "A student keeps reversing debits and credits. They understand the accounting equation (Assets = Liabilities + Equity) but consistently post entries backwards on exams."

**Expert:**
> **Intervention:**
>
> 1. **Diagnose the root cause**: Is it a memorization issue, a conceptual misunderstanding, or an application problem?
> 2. **T-account scaffolding**: Use T-accounts visually — have students draw accounts and physically trace the flow
> 3. **Rule reinforcement**: Drill the fundamental rules: Assets/Expenses increase with debits; Liabilities/Equity/Revenue increase with credits
> 4. **Mnemonic tools**: Use "DEAL/CLER" (Debits increase Expenses, Assets, Losses; Credits increase Expenses, Assets, Losses reversed) or similar
> 5. **Worked example comparison**: Show correctly posted entries side-by-side with incorrect ones; have student identify errors
> 6. **Increase practice volume**: Assign daily T-account exercises at the start of every class until mastery
> 7. **Alternative notation**: Introduce the accounting equation format (A - L = E) to cross-validate entries
>
> **Key consideration**: Debit/credit mastery is foundational — do not move to adjusting entries until this is solid.

## 9.2 Preparing a Capstone Project for Non-Accounting Majors

**User:** "The CEO of a small business in your community wants to bring their team for a 2-day accounting basics workshop. They sell products and are confused about inventory accounting."

**Expert:**
> **Workshop Design:**
>
> 1. **Needs assessment**: Interview the CEO to understand their business model, current accounting system, and pain points
> 2. **Scope the workshop**: Day 1 — financial statements basics, reading key numbers; Day 2 — inventory methods (FIFO, LIFO, weighted average), cost allocation
> 3. **Use their numbers**: Build all examples using anonymized versions of their actual transactions
> 4. **Practical outputs**: By end of Day 1, they can read their P&L and balance sheet. By end of Day 2, they understand how inventory method choice affects their tax bill
> 5. **Software integration**: If they use QuickBooks or Xero, connect the concepts to their actual software
> 6. **Take-home materials**: One-page reference guides for each topic covered
>
> **Key consideration**: Non-accounting audiences need concepts connected to business decisions, not journal entry mechanics.

## 9.3 Student Caught Cheating on Financial Statement Analysis Exam

**User:** "A student submitted a financial analysis that contained copied paragraphs from an online source without attribution. The student claims they didn't know it was plagiarism."

**Expert:**
> **Protocol:**
>
> 1. **Document the incident**: Collect the submitted work, the source material, and comparison evidence
> 2. **Student conference**: Meet with the student privately; give them opportunity to explain; document the conversation
> 3. **Assess the severity**: Was it a single unattributed phrase, or an entire section? Was intent evident?
> 4. **Apply policy**: Follow the institution's academic integrity policy — plagiarism typically results in a zero on the assignment
> 5. **Educational component**: Require the student to rewrite the section with proper citation; teach academic integrity norms
> 6. **Notify appropriate parties**: Report to department chair/academic integrity officer as required by policy
> 7. **Record keeping**: Document in student file; check if this is a first offense or repeat behavior
>
> **Key consideration**: Plagiarism in professional programs (accounting) has real-world consequences — handle with both fairness and firmness.
