# Scenario Examples

## 9.1 Common Scenario: Lead Teacher Gives Unclear Instructions

**User:** "The lead teacher gives instructions that half the class doesn't understand. Students keep asking me what to do. How do I handle this?"

**Expert:**
> **Professional Supportive Intervention:**
>
> 1. **Don't undermine publicly**: Never say "I'll explain it differently" in front of students — this implies the lead teacher was wrong.
> 2. **First attempt: rephrasing** (in the moment): "Let me add one thing to what Mr./Ms. [Teacher] said..." Repeat the instruction from a different angle.
> 3. **Check for understanding**: "Can someone tell me what we're supposed to do first?" Let a student who understood explain it — peer explanation is powerful.
> 4. **Post-lesson conversation**: Privately speak with the lead teacher. "I noticed a few students seemed confused. What can I do to support them?" Frame as "how can I help" not "you did something wrong."
> 5. **Proactive next time**: Before the next lesson, briefly ask the lead teacher: "Is there anything I can do to clarify directions for specific students?"
> 6. **Create a visual anchor**: If directions are complex, pre-prepare a written version or flowchart that students can reference.
> 7. **If this is a pattern**: Document instances. If it happens consistently, speak with the department head or coordinator, not as a complaint, but as a request for professional development support.
> 8. **Remember your role**: Your job is to support learning, not to judge the quality of instruction. Professional respect is essential.

## 9.2 Complex Scenario: Student Discloses Abuse to Teaching Assistant

**User:** "A student quietly told me that their parent hits them when they get a bad grade. What do I do?"

**Expert:**
> **Mandatory Reporting Protocol:**
>
> 1. **Stay calm and don't interrogate**: "Thank you for trusting me with that. I want to make sure you're safe." Do NOT ask follow-up questions like "how often?" or "where did they hit you?" — this can contaminate a potential investigation.
> 2. **Do not promise confidentiality you can't keep**: "I have to tell some people who can help keep you safe. Do you understand why?"
> 3. **Report immediately**: You are a mandatory reporter. Report to your school's designated reporter (usually the principal or guidance counselor) within 24 hours, or directly to Child Protective Services.
> 4. **Document exactly what was said**: Use the student's exact words. Do not add interpretation. "Student said, '[words used]'" — not "the student implied..."
> 5. **Support the student**: Do not investigate yourself. Do not confront the parent. Ensure the student knows they did the right thing by telling a trusted adult.
> 6. **Follow school protocol**: Your school has a specific procedure for suspected abuse. Follow it exactly.
> 7. **Seek support for yourself**: Processing disclosure of child abuse is emotionally difficult. Talk to your supervisor or an employee assistance program counselor.
> 8. **Do not discuss with other staff**: Confidentiality about the report is required.

## 9.3 Edge Case: Lead Teacher Expects TA to Do Personal Errands

**User:** "The lead teacher asked me to pick up their dry cleaning and buy them coffee. This feels wrong but I don't want to cause conflict."

**Expert:**
> **Professional Boundary Management:**
>
> 1. **It's okay to say no**: Personal errands are NOT part of a teaching assistant's job description. This is a boundary violation.
> 2. **Professional framing**: "I'm not able to do that during school hours. My priority is supporting student learning." You don't need to explain beyond this.
> 3. **Offer an alternative**: "I can't pick up your dry cleaning, but I'm happy to run copies or prepare materials if that would help."
> 4. **If pressure continues**: Document each request. Speak with your supervisor or HR representative. This is a workplace boundary issue.
> 5. **Trust your instinct**: If something feels wrong, it probably is. You do not have to comply with requests that feel inappropriate, even if phrased as "favors."
> 6. **Know your contract**: Your job duties are defined in writing. Personal errands are not among them.
> 7. **Don't isolate**: Talk to other TAs in your school. If this is happening to you, it may be happening to others. A collective conversation may be more effective.
> 8. **Focus on the students**: Your value is in supporting student learning — not in being a personal assistant. Let that be your anchor.
