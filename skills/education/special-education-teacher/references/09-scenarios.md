# Scenario Examples

## 9.1 Common Scenario: Parent Disagrees with IEP Goals

**User:** "A parent thinks their child's annual goals are too low and don't challenge their child enough. How do I respond?"

**Expert:**
> **Collaborative Goal Negotiation:**
>
> 1. **Listen first**: Thank the parent for their advocacy. "You know your child best. Help me understand what you're seeing at home that makes you feel the goals aren't ambitious enough."
> 2. **Explain the data**: Share baseline data — "Right now, Johnny is reading at a 2nd-grade level when his peers are at 4th. Here's what the data shows."
> 3. **The "high expectations" balance**: Explain that IEP goals must be "appropriately ambitious" — challenging enough to make real progress but realistic based on current data.
> 4. **Raise the bar WITH data**: If parent is right and the goals ARE too low, agree to raise them. "You're right — let's look at whether we should move this goal from 3rd-grade to 4th-grade level. Let me update the IEP."
> 5. **Offer supplemental goals**: Add a "stretch goal" or enrichment objective beyond the annual goal. This gives ambitious students more to work toward.
> 6. **Check for FAPE**: Remember — the goal must be designed to enable the child to make progress appropriate in light of their circumstances. Goals that are too low also violate FAPE.
> 7. **Document everything**: Note the conversation in the IEP meeting minutes; parent's concerns and how the team responded.
> 8. **Offer progress tracking**: "Let's meet again in 60 days to review the data. If your child is exceeding the goal, we'll raise it."

## 9.2 Complex Scenario: Student with Severe Behavior — Restrictive Setting Needed?

**User:** "A student with EBD is disrupting every class so severely that no general education setting is working. Should we move them to a more restrictive environment?"

**Expert:**
> **LRE Decision Framework:**
>
> 1. **Has the BIP been implemented with fidelity?** Before considering placement change, verify that the current behavior plan is actually being implemented.
> 2. **Have we exhausted supplementary aids?** Try: smaller class, co-teaching, behavior classroom aide, sensory tools, schedule modifications, social skills instruction — in that order.
> 3. **What does the data show?** If behavior has not improved after 2+ full cycles of BIP with fidelity, the setting may need to change.
> 4. **Consider a short-term intervention**: A temporary, more restrictive placement (e.g., 30-day behavioral intervention classroom) can sometimes provide the intensive support needed to return to general ed.
> 5. **Consult the team**: The IEP team must meet. This includes the parent, general ed teacher, special ed teacher, administrator, and relevant specialists.
> 6. **Document the continuum**: Show the team that all less-restrictive options were attempted. This protects the school legally.
> 7. **The answer**: Move to more restrictive ONLY if: (a) all less-restrictive options were attempted with fidelity, (b) the student's behavior is a danger to self or others, AND (c) the new setting can actually address the student's needs.
> 8. **Return planning**: Even in a more restrictive setting, develop a plan to return to less restrictive. Never send a student to a more restrictive placement without a clear exit strategy.

## 9.3 Edge Case: Teacher Not Following IEP Accommodations

**User:** "I noticed a general education teacher is not providing a student's agreed-upon accommodations — specifically, they refuse to give extended time on tests. What do I do?"

**Expert:**
> **Accommodation Enforcement Protocol:**
>
> 1. **Informal check-in first**: Privately speak with the teacher. "I noticed [student] didn't receive extended time on yesterday's test. Can we talk about what's happening?" Perhaps they didn't know the student had that accommodation on that specific assessment.
> 2. **Provide a reminder**: Share the relevant section of the IEP. "Here's what the IEP specifies for [student]. Can we problem-solve together on how to make this work?"
> 3. **Offer solutions**: Maybe the teacher is worried about logistics. Offer: (a) you proctor extended time, (b) test can be split across two sessions, (c) student takes test in a different room.
> 4. **Escalate if unresolved**: If the teacher continues to refuse, meet with your department head or special education coordinator. The teacher is legally obligated to implement accommodations.
> 5. **Document the non-compliance**: Keep records of the conversation and dates. If the student is not making progress due to missing accommodations, this is a denial of FAPE.
> 6. **Parent notification**: If the issue persists, inform the parent. Parents can file a complaint with the state education agency if accommodations are consistently denied.
> 7. **Do not implement for the teacher**: Don't just take over — empower the teacher to implement properly while you support them.
> 8. **Know your legal boundaries**: Accommodation denial is a civil rights issue. Document thoroughly; escalation is sometimes necessary.
