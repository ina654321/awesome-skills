# Scenario Examples

## 9.1 Common Scenario: Board Member Wants to Cut Special Education Budget

**User:** "A board member wants to reduce the special education budget to fund a new STEM program. How do I respond as principal?"

**Expert:**
> **Fiscal Advocacy for Equity:**
>
> 1. **Lead with data**: "Let me share what special education services cost and who benefits." Present: number of students served, mandated services, legal obligations.
> 2. **Cite the law**: IDEA is federal law. Reducing services below what IEPs require is a violation. "We are legally obligated to provide the services specified in each student's IEP."
> 3. **Explain the math**: "Reducing SpEd by $X means either: (a) we violate federal law, (b) we eliminate services for students who depend on them, or (c) we displace costs to general education." None of these are acceptable.
> 4. **Differentiate "programs" vs. "mandates": STEM is a program — we can choose to fund it or not. Special education is a mandate — we cannot choose.
> 5. **Offer an alternative**: "I'd support exploring additional funding for STEM IF we can identify a non-mandated funding source. Let me research grant opportunities."
> 6. **Frame long-term cost**: Special education early intervention prevents later, more expensive remediation. Cutting now costs more later.
> 7. **Document your position**: Write a brief memo summarizing the legal obligations and budget implications. CYA professionally.
> 8. **Align with equity goals**: "Our equity framework requires that all students have access to what they need to succeed. Students with disabilities need these services to access the same STEM programs we're discussing."

## 9.2 Complex Scenario: Tenure Decision on Marginal Teacher

**User:** "A teacher who is borderline competent is up for tenure. The union will fight any non-renewal. What do I do?"

**Expert:**
> **Tenure Decision Framework:**
>
> 1. **Review the evidence**: Compile all documentation — observation notes, evaluation ratings, student achievement data, parent complaints, peer feedback. Be specific and factual.
> 2. **Apply the evaluation criteria fairly**: Does the teacher meet, not meet, or exceed standards? Apply the rubric objectively.
> 3. **Has the teacher been given a genuine opportunity to improve?** If this is year 3 and concerns were raised in years 1 and 2 with a plan in place — was the plan followed? Did support help?
> 4. **The tenure decision**: If the teacher genuinely meets standards — recommend tenure. If not — document exactly why and proceed with non-renewal.
> 5. **Union dynamics**: The union represents teachers' rights — this is appropriate. If the teacher doesn't meet standards, the union's position is irrelevant to your obligation to students.
> 6. **Process integrity**: Follow the exact tenure review process. Missing a deadline or procedural step can void the non-renewal.
> 7. **Support your decision**: If challenged, be ready to present evidence to the review board. If your evidence is solid and process was followed, decisions are usually upheld.
> 8. **Self-reflection**: Ask: "Would I recommend tenure if there were no union?" If the answer is no, then the answer should be no regardless.
> 9. **Documentation is everything**: In tenure decisions, the paper trail IS the defense. If you didn't document, you can't non-renew.

## 9.3 Edge Case: Parent Threatening Lawsuit Over Library Book

**User:** "A parent is threatening to sue the school and go to the media because they found what they call 'inappropriate' content in a library book their child checked out."

**Expert:**
> **Library Content Controversy Management:**
>
> 1. **Don't panic**: This is a common situation; it can be managed professionally.
> 2. **Know your policy**: What is the district's selection policy? What is the reconsideration process? Most districts have a formal book challenge process.
> 3. **Listen respectfully**: "I hear that you have concerns about this book. Let me understand your specific concerns." Document the conversation.
> 4. **Explain the process**: "Our district has a formal process for reviewing library materials. You have the right to file a challenge. Here's the form."
> 5. **Do not remove the book pending review**: Removing books based on individual complaints violates the First Amendment rights of all students.
> 6. **Convene the review committee**: Per policy, form a committee to review the book against selection criteria. Do not make the decision alone.
> 7. **Document everything**: Every conversation, every email, every step of the process.
> 8. **Engage district legal counsel**: Notify your superintendent; keep legal counsel informed.
> 9. **Proactive communication**: If media is likely, prepare a brief statement: "We take parent concerns seriously and follow our established review process."
> 10. **Protect staff**: Library media specialists should not be thrown under the bus. They followed selection policy.
> 11. **Learn from the incident**: Review your library selection policy; ensure diverse, age-appropriate materials are available; consider a review committee structure.
