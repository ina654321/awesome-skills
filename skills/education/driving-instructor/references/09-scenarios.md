# Scenario Examples

## 9.1 Common Scenario: Parallel Parking Anxiety

**User:** "My teenager is completely terrified of parallel parking. What should we practice?"

**Expert:**
> **Graduated Exposure Approach:**
>
> Don't force parallel parking immediately. Build confidence in sequence:
>
> 1. Practice visual alignment (walk beside car, estimate 3-foot clearance from curb)
> 2. Practice 90-degree backing into a painted rectangle in an empty lot
> 3. Progress to a straight curb with one car as reference point
> 4. Then practice full parallel parking in low-traffic areas during off-peak hours
>
> Remind teen: First gear is not required in parking spaces — creep forward and adjust. The key reference point is aligning your front bumper with the rear bumper of the car ahead, then turning the wheel hard toward the curb at 45 degrees.

## 9.2 Complex Scenario: Parent-Teen Conflict During Practice

**User:** "Every time I try to teach my daughter to drive, we end up arguing. How do I stay calm?"

**Expert:**
> **De-escalation Protocol:**
>
> 1. **Establish pre-drive rules**: Agree on a signal (e.g., raised hand) meaning "let's pause." Pull over to discuss, never while moving.
> 2. **Frame coaching as observation**: "I'm your co-pilot, not your critic." Use "I noticed..." statements, not "You should..."
> 3. **Schedule separate "driving time"**: Not combined with family errands when tension is likely
> 4. **Consider professional lessons**: Even 2-3 sessions with an instructor can reduce parent pressure
>
> Key principle: The goal is not perfection — it's developing judgment. Celebrate good decisions, not flawless execution.

## 9.3 Edge Case: Student With Learning Disability (ADHD)

**User:** "My son has ADHD and struggles with processing everything while driving. Any accommodations?"

**Expert:**
> **Structured Input Strategy:**
>
> 1. Use "think-aloud" method: Ask student to narrate everything they see and plan to do
> 2. Chunk information: Process one hazard at a time before combining
> 3. Externalize memory aids: Checklists taped to dashboard for routine procedures
> 4. Shorter, more frequent lessons: 30-40 minutes rather than 60+ to maintain focus
> 5. Reduce in-car distractions: No music, no phone, no passengers during skill-building phases
>
> Consult with occupational therapist who specializes in driving for formal assessment and adaptive equipment recommendations.
