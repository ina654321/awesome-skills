# Scenario Examples

## 9.1 Common Scenario: Mother with Cracked Nipples Complaining of Pain

**User:** "A new mother in our postpartum unit is crying every time she breastfeeds due to nipple pain. What should the nurse assess?"

**Expert:**
> **Nipple Pain Assessment Protocol:**
>
> 1. **LATCH assessment**: Score each feeding — latch quality is the most common cause of nipple trauma
> 2. **Inspect nipples**: Look for cracks (usually at 6 or 12 o'clock = tongue-tie or poor latch angle), bleeding, blanching (circulation issue), or vasospasm (white streaks)
> 3. **Assess breast**: Fullness, engorgement, blocked duct? Engorgement can cause poor latch
> 4. **Check positioning**: Cross-cradle or football hold? Shoulder-to-ear alignment? Baby's body facing mother's body?
> 5. **Key question**: "When does the pain start?" Pain at latch (sharp, stabbing) = latch problem. Pain mid-feed (burning, throbbing) = candidiasis or vasospasm.
> 6. **Interventions**:
>    - For latch issues: Break suction with finger, reposition, try again
>    - Apply expressed breast milk after feeds (natural healing)
>    - Medical-grade lanolin if no allergy
>    - Hydrogel pads for comfort
>    - Consider nipple shield if severe (temporary measure only)
> 7. **Refer**: If pain persists >48 hours despite intervention, refer to IBCLC or physician
> 8. **Documentation**: Record pain level, nipple condition, interventions, and maternal response

## 9.2 Complex Scenario: Postpartum Hemorrhage

**User:** "During your shift, a patient who delivered 2 hours ago shows signs of heavy bleeding. Walk me through your response."

**Expert:**
> **PPH Response Protocol (Obstetric Hemorrhage):**
>
> 1. **Call for help**: Activate emergency response — "I need the obstetric emergency team to room [number] now."
> 2. **ABCs**: Ensure airway and breathing; administer oxygen via mask
> 3. **Massage uterus**: Bimanual uterine massage immediately. A boggy uterus is the most common cause — massage often stops bleeding.
> 4. **IV access**: Large bore IV (16G or 18G); draw blood for CBC, crossmatch
> 5. **Medications**: Administer uterotonics per protocol (Oxytocin → Methylergonovine → Carboprost → Misoprostol — per order or per emergency protocol)
> 6. **Fluid resuscitation**: NS or RL bolus; prepare for transfusion if needed
> 7. **Monitor**: Vital signs every 5 minutes; Foley catheter to monitor urine output
> 8. **Document**: Time of onset, estimated blood loss (weigh pads), interventions, response
> 9. **Notify physician**: After initial stabilization, ensure provider is aware and has assessed the patient
> 10. **Post-event**: Debrief, complete incident report, offer emotional support to patient and staff
>
> Remember: A blood loss of >500mL (vaginal) or >1000mL (C-section) in the first 24 hours = postpartum hemorrhage. Act fast.

## 9.3 Edge Case: Mother Refuses Treatment, Wants to Leave AMA

**User:** "A postpartum mother who had a complicated delivery (preeclampsia) wants to leave against medical advice. Her baby is also in the NICU. What do you do?"

**Expert:**
> **AMA Decision Support:**
>
> 1. **Understand the motivation**: Sit down and talk. Is she overwhelmed? Does she have support at home? Is she worried about her NICU baby? Address the underlying concern.
> 2. **Explain risks clearly**: Preeclampsia can deteriorate rapidly postpartum. She needs BP monitoring. Leaving AMA without monitoring is dangerous.
> 3. **Mini-Mental State check**: Is she refusing due to postpartum psychosis or severe depression? If so, this is a mental health emergency, not an AMA situation.
> 4. **Discuss NICU baby**: If her baby is in NICU, does she understand that her departure affects her ability to visit or breastfeed?
> 5. **Negotiate alternatives**:
>    - Can she be discharged today with same-day follow-up?
>    - Home BP monitoring program?
>    - Shorter-stay observation with clear discharge criteria?
> 6. **If she insists on leaving**: Provider must explain risks; patient signs AMA form; ensure she has emergency contact numbers
> 7. **Documentation**: Complete AMA form; document that risks were explained; note mental status assessment
> 8. **Child protective services**: If you believe the baby is at risk, consult social work and potentially report to CPS, but this is a last resort and only if there are safety concerns
