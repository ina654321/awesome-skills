# Scenario Examples

## 9.1 Common Scenario: Child Makes Great Progress in Session, No Carryover at Home

**User:** "A language-disordered child hits 90% accuracy in therapy but parents report zero carryover at home. What's happening?"

**Expert:**
> **Generalization Failure Analysis:**
>
> 1. **Isolated practice context**: Therapy room is a highly controlled, reinforcing environment. Home is not. This is called "context-dependent learning."
> 2. **Parent education gap**: Are parents even aware of what the child is working on? Send weekly home program notes with ONE specific strategy.
> 3. **Too many targets**: If therapy is working on 5 things, there's no bandwidth for generalization. Narrow to 1-2 priority targets.
> 4. **Make it functional**: The word "fork" matters more at home than the word "horse." Choose targets that are relevant to daily life.
> 5. **Train the trainer**: Teach parents HOW to correct — not just WHAT to work on. Model the correction technique during the session so parents can watch.
> 6. **Video model**: Record a successful session. Parents watch and replicate the same interaction style at home.
> 7. **Incidental teaching**: Parents don't need structured therapy — they need to embed learning into daily routines. "Can you hand me the BIG cup?" during breakfast is powerful.
> 8. **Gradual release**: Start with therapist prompting parent → parent prompts with therapist observing → parent implements independently.

## 9.2 Complex Scenario: Parent Refuses Bilingual Assessment, Wants English Only

**User:** "A bilingual family refuses a bilingual assessment, insisting their child only needs English. The child is failing academically."

**Expert:**
> **Bilingual Assessment Advocacy:**
>
> 1. **Explain the legal/ethical basis**: IDEA requires assessment in the child's native language. This isn't optional — it's federal law.
> 2. **Explain the practical reason**: "If we only test in English, we can't tell if the problem is English proficiency or a true language disorder. We could misdiagnose."
> 3. **Offer compromise**: "We can do a bilingual evaluation. One session in English, one in [Spanish/Mandarin/other]. This gives us the complete picture."
> 4. **Bring data**: Share research showing that bilingual assessment leads to more accurate diagnosis and more effective treatment.
> 5. **Explain the cost of not assessing**: If the child has a true disorder and we miss it because we only tested English, we're delaying intervention — with lifelong academic consequences.
> 6. **Offer interpreter support**: "We have certified interpreters who can help. There's no cost to you."
> 7. **Document everything**: Document that you explained the legal requirement, the reason for bilingual assessment, and that the parent refused.
> 8. **If parent still refuses**: You may need to proceed with English-only assessment but clearly document limitations in the report: "Assessment validity limited; true language abilities may be underestimated."
> 9. **Offer re-evaluation**: "We can revisit this in one year, and we can do a bilingual assessment then if you'd like."

## 9.3 Edge Case: Stuttering Client Asking About "Cure"

**User:** "A 12-year-old with moderate stuttering asks me directly, 'Can you fix me?' How do I respond?"

**Expert:**
> **Developmentally Appropriate Stuttering Counseling:**
>
> 1. **Validate the feeling**: "I can hear how much this matters to you, and it takes courage to ask that question directly."
> 2. **Be honest with hope**: "Here's what I know: there is no quick 'fix' for stuttering. But there IS a lot we can do to make speaking easier and more comfortable."
> 3. **Explain the neurological basis**: "Stuttering is about how your brain plans speech. It's not something you caused, and it's not something you can just think away. But we can train your speech system to work more smoothly."
> 4. **Share evidence**: "Most people who work on stuttering therapy see real improvement. Some become nearly fluent; others stutter less and feel much more confident."
> 5. **Address the emotional component**: "I also want you to know — stuttering doesn't define who you are. Many successful people stutter. What matters most is how you feel about communicating."
> 6. **Present the plan**: "Let me tell you what we'll work on together: making your speech smoother, building your confidence, and helping you feel comfortable in conversations."
> 7. **Parent follow-up**: Have a separate conversation with parents about realistic expectations and the importance of not putting pressure on fluency.
> 8. **Refer if needed**: If the child shows signs of social anxiety or depression related to stuttering, refer to a psychologist familiar with stuttering.
