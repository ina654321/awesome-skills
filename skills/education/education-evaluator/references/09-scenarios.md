# Scenario Examples

## 9.1 Common Scenario: Vendor Claims vs. Evidence

**User:** "A vendor claims their product has 'research-proven results' but I can't find the study. How do I verify?"

**Expert:**
> **Evidence Verification Protocol:**
>
> 1. **Request the study directly**: Ask the vendor for the specific citation. Reputable vendors will share. If they refuse or deflect, this is a red flag.
> 2. **Check the study quality**: Is it published in a peer-reviewed journal? Was it an RCT or quasi-experimental? What was the sample size? Were results replicated?
> 3. **Check What Works Clearinghouse**: Search at ies.ed.gov/ncee/wwc/ — the gold standard for evidence quality.
> 4. **Look for independent replication**: A single study is not proof. Look for multiple independent replications across different contexts.
> 5. **Compare effect sizes**: Even if statistically significant, is the effect practically meaningful? A 0.05-point improvement on a 100-point scale is negligible.
> 6. **Check sample demographics**: Were the study participants similar to your student population? A study on urban gifted students may not generalize to rural mixed-ability classrooms.

## 9.2 Complex Scenario: Evaluating Mid-Year Program Adjustment

**User:** "We're halfway through the school year and need to decide whether to continue with the new literacy app. What evaluation can we do quickly?"

**Expert:**
> **Rapid Formative Evaluation:**
>
> 1. **Usage data review**: How many students are actually using the app weekly? Active user rate >70% is healthy; <40% signals engagement problems.
> 2. **Teacher perception survey**: 10-question Likert survey on ease of use, curriculum alignment, and perceived student benefit. Can deploy in one week.
> 3. **Student self-report**: Quick exit ticket asking "Did you learn something today?" with one open-ended question.
> 4. **Comparison cohort**: If possible, compare assessment scores of students using the app vs. similar students not using it.
> 5. **Decision framework**: If usage >70%, teachers perceive value, and trends are positive — continue with monitoring. If usage <50% or negative teacher feedback — discontinue and reallocate budget.
> 6. **Document decision rationale**: Even mid-year changes need clear documentation for accountability.

## 9.3 Edge Case: Conflicting Results Across Stakeholder Groups

**User:** "Teachers love the program, students are neutral, but test scores dropped. What does this mean?"

**Expert:**
> **Conflicting Data Analysis:**
>
> 1. **Temporal disconnect**: Test scores reflect learning from weeks or months ago. Teacher and student perceptions reflect recent experiences. They may be measuring different time periods.
> 2. **Measurement validity**: Are the test scores measuring the same constructs the program teaches? Misaligned assessments explain this pattern.
> 3. **Implementation fidelity**: Was the program implemented as designed? Teacher enthusiasm often precedes student outcome changes by one or more cycles.
> 4. **Regression to the mean**: If the school selected the program because prior scores were low, some score improvement is expected naturally.
> 5. **Recommendation**: Do not make a discontinuation decision based on one cycle of data. Extend evaluation for another term. Investigate assessment alignment. Conduct student focus groups to understand neutral perceptions.
