# Common Pitfalls & Anti-Patterns

## 10.1 Anti-Patterns

| # | Anti-Pattern | Severity | Quick Fix |
|---|---------------|:---:|-----------|
| 1 | **Evaluating after the fact** | High | Build evaluation planning into program design from day 1 |
| 2 | **Cherry-picking favorable data** | High | Pre-register evaluation questions and metrics before data collection |
| 3 | **Ignoring qualitative data** | High | Mixed methods catch what numbers miss — always include interviews |
| 4 | **Over-relying on self-reported data** | Medium | Self-report is subject to social desirability bias; triangulate |
| 5 | **Confusing activity with outcomes** | Medium | Outputs (sessions completed) ≠ outcomes (skills mastered) |

## 10.2 Evaluation Design Mistakes

1. **No comparison group**: Without a control or comparison, you cannot attribute change to the intervention
2. **Short timeframe**: 4-week pilots cannot measure sustainable behavior change or long-term retention
3. **Hawthorne effect**: Participants may perform better simply because they are being observed
4. **Attrition bias**: If dropout students differ systematically from completers, results are biased
5. **Ceiling/floor effects**: If students are already at max/min scores, improvement cannot be detected

## 10.3 Quality Checklist

- [ ] Evaluation questions defined and pre-registered before data collection
- [ ] Logic model or theory of change developed and shared with stakeholders
- [ ] Mixed-methods approach (quantitative + qualitative) planned
- [ ] Sample size calculated for statistical power
- [ ] IRB or ethics approval obtained if using human subjects data
- [ ] Data collection instruments piloted and validated
- [ ] Triangulation plan documented (which sources confirm which findings)
- [ ] Stakeholder member-checking conducted on draft findings
- [ ] Recommendations are specific, actionable, and linked to evidence
- [ ] Limitations section honestly addresses threats to validity
