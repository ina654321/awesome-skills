# Scenario Examples

## 9.1 Common Scenario: Student Struggling with Literature Review

**User:** "My master's student has read 40 papers but can't seem to organize them into a coherent literature review. How do I help?"

**Expert:**
> **Scaffolding Approach:**
>
> 1. **Stop reading, start organizing**: Ask the student to stop accumulating papers. 40 papers is sufficient for a master's literature review — the issue is structure, not more sources.
> 2. **Thesis-first framework**: Ask student to write the conclusion of the literature review first — what argument do they want the review to make? Then work backward.
> 3. **Thematic clustering**: Sort papers by themes (not chronologically). Give student 5-7 colored sticky notes, each representing one theme. Move papers into piles.
> 4. **Writing rhythm**: Use the "30-minute sprint" method — write for 30 minutes, no editing, then 10-minute break. Stop at a place where you know what comes next.
> 5. **Chapter outline**: Require an outline with 3 levels of detail before writing any section. Review and approve outline before student writes prose.

## 9.2 Complex Scenario: Student Wants to Change Research Direction Mid-Dissertation

**User:** "After 18 months of work, my PhD student says they want to switch from quantitative to qualitative methods. What should I do?"

**Expert:**
> **Decision Framework:**
>
> 1. **Understand the motivation**: Is this motivated by genuine intellectual reasons or avoidance of statistical difficulty? Have an honest conversation about both.
> 2. **Timeline impact**: A method switch at this stage typically adds 6-12 months. Is the student prepared for this?
> 3. **What data exists?** Can any existing data be repurposed for qualitative analysis? Sometimes partial overlap exists.
> 4. **New skills required**: Qualitative analysis requires training in coding, thematic analysis, and potentially ethnographic methods. Plan for this.
> 5. **Committee consultation**: Convene committee meeting. The decision needs committee approval, and the student's funding situation may need reconsideration.
> 6. **Written proposal**: Require student to write a 3-page rationale for the switch and revised timeline. This often clarifies thinking.
> 7. **Decision**: If intellectually justified and timeline is acceptable to student — approve with conditions. If not — help student articulate why the current path remains viable.

## 9.3 Edge Case: Mental Health Crisis in Graduate Student

**User:** "A doctoral student is showing signs of severe anxiety and depression. They haven't submitted work in 3 months. What do I do?"

**Expert:**
> **Support and Action Protocol:**
>
> 1. **Private, non-judgmental conversation**: "I've noticed you seem to be going through a difficult time. I'm concerned about you. How are you really doing?" Avoid framing as an academic problem first.
> 2. **Provide campus resources**: Share counseling center contact information directly. Offer to help them make an appointment if they feel unable to.
> 3. **Do not withdraw supervision**: Students in crisis need MORE support, not less. Temporarily increase meeting frequency if appropriate.
> 4. **Flexibility in timeline**: Work with graduate program to extend deadlines without punitive academic consequences where possible.
> 5. **Document support provided**: Note that you have offered resources and flexibility. This protects both you and the student.
> 6. **Set a check-in plan**: Agree on a specific date to reconnect. "Let's meet again in two weeks. If you're not ready by then, please email me so we can adjust."
> 7. **Escalation**: If student expresses suicidal ideation, contact campus crisis services immediately.
