# Scenario Examples

## 9.1 Common Scenario: Trainee Cannot Keep Up with Lab Exercises

**User:** "During a hands-on networking lab, half the class is done in 10 minutes while the other half is still on step 2. How do I manage this?"

**Expert:**
> **Differentiated Instruction Response:**
>
> 1. **Pre-prep**: Create two lab guides — standard (with step-by-step screenshots) and advanced (with fewer hints, bonus challenges)
> 2. **Checkpoint system**: Place numbered checkpoints in the lab; trainees show you completion before advancing. Quick way to see who's on track.
> 3. **Pair work**: Pair fast finishers with those who need help — teaching reinforces learning for both
> 4. **Micro-lecture, not full lecture**: Keep instruction under 10 minutes per block; then practice immediately
> 5. **Fast-track bonus**: Early finishers get a "bonus challenge" (e.g., configure the same setup via command line instead of GUI) — they feel challenged, not bored
> 6. **Breather step**: For slower trainees, add a "reflection" checkpoint: "Before you continue, explain in one sentence what you just did and why." This builds understanding, not just replication.
> 7. **Don't leave them behind**: Circulate the room every 5 minutes. Catch confusion early.

## 9.2 Complex Scenario: Training for Layoffs vs. Training for Growth

**User:** "We have a team of 20 being retrained from legacy ERP to cloud ERP. Half will be laid off after migration. Do I train them differently?"

**Expert:**
> **Ethical Training Design:**
>
> 1. **Be transparent**: Employees deserve to know their situation. Explain that some roles will change, but training still benefits their careers.
> 2. **Don't sandbag**: Training quality should be the same for all. Under-training those being laid off is unethical and often legally problematic.
> 3. **Tailor emphasis**: For employees transitioning out — focus on resume building, interview skills, and portable certifications. For those staying — focus on advanced cloud administration.
> 4. **Separation timeline**: Plan training so that those being retained get additional advanced modules after the migration is complete.
> 5. **Outplacement support**: Partner with HR to offer job search assistance, interview coaching, and networking resources alongside technical training.
> 6. **Document decisions**: Record why you structured training as you did. This protects the organization if anyone challenges fairness.

## 9.3 Edge Case: Technical Issue Breaks Lab for Entire Class

**User:** "Right in the middle of the critical lab exercise, the server goes down and we can't restore it for 45 minutes. What do I do with the remaining class time?"

**Expert:**
> **Recovery Protocol:**
>
> 1. **Stay calm and transparent**: "The server is experiencing issues — this happens in real IT environments too. Let's use this time productively."
> 2. **Switch to theoretical mode**: Conduct a whiteboard design session — have students design the architecture they'd just configured, explaining each component.
> 3. **Paper troubleshooting**: Use the time for troubleshooting scenarios on paper — "Here's a log file with errors. What's your diagnosis?"
> 4. **Server restored?**: Catch up the group in 5-minute dense session with key steps highlighted.
> 5. **Server still down at end of class**: Extend the lab to a follow-up session or provide recorded lab walkthrough for self-study. Do not skip the hands-on component entirely — IT training without hands-on practice has poor transfer.
> 6. **Document the incident**: Note this for lab environment improvement. Server reliability is a training quality issue.
