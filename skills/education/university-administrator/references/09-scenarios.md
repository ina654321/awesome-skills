# Scenario Examples

## 9.1 Common Scenario: Faculty Senate Opposes Administrative Decision

**User:** "Our faculty senate voted to oppose a new academic program restructuring I'm proposing. The provost supports it. What do I do?"

**Expert:**
> **Shared Governance Navigation:**
>
> 1. **Respect the process**: Faculty governance is not an obstacle — it's a legitimate voice. Ignoring it leads to conflict, delays, and poor outcomes.
> 2. **Schedule a dialogue**: "I respect the senate's vote. I'd like to understand the specific concerns. Can we meet?"
> 3. **Listen to understand**: Don't defend your position yet. Ask: "What specifically concerns you? What data would change your view?"
> 4. **Address the substance**: Faculty concerns are often about substance, not obstruction. Find out if there are legitimate academic quality issues.
> 5. **Find common ground**: Is there a modified version of the plan that addresses faculty concerns while achieving your goals?
> 6. **Present your evidence**: Share data supporting the decision — enrollment trends, resource constraints, student demand. Facts inform the debate.
> 7. **Compromise if appropriate**: Sometimes "good enough and approved" beats "perfect but blocked."
> 8. **Know your authority**: You have decision-making authority on certain matters. Faculty governance has consultative or shared authority on others. Know the distinction.
> 9. **Escalate if needed**: If irreconcilable, this may need to go to the board or chancellor. Document your consultation with faculty.
> 10. **Move forward collaboratively**: Even after a decision, work to maintain faculty relations. A divided campus serves no one.

## 9.2 Complex Scenario: Enrollment Drops 15% — Budget Crisis Imminent

**User:** "We just finished enrollment and it's 15% below projection. We have a balanced budget based on those numbers. How do I respond?"

**Expert:**
> **Enrollment Crisis Management:**
>
> 1. **Immediate financial assessment** (Week 1): Finance office models scenarios — 5% shortfall vs. 15% shortfall. Identify the cash runway.
> 2. **Convene leadership** (Week 1): President, CFO, provost, enrollment management. Understand the full picture together.
> 3. **Analyze the drop**: Is this demographic (fewer high school graduates)? Competitive (students choosing peers)? Internal (enrollment funnel problems)? Each has different solutions.
> 4. **Short-term cost containment** (Weeks 2-4): Hiring freeze (except critical positions), discretionary spending freeze, defer non-essential purchases.
> 5. **Revenue maximization**: Can we increase summer enrollment? Expand adult-learner programs? Accelerate international student recruitment?
> 6. **Mid-term budget revision**: Present revised budget to board. Transparency is essential — don't hide the problem.
> 7. **Enrollment strategy pivot**: Partner with enrollment management for immediate-yield outreach — waitlist admits, deposit deadline extensions.
> 8. **Long-term structural review**: Is the institution's cost structure sustainable at current enrollment? This may require program consolidation or strategic realignment.
> 9. **Communicate proactively**: Board, faculty, staff, and donors (if applicable) need to hear from leadership — not rumors. Brief them early.
> 10. **Don't cut academic quality first**: Retention of current students is more cost-effective than new recruitment. Protect the core academic experience.

## 9.3 Edge Case: Title IX Complaint Against a Senior Professor

**User:** "A Title IX complaint was filed against a well-funded, highly published senior professor. The provost is pressuring me to 'handle it quietly.' What are my obligations?"

**Expert:**
> **Title IX Compliance Protocol:**
>
> 1. **Know the law**: Title IX is federal law. Retaliation against complainants is prohibited. "Handling it quietly" is not an option.
> 2. **Notify Title IX coordinator immediately**: The moment you know, you are obligated to report to the Title IX coordinator. Don't delay.
> 3. **Notify legal counsel**: Keep the university's general counsel informed. This situation has legal exposure.
> 4. **Interim measures**: The complainant has the right to interim measures — change in class schedule, no contact orders, counseling referrals. These are not punitive.
> 5. **Do not conduct your own investigation**: An independent, trained investigator (not a friend of the accused) must handle this.
> 6. **Do not pressure the complainant**: No one — including the provost — should pressure a complainant to withdraw. This is retaliation.
> 7. **Due process for the accused**: The professor also has rights. Fair, prompt, and thorough investigation protects everyone.
> 8. **Protect confidentiality**: Share information only on a need-to-know basis. Both parties deserve privacy.
> 9. **Document everything**: Every conversation, every decision, every action. Paper trail is essential.
> 10. **Follow the policy**: The university's Title IX policy exists precisely for this situation. Follow it exactly.
> 11. **If pressure continues from provost**: Escalate to the president or board. "Handling it quietly" in a Title IX case can expose the university to federal investigation and loss of federal funding.
