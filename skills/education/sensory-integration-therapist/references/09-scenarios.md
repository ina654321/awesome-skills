# Scenario Examples

## 9.1 Common Scenario: Child Unable to Tolerate Messy Play

**User:** "A 5-year-old with sensory sensitivities refuses to touch anything messy — finger paint, playdough, sand. How do I introduce tactile tolerance?"

**Expert:**
> **Graduated Exposure for Tactile Tolerance:**
>
> 1. **Start with the least aversive**: Begin with dry materials (rice, dry beans) in a container — less sensory intensity
> 2. **Use a barrier first**: Let child use a spoon or glove to interact with messy materials before direct touch
> 3. **Offer choice**: "Do you want to touch the playdough with your left hand or right hand?" Choice reduces anxiety
> 4. **Pair with positive input**: Combine aversive touch with calming input (deep pressure through shoulders, or slow linear swinging) before introducing messy material
> 5. **Short exposure**: Start with 30 seconds only. "Let's put your hand in for just 5 seconds." Reward with preferred activity
> 6. **Gradual increase**: Increase duration by 5 seconds per session when tolerated. No pressure — if refusal, return to prior level
> 7. **Self-regulation co-regulation**: Therapist provides calm, regulated presence. If therapist is anxious, child mirrors that
> 8. **Celebrate effort, not just success**: "You kept your hand near the playdough — that took courage!"

## 9.2 Complex Scenario: Over-responsive vs. Under-responsive Sensory Patterns

**User:** "I can't tell if a child is over-responsive or under-responsive to auditory input. She covers her ears but also doesn't respond when I call her name. What do I look for?"

**Expert:**
> **Differentiating Sensory Patterns:**
>
> 1. **Timing**: Over-responsive children react IMMEDIATELY to sound. Under-responsive children show delayed or no reaction.
> 2. **Habituation**: Over-responsive: doesn't habituate — the sound bothers them every time. Under-responsive: may not habituate but also doesn't show distress.
> 3. **Discrimination**: Call the child's name vs. another name. Over-responsive: flinches to both; under-responsive: ignores both but responds to their name.
> 4. **Threshold context**: Under-responsive children often show a response to STRONG input (very loud, very rhythmic) but not to moderate input
> 5. **Co-occurrence**: Auditory defensiveness often co-occurs with tactile defensiveness. Pure under-responsiveness often co-occurs with proprioceptive seeking
> 6. **The answer**: This child likely has a MIXED pattern — both patterns can coexist in the same sensory system
> 7. **Treatment approach**: Use calming input (slow rocking, weighted blanket) before auditory activities for over-responsive elements. Increase volume and salience to engage under-responsive elements.
> 8. **Refer for audiology**: Always rule out hearing impairment before attributing behavior to sensory processing

## 9.3 Edge Case: Parent Demanding More Sensory Input at Home

**User:** "A parent wants me to give them a 3-hour sensory diet for their child to do at home every day. That's too much. How do I respond?"

**Expert:**
> **Parent Coaching on Appropriate Sensory Dose:**
>
> 1. **Acknowledge the parent's engagement**: This is a good sign — an engaged parent is the best predictor of therapy success
> 2. **Educate on "just right challenge"**: Explain that too much input without therapeutic guidance can cause dysregulation, not improvement. "More" isn't always better.
> 3. **Offer a structured but reasonable plan**: "Here's a 15-20 minute sensory routine to do in the morning and afternoon." Write it down as a visual schedule.
> 4. **Prioritize**: Focus on 2-3 key activities that address the child's primary sensory needs. Don't overwhelm the family.
> 5. **Explain the therapist's role**: "The equipment in our clinic — the swing, the crash pad — requires professional guidance for safe use. Some activities are not safe without supervision."
> 6. **Collaborative scheduling**: Work with the family to identify realistic times. "When is your family least stressed?" Build into existing routines (bath time = deep pressure; bedtime = calming).
> 7. **Monitor and adjust**: "Let's check in after 2 weeks. If it's working, we add more. If the child is dysregulated, we adjust."
> 8. **Set boundaries**: If parent continues to demand more than is evidence-based or safe, address in a team meeting and document your clinical reasoning.
