# Wood Carving — Real-World Scenarios

## Scenario 1: First Relief Panel Commission

**Context:** A client commissions a 12×18 inch walnut relief panel of a horse for their riding stable. Budget: $800. Timeline: 6 weeks.

**Assessment:**
- Walnut is a medium-hard wood (1010 Janka) — appropriate for intermediate carver
- Horse anatomy requires anatomical study before carving
- High relief will show muscles, mane, and dynamic pose
- Client may want staining/finish or natural oil finish

**Planning:**
1. Source quarter-sawn walnut for consistent grain (flat-sawn will show wide, wild grain)
2. Reference: classical Greek horse sculptures for anatomy
3. Sketch design on paper first, then transfer to wood
4. Rough out with #1 chisel and mallet, refine with gouges
5. Final detail with V-tool for mane strands and hoof definition

**Technical decisions:**
- Relief depth: Background 2mm, secondary elements 8mm, horse 15-20mm
- Sharp highlight edges on horse contours create dramatic shadow
- Leave some natural defects (small knots) for authenticity

## Scenario 2: Correcting a Mistake on an Antique Restoration

**Context:** A museum brings in a 19th-century European carving with a broken finger on a cherub. The break is fresh (recent transport damage).

**Challenge:**
- Must match original carving style (Baroque, high detail)
- Original wood likely old-growth European linden
- Repair must be reversible (museum conservation standard)
- Cannot remove original patina

**Approach:**
1. Carve replacement finger from matched aged linden (salvage wood)
2. Use conservation-grade adhesive (Beva 371 or similar reversible adhesive)
3. Match surface texture with fine carving and sanding
4. Do NOT fill or patch — match original wood
5. Document repair with photographs and report for museum records

**Alternative if wood is unavailable:**
- Use tinted reversible filler (conservation standard)
- Slightly undercut replacement so it reads as a restoration (museum ethics)

## Scenario 3: Carving Outdoor Signage

**Context:** A brewery needs a carved wooden sign: 24×36 inch, "The Rusty Anchor." Pine or cedar, must last outdoors 5+ years.

**Material selection:**
- Eastern White Pine: 380 Janka, easy to carve, but requires annual maintenance
- Western Red Cedar: 350 Janka, natural rot resistance, carved detail holds
- White Oak: 1360 Janka, extremely durable but difficult carving
- Decision: Cedar with carved inlay of contrasting wood (basswood or cherry) for letters

**Construction:**
- Carve letters and design into cedar board
- Inlay basswood fish and anchor elements
- Routhing and incising background texture
- Back of sign carved hollow for drainage and weight reduction

**Finishing for outdoor durability:**
1. Penetrol or boiled linseed oil as base coat (3 coats)
2. Marine varnish topcoat (Epifanes or similar)
3. Stainless hardware for mounting
4. Overhang to shed water
5. Annual inspection and recoat

## Scenario 4: Teaching a Child to Carve Safely

**Context:** A 10-year-old wants to learn wood carving with their grandparent. Grandparent is an experienced carver but has never taught children.

**Safety assessment:**
- 10-year-old can safely use chip carving knife with supervision
- Not ready for gouges or mallet work
- Small, manageable project first
- Teach respect for sharp edges, not fear

**First project: Chip-carved wooden bookmark**
- Basswood board 2×8 inches
- Simple geometric pattern (triangles, crescents)
- Chip carving knife with finger guards (Flexcut detail knife)
- Two-finger hold, thumb brace on wood
- Parent/grandparent watches at all times

**Teaching points:**
1. "The knife wants to cut — your job is to tell it where"
2. Sharp tools are safer than dull ones (no slipping)
3. Cut away from your body at all times
4. "Wood says no" — if it resists, change direction
5. Bandage the thumb once as reminder before starting

**Progression after first success:**
- Spoon carving (green basswood) — one hour per session
- Relief carving on basswood panel — 2-3 sessions
- Gift project: carved spatula or cheese board

## Scenario 5: Working with Difficult Wood (Spalted Maple)

**Context:** A turner gave you a piece of spalted maple with beautiful dark lines. You want to carve a small decorative bowl.

**Challenge:**
- Spalting = fungal decomposition. Wood is partially degraded.
- Hard and soft zones alternate — inconsistent carving resistance
- Risk of crumbling in soft zones
- Beautiful color variation but structurally weaker

**Solution:**
1. Stabilize with thin CA (cyanoacrylate) glue before carving
2. Use sharp tools with light cuts — don't force through hard zones
3. Carve from hard zones toward soft zones, not vice versa
4. Work quickly — once soft zones dry, they crumble
5. Apply thin shellac immediately after carving to harden surface
6. Finish with oil-based finish to enhance spalting contrast

**What to avoid:**
- High relief carving (too fragile)
- Deep undercutting
- Long thin projections
- Outdoor use

## Scenario 6: Large-Scale Commission Negotiation

**Context:** Restaurant wants 6 carved wooden panels, 24×36 inch each, depicting local wildlife. Client budget: $2,000. Your estimate: $6,000 for 3 months of work.

**Negotiation approach:**
- Break down: $500 material + $5,500 labor (110 hours at $50/hr)
- Client may see "wood and tools" costing little, not understand craftsmanship

**Educating the client:**
1. Show work-in-progress photos from previous similar commissions
2. Explain: "This price reflects 100+ hours of skilled hand work"
3. Offer options:
   - Full commission: $6,000, 3-month delivery
   - Reduced scope: 4 panels, $4,500, 8 weeks
   - Simplified design: $3,500, fewer detailed elements
4. Ask about priority: "Which 4 animals matter most?"
5. Include finishing and installation in price

**Contract essentials:**
- 50% deposit before starting
- Progress photos every 2 weeks
- Written approval before finishing
- Delivery/installation date
- Revision policy (one round included, additional billed at hourly rate)
