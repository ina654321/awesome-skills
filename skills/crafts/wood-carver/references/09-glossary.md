# Wood Carving — Glossary

## Tools & Equipment

**Gouge** — Curved chisel with a swept cutting edge. Used for carving concave shapes, bowls, and curved surfaces. Classified by sweep number (#1=flat to #11=deep bowl).

**V-Tool (Veiner)** — Carving tool with a V-shaped cutting edge. Used for fine lines, separating elements, lettering, and creating deep narrow channels.

**Skew Chisel** — Flat chisel with the blade angled 45° from the handle axis. Excellent for smooth, shearing cuts on end grain and flat surfaces.

**Spoon Gouge** — Deep U-shaped gouge used for carving deep concavities, bowl forms, and organic flowing shapes.

**Parting Tool** — Narrow V-tool used for separating elements and creating fine lines. Also used to clean out tight corners.

**Mallet** — Striking tool for driving chisels. Types: wooden (soft maple) for controlled strikes, synthetic (plastic/rubber) for heavy removal.

**Rasp** — Coarse file with raised teeth for rapid material removal. Used for rough shaping before chisel work.

**Card Scraper** — Thin steel sheet with burred edge for smoothing surfaces without leaving scratch marks.

**Diamond Sharpener** — abrasive tool (diamond-impregnated plate) for flattening and initial sharpening of carving tools.

**Leather Strop** — Leather surface charged with polishing compound for honing carving tools to razor sharpness.

**Slip Stone** — Small oilstone shaped to sharpen inside curves of gouges.

**Pillar Drill / Hand Drill** — Drilling equipment for creating holes in wood for inlay, dowel joints, or decorative work.

## Wood Species & Properties

**Basswood (Tilia americana)** — Softest carving wood (410 Janka). Fine grain, minimal odor. Ideal for beginners and detailed carving. Cream-white color.

**Butternut (Juglans cinerea)** — Soft (490 Janka), warm brown color with attractive grain. Easy to carve, takes detail well.

**Cherry (Prunus serotina)** — Medium hardness (950 Janka). Fine grain, ages to rich reddish-brown. Moderate difficulty, excellent for furniture.

**Walnut (Juglans nigra)** — Medium hardness (1010 Janka). Rich dark brown, strong grain. Popular for sculptural work.

**White Oak (Quercus alba)** — Hard (1360 Janka). Distinctive ray figure. Durable, suitable for outdoor work and architectural carving.

**Mahogany (Swietenia)** — Medium hardness (800-900 Janka). Straight grain, reddish-brown. Excellent for fine furniture carving.

**Lime/Linden (Tilia europaea)** — European basswood equivalent. Preferred wood for Chinese and European classical carving traditions.

**Green Wood** — Freshly cut wood with high moisture content. Used for relief carving and hollowing; easier to carve but requires drying afterward.

## Carving Techniques

**Relief Carving** — Carving in which the design projects from a flat background plane. Types: incised (shallow), bas-relief (low), high relief, and pierced (through-cut).

**Chip Carving** — Geometric pattern carving using knife chips removed at controlled angles. Predates other carving forms.

**Figure Carving** — Carving of the human form, animals, or decorative naturalistic elements in three dimensions.

**Hollowing** — Removing material from the interior of a block or bowl blank to create thin walls.

**Green Woodworking** — Working with freshly cut, unseasoned wood. Easier cutting, but requires knowledge of wood movement during drying.

**Wheel Carving** — Using rotating cutting wheels for rapid removal, primarily for production decorative carving.

## Surface & Quality Terms

**Grain** — The direction, pattern, and texture of wood fibers. Determines carving behavior and finished appearance.

**End Grain** — The surface exposed when wood is cut perpendicular to the grain direction. Shows growth ring pattern.

**Side Grain (Tangential)** — Surface cut parallel to the grain but tangent to growth rings.

**Heartwood** — Dense, dark inner portion of mature tree. More stable but harder to carve.

**Sapwood** — Lighter colored outer wood layer. Softer, more prone to insect attack.

**Knot** — Imperfection where a branch meets the trunk. Creates extremely hard and irregular grain.

**Whisker** — Small fiber protruding above the surface after planing. Removed by card scraper.

**Snipe** — Thin, weak end section of a board where grain is narrow or wavy.

## Finishing Terms

**De-whisker** — Remove tiny wood fibers with card scraper before applying finish.

**Raise Grain** — Deliberately dampen wood to make fibers stand up, then sand when dry for smoother surface.

**French Polish** — Traditional finish using shellac applied with a pad. Produces high-gloss, deep finish on fine carvings.

**Oil Finish** — Penetrating oil (linseed, tung, danish) that soaks into wood. Low sheen, natural appearance.

**Shellac** — Natural resin dissolved in alcohol. Versatile sealer and finish; used under most other finishes.

**Gesso** — Priming compound (whiting + hide glue) applied to wood before painting or gilded finish.

**Patina** — Natural aging surface that develops on carved wood over time, particularly on oak and walnut.

## Anatomical Terms for Figure Carving

**Undercut** — Removing material beneath projecting elements to create shadow and definition.

**Highlighting Edge** — The sharp line between light and shadow on a carved form. Creates visual definition.

**Ground Line** — The base plane on which a carved figure or relief stands.

**Depth Perspective** — Using varying carving depths to create illusion of distance in relief work.

**Bolection** — A molding that projects from the surface and has a rounded or grooved profile.
