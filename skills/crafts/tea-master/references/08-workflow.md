# Tea Mastery — Troubleshooting

## Brewing Issues

### Green Tea Tastes Bitter Despite Low Temperature

**Symptom:** Even at 75°C, green tea is bitter and astringent.

**Diagnosis:**
```yaml
Common causes:
  1. Leaf amount too high (more leaf = more catechins)
  2. Steep time too long (even 45s can be too long)
  3. Water too hard (minerals amplify bitterness)
  4. Old tea (oxidized catechins = more bitter)

Check:
  - How much tea are you using? (Should be 3-4g/100ml)
  - Steep time? (45s max for most sencha)
  - Water type? (Tap water with chlorine amplifies bitterness)
```

**Fix:**
```yaml
Step 1: Reduce leaf amount
  - Try 2-3g per 100ml instead of 4-5g
  - Bitter compounds are dose-dependent

Step 2: Shorten steep time
  - Try 30 seconds
  - If still bitter: Try 15-20 seconds

Step 3: Check water quality
  - Use filtered spring water
  - Avoid tap water
  - Test: If it tastes bitter in the shop but not at home, water is the issue

Step 4: Tea quality
  - Old or poorly stored tea oxidizes
  - Fresh spring harvest tea is naturally sweeter
  - Try a different source
```

### Aged Pu-erh Has Musty/Moldy Smell

**Symptom:** Stored pu-erh smells musty, like mold or mildew.

**Diagnosis:**
```yaml
Possible causes:
  1. Humidity too high (>80% RH) — mold growth
  2. Poor ventilation — stagnant air
  3. Contamination from other storage items
  4. Stored in kitchen (absorbs cooking odors)

Mold vs. Storage Character:
  - Musty/moldy: Harmful, unhealthy
  - Earthy/Wet stone: Normal aged pu-erh character
  - Fishy/Sea smell: Oxidation issue
```

**Fix:**
```yaml
For mild mustiness (storage character):
  1. Air the cake in a clean, dry area for 1-2 hours
  2. The smell should dissipate if it's just storage character
  3. Return to proper storage

For mold (harmful):
  1. Do NOT consume if visible mold is present
  2. Inspect under bright light for mold on leaves
  3. If mold is visible: The cake may be ruined
  4. If only smell without visible mold:
     - Air for 24 hours in clean, dry environment
     - If smell persists, it's mold — discard

Prevention:
  - Store at 60-70% RH (not >75%)
  - Ensure air circulation
  - Keep away from kitchen odors
  - Use clean storage containers
```

### Oolong Tea Has Fishy/Seaweed Smell

**Symptom:** Oolong tea smells like fish or seaweed.

**Cause:**
```yaml
Two possible explanations:
  1. Processing residue (normal)
     - Some Taiwanese high-mountain oolongs have a 
       seaweed-like aroma from processing
     - This dissipates after first steep

  2. Contamination (problem)
     - Stored near seafood or in humid conditions
     - Absorption of external odors
```

**Fix:**
```yaml
For processing character (normal):
  1. First steep: Discard the tea (wash/awakening steep)
  2. Smell the second steep — should be normal
  3. If smell persists: It's contamination, not processing

For contamination:
  1. Air the tea in a clean, dry area for 24 hours
  2. Store in clean, dry container
  3. If odor persists: The tea may be ruined
```

## Tea Quality Issues

### Tea Has No Flavor — "Watery"

**Symptom:** Tea tastes like colored water, no body or complexity.

**Diagnosis:**
```yaml
Possible causes:
  1. Too little tea (common)
  2. Water too hot (extracts quickly but mutes flavor)
  3. Too short steep time
  4. Tea is old/exhausted
  5. Water quality (distilled = flat)
```

**Fix:**
```yaml
1. Increase leaf amount:
   - Most people under-dose tea
   - Try 5g per 100ml instead of 3g

2. Adjust temperature:
   - Try slightly higher temperature
   - Green tea: Try 80°C instead of 70°C
   - Oolong: Try 95°C instead of 90°C

3. Extend steep time:
   - Sencha: Try 60 seconds
   - Oolong: Try 45 seconds

4. Check water:
   - Add minerals back to distilled water
   - Use spring water, not distilled

5. Try fresh tea:
   - Taste fresh tea from a reputable source
   - Compare against your current tea
```

### Tea Has a Metallic or Plastic Taste

**Cause:**
```yaml
1. Glazed pottery with contaminated glaze
2. Plastic contamination from storage
3. Metal from kettle (if iron/cast iron)
4. Residue from cleaning products
```

**Fix:**
```yaml
For pottery:
  1. Boil in clean water 2-3 times
  2. If taste persists, the vessel may be unsuitable
  3. Use porcelain or glass for problematic teas

For cast iron kettles:
  1. Season the kettle: Boil water, discard, repeat 3x
  2. Use only for boiling, not brewing
  3. Never leave water standing in iron kettle

For storage:
  1. Transfer to clean, dry glass or ceramic
  2. Avoid plastic containers (can impart taste)
```

## Equipment Issues

### Yixing Pot Has Developed a "Dry" Taste

**Symptom:** Previously seasoned Yixing pot has developed an off taste.

**Cause:**
```yaml
1. Left empty and dry for too long
2. Stored near strong odors
3. Never fully dried between uses
4. Used with tea type that requires re-seasoning
```

**Fix:**
```yaml
1. Seasoning process:
   - Boil the pot in tea (weak tea, same tea type) for 1 hour
   - Let cool in the liquid
   - Remove, let dry
   - Repeat 2-3 times

2. For persistent odors:
   - Boil in water with rice or pu-erh tea leaves
   - Or use dedicated pu-erh for pu-erh pots

3. Prevention:
   - Keep pot with a damp cloth when not in use
   - Never leave completely dry
   - Store with a small piece of damp tea inside
```

### Gaiwan Too Hot to Handle

**Symptom:** Gaiwan gets too hot during brewing, causing spills or burns.

**Fix:**
```yaml
Proper technique:
  1. Hold the gaiwan by the rim only
  2. Use thumb and index finger to pinch rim
  3. Rotate wrist to pour — never touch the body

If still too hot:
  1. Use a saucer to hold the gaiwan
  2. Wrap fingers around the saucer instead
  3. Gradually build tolerance (skin adapts)
  4. Practice with just hot water first

Equipment fix:
  1. Use thinner porcelain (transfers heat faster)
  2. Use a larger gaiwan (more grip surface)
  3. Consider a lid that's easier to manipulate
```

## Tea Storage Issues

### Tea Absorbed Kitchen Odors

**Symptom:** Stored tea has absorbed cooking smells (garlic, spice, smoke).

**Fix:**
```yaml
For mild absorption:
  1. Place tea in clean, dry container with dry tea leaves (pu-erh)
  2. The pu-erh will absorb the odors over 1-2 weeks
  3. Check and replace if fully absorbed

For severe absorption:
  1. The tea is likely ruined
  2. Very difficult to fully remove absorbed odors
  3. Use for cooking rather than drinking
  4. Future: Store in odor-proof containers

Prevention:
  1. Never store tea near cooking areas
  2. Use sealed containers
  3. Use separate storage for aromatic teas (oolong, pu-erh)
```

### Green Tea Lost Color and Flavor in Refrigerator

**Symptom:** Stored green tea in refrigerator has faded and lost sweetness.

**Cause:**
```yaml
Refrigerator storage issues:
  1. Moisture: Fridges have humidity
  2. Odor: Tea absorbs all fridge smells
  3. Condensation: Each opening causes moisture exposure
```

**Fix:**
```yaml
Proper cold storage:
  1. Use completely airtight container
  2. Double-bag with ziplock (remove all air)
  3. Bring to room temperature before opening (prevents condensation)
  4. Wait 2 hours before opening — opening cold = condensation

Best practice:
  - Green tea for short-term (1-3 months): Room temperature is fine
  - Long-term (6+ months): Cold storage with airtight sealing
  - Always: Cool, dry, dark location
```

## Tea Ceremony Issues

### Gongfu Cha — Tea Leaves Won't Unfurl

**Symptom:** During multiple steepings, tea leaves remain tightly compressed and don't unfurl.

**Cause:**
```yaml
1. Pressed too tightly (cake, brick)
2. Very old tea (re-hydration takes time)
3. Water temperature too low
```

**Fix:**
```yaml
For tightly pressed tea:
  1. Use higher temperature water
  2. First steep: Longer wash (10-20 seconds)
  3. Second steep: Let sit 30 seconds before pouring
  4. Use a pin to gently open the cake before brewing

For aged/old tea:
  1. Wake up the tea: Wash 2-3 times with hot water
  2. Let tea rest in hot water for 1-2 minutes before first steep
  3. Patience: Some pu-erh takes 5+ steeps to fully open

For very old pu-erh (20+ years):
  1. Extended wake-up (wash 3-5 times)
  2. Very long first steep (30-60 seconds)
  3. The tea may never fully unfurl — it's normal
```

### Tea Stains on Porcelain or Clay

**Symptom:** Teacups and pots have developed brown/tea stains.

**Fix:**
```yaml
For porcelain:
  1. Baking soda paste: Mix baking soda with water, scrub
  2. Denture cleaning tablet: Soak in warm water with tablet
  3. Lemon juice: Soak to remove stains

For Yixing/clay:
  1. DO NOT clean with anything — stains are part of seasoning
  2. If stains are mold (green/blue): Boil in water
  3. If stains affect taste: Season again with tea
  4. Never use soap on Yixing — it will strip the patina

Prevention:
  - Rinse cups immediately after use
  - Don't leave tea sitting in cups
  - Use dedicated cup for each tea type
```
