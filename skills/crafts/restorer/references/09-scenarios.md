# Art Conservation & Restoration — Glossary

## Core Conservation Terms

**Conservation (保存修復)** — The profession dedicated to preserving cultural heritage. Combines art history, science, and craft. Goal: stabilize and preserve.

**Restoration (修復)** — The act of returning an object to a known or assumed state. Includes inpainting, cleaning, and repair.

**Minimum Intervention** — The guiding principle that treatment should do the minimum necessary to stabilize and preserve. Don't fix what isn't broken.

**Reversibility** — The principle that all treatments should be undoable without damaging the original. All conservation materials should be removable.

**Distinguishability** — The standard that fills and inpainting should be visually detectable under raking light or magnification.

**Patina** — The surface character that develops over time. Includes surface accretions, oxidation, and wear. Should generally be preserved.

**Provenance** — The documented history of an object's ownership. Critical for authentication and cultural heritage.

## Damage & Deterioration

**Craquelure** — Network of fine cracks in paint layer. Caused by aging, drying, or impact. Stable craquelure is acceptable to leave.

**Foxing** — Small brown spots on paper. Caused by iron in the paper or mold activity.

**Cockling** — Waviness in paper caused by uneven expansion/contraction from humidity changes.

**Delamination** — Separation of layers (canvas from ground, paint from ground, veneer from substrate).

**Pitting** — Small depressions in surface from impact, corrosion, or biological activity.

**Efflorescence** — White crystalline deposits on surface (usually salts). Can indicate underlying moisture.

**Chalking** — Surface degradation where paint turns to powder. Caused by UV exposure or chemical degradation.

**Crazing** — Fine network of cracks in varnish or glaze. Caused by stress from different expansion rates.

**Flooding** — Water damage causing swelling, staining, or mold growth.

**Mold / Mildew** — Biological growth on organic materials in humid conditions. Hazardous to health and causes permanent staining.

## Treatment Materials

**Paraloid B-72** — Thermoplastic resin. The gold standard conservation adhesive. Reversible with acetone/ethanol years later.

**Klucel G** — Hydroxypropyl cellulose. Used for sizing paper and as a consolidant. Water or alcohol soluble.

**Wheat Starch Paste** — Traditional archival adhesive. Made from wheat starch and water. Reversible with water.

**BEVA 371** — Ethylene vinyl acetate film adhesive. Heat-set, reversible with mineral spirits.

**Isinglass** — Fish bladder collagen. Traditional adhesive, very strong, water-soluble. Used in painting consolidation.

**Japanese Tissue** — Long-fiber tissue (Kozo/Mitsumata). Acid-free, archival backing material.

**Glass Fiber Screen** — Inert, strong support for fragile paper. Does not yellow.

**Silica Gel** — Desiccant for humidity control in storage. Indicating gel changes color when saturated.

## Cleaning Methods

**Dry Cleaning** — Using erasers, smoke sponge, or grated eraser to remove surface dirt without liquid.

**Wet Cleaning / Aqueous Cleaning** — Using water or buffered solutions to remove acidity, dirt, or soluble degradation products.

**Solvent Cleaning** — Using organic solvents (acetone, ethanol, white spirit) to remove dirt, wax, or varnish.

**Mechanical Cleaning** — Using scalpel, bamboo skewer, or other tools to remove debris without chemicals.

**Laser Cleaning** — Using focused laser light to ablate surface contamination. Precise but expensive.

**Ion Exchange Resin** — Aqueous method using ion exchange to remove acid from paper without water immersion.

**Misting** — Applying water as fine mist for controlled humidification. For relaxing paper fibers.

## Examination & Analysis

**Raking Light** — Light angled across surface to reveal surface topography, craquelure, and texture.

**Normal Light** — Direct overhead light for overall color and condition assessment.

**UV Fluorescence** — UV light causing materials to fluoresce differently. Reveals overpainting, restorations, and varnish age.

**IR Reflectography** — Infrared light penetrating paint layers to reveal underdrawings.

**X-Radiography** — X-rays passing through object to reveal construction, metalwork, pentimenti.

**Cross-Section** — A tiny sample cut from the edge to examine layer structure under microscope.

**pH Testing** — Using pH pens or spots to measure acidity of paper. pH < 6.5 = acidic.

## Structural Treatment

**Facing** — Applying a temporary adhesive + tissue to the paint surface for protection during structural treatment.

**Lining** — Attaching a new support to the back of a painting canvas. Traditional (hot paste) or modern (BEVA).

**Mounting** — Attaching paper artwork to a secondary support.

**Deacidification** — Treating acidic paper with alkaline solution (calcium hydroxide, magnesium bicarbonate) to neutralize acid.

**Humidification** — Introducing moisture to relax paper fibers for flattening.

**Pressing** — Placing paper between blotter and board under weight to flatten.

**Consolidation** — Bonding flaking or lifting paint/veneer/paper back to its support.

## Inpainting & Fill

**Inpainting** — Filling losses in paint layer with reversible conservation-grade pigments.

**Retouching** — The traditional (now deprecated) term for inpainting. Modern conservators use "inpainting."

**Fills** — Replacing missing areas of canvas, paper, or wood with matching material.

**Mutilation** — Areas of loss that are NOT filled (left as voids) to preserve evidence.

**Casting** — Making a mold of a loss and casting replacement material (often polyester resin or plaster).

## Environment & Storage

**RH (Relative Humidity)** — The percentage of moisture air can hold at a given temperature. Primary control factor.

**Lux** — Unit of illuminance. Lux < 150 for light-sensitive materials.

**Cold Storage** — Storage at low temperature (below 4°C) for highly vulnerable materials (color photographs, acetate film).

**Anoxic Storage** — Storage in oxygen-depleted environment to prevent oxidation and pest damage.

**Microclimate** — A small enclosed environment (sealed box) with controlled humidity using silica gel.

**Archive / Archival** — Materials or conditions suitable for long-term preservation. pH neutral, acid-free, stable.

## Frames & Housings

**Window Mat** — Cardboard with window cutout to isolate artwork from glass. Minimum 4-ply.

**Spacer / Mat-to-Glass Gap** — Space between artwork surface and glass to prevent contact damage.

**T-hinge** — Hinge made from Japanese tissue and wheat starch paste for attaching artwork to mount.

**Phase Box** — Inexpensive archival housing made from corrugated board and cloth.

**Hollinger Box** — Metal-edge archival box. Standard for museum collections.

**Mylar Sleeve** — Plastic sleeve for storing photographs. Must be polyester (Mylar), not PVC.
