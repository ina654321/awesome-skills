# Art Conservation & Restoration — Troubleshooting

## Surface Cleaning Issues

### Smoke Damaged Artwork — Inappropriate Cleaning Method

**Symptom:** Smoke-damaged paper artwork cleaned with water, causing bleeding of inks.

**Diagnosis:**
```yaml
Why water failed:
  - Iron gall ink (common in manuscripts) is water-soluble
  - Watercolor pigments can bleed
  - Fountain pen inks vary by formulation
  - Wet cleaning mobilized soot into paper fibers

Alternative approach:
  - Dry cleaning first: smoke sponge or eraser crumbs
  - Test any wet method on corner sample first
  - For iron gall ink: Buffered alkaline wash (calcium hydroxide) may stabilize
```

**Fix:**
```yaml
For iron gall ink manuscripts:
  1. Dry clean: Smoke sponge or grated eraser
  2. Test wet cleaning: Apply water to corner with brush
  3. If no bleeding: Very gentle wet cleaning with deionized water
  4. If bleeding: Leave dry; document dry-only condition
  5. pH testing: Check if alkaline wash needed (iron gall degrades paper)

For water-based inks that bled:
  1. Assess extent of bleeding — may be permanent
  2. Consolidation may be needed: Apply Klucel G in ethanol
  3. Document condition before and after
```

### Varnish Removal — Fails to Dissolve

**Symptom:** Tried to remove varnish with solvent, but varnish remains intact.

**Diagnosis:**
```yaml
Possible causes:
  1. Wrong solvent for varnish type
  2. Varnish has aged beyond solubility
  3. Confused varnish with original surface

Varnish identification:
  - Dammar: Soluble in turpentine, aliphatic hydrocarbon
  - MS2A (synthetic): Soluble in white spirit
  - Polyvinyl butyral: Soluble in ethanol
  - Acrylic (Plextol): Soluble in acetone, xylene
  - Wax: Soluble in petroleum benzine

Pre-test: Apply small amount of solvent to cotton swab
  - Test on varnish under painting edge
  - If varnish swells/dissolves in 30 seconds: correct solvent
  - If no reaction: try different solvent class
```

**Fix:**
```yaml
For aged dammar that won't dissolve:
  1. Apply thick paste (ARRem® or cellulose ether gel) with solvent
  2. Cover with plastic film to prevent evaporation
  3. Leave for 15-30 minutes
  4. Remove with cotton swab
  5. Repeat as needed

For misidentified material:
  1. Stop immediately
  2. Consult conservation scientist
  3. Run FTIR analysis to identify material
  4. Proceed only after identification
```

## Structural Repair Issues

### Painting Canvas — Bulging and Detachment

**Symptom:** Canvas has detached from strainer and is bulging forward. Paint layer is at risk.

**Fix:**
```yaml
For minor bulging:
  1. Increase stretcher tension: Tap corners with mallet
  2. If no strainer: Tacking edge restoration

For significant bulging:
  1. Remove painting from stretcher
  2. Place face-down on clean blotter
  3. Humidify back of canvas locally (damp chamber, not direct water)
  4. Apply gentle pressure through blotter to relax fibers
  5. Allow to dry flat under weights
  6. Re-stretch on new or cleaned stretcher

For paint layer at risk:
  1. DO NOT re-stretch without consolidation
  2. Apply facing tissue (Beva film) to paint layer
  3. Let facing cure
  4. Re-stretch canvas
  5. Remove facing with heat spatula
```

### Glass Cracking in Framed Work

**Symptom:** Glass in frame has cracked, potentially damaging artwork behind it.

**Fix:**
```yaml
Immediate:
  1. Remove frame carefully
  2. Check artwork for glass contact damage
  3. Document any debris from breakage

Replacement:
  1. Measure glass precisely
  2. Specify: Museum glass (UV-filtering), standard glass, or acrylic
  3. Cut mat-to-glass gap (8-10mm minimum)
  4. Use spacers (foam or ragmat) to prevent future contact
  5. Seal back of frame with paper tape to prevent dust entry
```

## Mold & Pest Issues

### Active Mold Growth

**Symptom:** Mold actively growing on artwork (fuzzy, discolored patches, musty odor).

**Fix:**
```yaml
CRITICAL: Active mold is hazardous — wear N95 respirator, gloves, eye protection

Immediate:
  1. Isolate: Place in sealed plastic bag or chamber
  2. Do NOT clean wet
  3. Freeze if possible (-30°C) to halt growth

Assessment:
  1. Assess extent: Surface only or deep penetration?
  2. For paper: Mold usually surface
  3. For wood: May be deep
  4. Run sampling for species identification if needed

Cleaning (after isolating):
  1. Vacuum with HEPA filter attachment (gentle)
  2. Wipe with 1% thymol in ethanol (fungicide)
  3. Apply locally with cotton swab, not soaking
  4. Let dry slowly in clean environment
  5. Freeze-dehydrate for valuable pieces

Prevention:
  - Maintain RH below 60%
  - Ensure air circulation
  - Monitor with mold indicators
```

### Insect Damage (Active or Past)

**Symptom:** Exit holes, tunnels, or powdery residue on wood or paper objects.

**Fix:**
```yaml
For past damage (no active insects):
  1. Document extent of damage with photographs and measurements
  2. Consolidate damaged areas: Paraloid B-72 in acetone
  3. Fill exit holes if structurally needed: Matching material + adhesive
  4. Seal object to prevent re-infestation

For suspected active infestation:
  1. FREEZE: -30°C for 72 hours or -20°C for 1 week (kill all life stages)
  2. Double-bag in plastic before freezing
  3. Thaw slowly to prevent condensation
  4. Assess damage after thawing
  5. Fumigation (with conservation-grade fumigant) only if necessary

Prevention:
  - Regular inspection
  - Maintain RH below 60%
  - Quarantine new acquisitions
  - Monitor with sticky traps
```

## Documentation & Reporting Issues

### Incomplete Condition Report

**Symptom:** Condition report omits critical damage or misidentifies materials.

**Fix:**
```yaml
Minimum required for detailed report:
  - Dimensions (height × width × depth, in cm and inches)
  - Media and support (be specific: "oil on canvas", not just "painting")
  - Signature/inscription location and legibility
  - Overall condition: Good / Fair / Poor / Critical
  - Specific damage with precise location (use zone system or grid)
  - Cause of damage when known
  - Previous treatments (if known)
  - Storage/display recommendations

Zone system:
  Divide work into quadrants or grid (A1, A2, B1, B2, etc.)
  Reference damage: "Crease in B2 quadrant, upper right"
```

## Ethical Dilemmas

### Client Requests Over-Restoration

**Symptom:** Client wants complete revarnishing and repainting of areas to "make the painting look new."

**Fix:**
```yaml
Step 1: Explain AIC Code of Ethics
  - Minimum intervention principle
  - Preservation of original material
  - Distinguishability of fills and inpainting

Step 2: Explain what was lost
  - Original patina cannot be recreated
  - Overpainting destroys evidence of age and authenticity
  - Future conservators will be unable to see original surface

Step 3: Offer legitimate options:
  1. Surface cleaning only
  2. Varnish removal + revarnishing (if original varnish yellowed)
  3. Inpainting of losses (with reversible materials, distinguishable)
  4. Structural consolidation only

Step 4: Document refusal
  - Written statement that you recommended against over-restoration
  - Note in treatment proposal
  - Client's informed consent if proceeding against recommendation
```

## Environmental Emergency Response

### Flood-Damaged Artwork

**Symptom:** Artwork has been exposed to flood water (potentially contaminated).

**Fix:**
```yaml
CRITICAL: Flood water is contaminated — wear full PPE

Immediate Response (within 48 hours):
  1. Do NOT attempt to dry rapidly (causes cockling, warping, damage)
  2. Remove from water immediately
  3. Rinse with clean water if contaminated (mud, sewage)
  4. For paper: Place between clean blotter, replace frequently
  5. For paintings: Remove from wet stretcher, place face-up on blotter

Slow Drying:
  - Air dry slowly in clean, controlled environment (45-55% RH, 18-21°C)
  - Place blotter changes 2-3 times daily initially
  - Allow 2-4 weeks for complete drying
  - Flatten under light weight as drying

For waterlogged paintings:
  1. Remove stretcher (wet wood warps)
  2. Sandwich between cotton and polyester wadding
  3. Roll loose (not tight) for transport
  4. Transport to conservation lab face-up
  5. DO NOT roll tightly or roll face-down

Contamination:
  - Flood from sewage: Document, advise client of health risk
  - Mold will develop if not properly dried within 48-72 hours
```
