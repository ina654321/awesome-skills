# Art Conservation & Restoration — Real-World Scenarios

## Scenario 1: 19th-Century Oil Painting — Yellowed Varnish and Craquelure

**Context:** A family heirloom oil painting (c. 1880, 24×30") has yellowed varnish and craquelure. The family wants it "cleaned up and looking better."

**Assessment:**
```yaml
Condition Survey:
  Varnish: Yellowed, slightly dirty surface
  Paint: Stable craquelure (no flaking or cupping)
  Ground: Intact
  Canvas: Original, slightly loose from stretcher
  Frame: Victorian gilt frame, worn gilding

Key question: Is this a valuable painting or sentimental family piece?
```

**Treatment Proposal:**
```yaml
Phase 1: Documentation
  - Photograph: Overall, raking light, UV fluorescence
  - UV reveals: Original varnish layer, no overpainting
  - Condition report with damage mapping

Phase 2: Surface Cleaning
  - Dry clean: Smoke sponge to remove surface dirt
  - Wet clean: Test small area with saliva/water
  - Varnish removal: Gel solvent (agar or cellulose ether with white spirit)
  - Reveals: Original colors significantly brighter

Phase 3: Structural
  - Re-tension canvas on new stretcher (if needed)
  - Check for any paint instability
  - Consolidate any lifting areas with dilute Beva

Phase 4: Compensation
  - Inpaint losses only (not entire painting)
  - Use conservation-grade pigments in Beva 371
  - Distinguishable under raking light
  - Document all inpainting locations

Phase 5: Varnishing
  - Synthetic varnish (MS2A or similar)
  - Provide UV protection and saturation

NOT to do:
  - "Restore" the entire painting (overpainting)
  - Remove stable craquelure (part of the work's history)
  - Apply heavy overpaint to even the surface
```

## Scenario 2: Water-Damaged Architectural Drawing

**Context:** A set of 12 architectural drawings (charcoal on trace, 1950s) was stored under a leaking pipe. Papers are cockled, with some surface dirt and minor tears.

**Assessment:**
```yaml
Condition:
  - 8 drawings: Minor cockling, surface dirt, small tears
  - 3 drawings: Moderate cockling, tears, smudging
  - 1 drawing: Severe cockling, tear across drawing, heavy soiling

Type: Charcoal on tracing paper (very fragile)
  - Charcoal is water-sensitive
  - Tracing paper is thin and easily torn
  - Wetting could fix the charcoal to other surfaces
```

**Treatment:**
```yaml
For the 8 minor drawings:
  1. Dry clean: Kneaded eraser or art gum
  2. Humidify: Mist chamber (not direct water on charcoal)
  3. Flatten between blotters under weights
  4. Repair tears: Japanese tissue + wheat starch paste
  5. Dry flat

For the 3 moderate drawings:
  1. Dry clean first
  2. Local humidification with suction table
  3. Flatten under weights
  4. Tear repair: Japanese tissue + wheat starch paste
  5. Support: Mount on conservation-grade board

For the 1 severe drawing:
  1. Do NOT wet (charcoal will smudge)
  2. Dry clean gently
  3. Flatten under weights only
  4. Large tear repair: Japanese tissue on verso
  5. Consolidate charcoal if loose: Klucel G in ethanol
  6. Document that wet treatment not possible

Storage:
  - Flat storage in archival folders
  - Interleave with glassine
  - Humidity-controlled cabinet
```

## Scenario 3: Bronze Sculpture — Active Bronze Disease

**Context:** A small outdoor bronze sculpture (c. 1920) shows active green powdery deposits. This indicates "bronze disease" — a ongoing corrosion problem.

**Assessment:**
```yaml
Bronze Disease:
  - Caused by: Chlorides in the metal, activated by moisture
  - Appearance: Light green powder, crystalline growths
  - Problem: If untreated, continues to corrode, eventually destroying form

Visual examination:
  - Powdery green deposits on lower surfaces (water pooling areas)
  - Slight pitting of surface
  - Previous wax coating partially intact

Diagnosis:
  - Active bronze disease confirmed
  - Condition: Progressive
```

**Treatment:**
```yaml
Phase 1: Mechanical Cleaning
  1. Wear gloves (skin oils accelerate corrosion)
  2. Mechanical removal: Wooden sticks, brushes
  3. Remove all loose powder carefully
  4. Do NOT use water (activates chlorides)

Phase 2: Chemical Treatment
  1. Apply sodium sesquicarbonate paste (or commercial bronze disease treatment)
  2. Cover with plastic to prevent drying
  3. Leave for 24-48 hours
  4. Rinse with deionized water
  5. Repeat if crystals reappear

Phase 3: Passivation
  1. Apply benzotriazole (BTA) inhibitor solution
  2. Prevents future chloride activation
  3. Allow to dry

Phase 4: Protection
  1. Microcrystalline wax (Renaissance Wax)
  2. Apply thin coat with cloth
  3. buff to shine
  4. Recommend: Indoor storage or climate-controlled display

Prevention:
  - Keep dry
  - Monitor quarterly for recurrence
  - Bronze disease can recur if chlorides are deep in metal
```

## Scenario 4: Silver Photo Print — Silver Mirroring

**Context:** An early 20th-century gelatin silver print (8×10") shows yellow/brown discoloration and mirror-like areas on highlights.

**Assessment:**
```yaml
Silver Mirroring:
  - Caused by: Oxidation of silver in highlights
  - Appearance: Brownish patches, mirror-like reflections
  - Cause: Improper storage (contact with glass, paper, or PVC)

Severity:
  - Surface mirroring: Reversible
  - Deep oxidation: May not be fully reversible
```

**Treatment:**
```yaml
If surface mirroring (reversible):
  1. Test with cotton swab: If tarnish transfers, treatment possible
  2. Apply photographic reducer or silver dip (formula from Kodak)
  3. Time-critical: Observe under magnification
  4. Stop reaction in water bath
  5. Wash thoroughly (30 minutes in running water)
  6. Photograph before and after

If mirroring is fixed (not transferable):
  1. Leave as-is (treatment would damage image)
  2. Document extent
  3. Prevent further degradation:
     - Remove from acidic mat board
     - Store in archival sleeve
     - Keep in dark, cool, dry conditions

Prevention:
  - Never use PVC plastic (releases hydrochloric acid)
  - Use archival mats and boxes
  - Keep at 30-40% RH
  - Keep at 18°C or below
```

## Scenario 5: Textile — Moth Damage on Silk

**Context:** A 19th-century silk sampler has holes where moths have eaten the fabric. The family wants the holes filled to "make it look whole."

**Assessment:**
```yaml
Condition:
  - Overall: Stable, fragile silk
  - Damage: Multiple holes (1-5mm) across surface
  - Pattern: Part of the design
  - Soiling: Surface dirt, slight discoloration

Ethical Decision:
  - Holes are part of the sampler's history
  - Filling holes would alter the artifact
  - A sampler showing its age is more valuable than a "restored" one
```

**Treatment:**
```yaml
NOT Recommended:
  - Filling holes with matching silk (alters artifact)
  - Dyeing to even color (irreversible)
  - Applying backing to cover holes (hides evidence)

Recommended:
  1. Surface cleaning: Vacuum with screen
  2. Deacidify if needed (calcium hydroxide)
  3. Stabilize loose threads
  4. Mount on conservation board (not adhered, pinned or tacked)
  5. Frame with glass and spacer (prevents handling damage)
  6. Display guidelines: No direct sunlight, controlled humidity

If filling is essential (client insists):
  - Use dyed silk patch behind, visible from back only
  - DO NOT fill holes in the silk itself
  - Document that filling was done at client's request
```

## Scenario 6: Ceramic Bowl — Broken and Incomplete

**Context:** A Ming Dynasty blue-and-white porcelain bowl (15th century) was broken into 8 pieces, with one piece missing. The bowl is valued at $25,000.

**Assessment:**
```yaml
Condition:
  - 8 fragments present
  - 1 fragment missing (10% loss)
  - All fragments are stable, no active deterioration

Value consideration:
  - Provenance: Family collection, documentation
  - Condition: Good considering age and damage
  - Restoration adds value IF done properly
  - Bad restoration (epoxy, overpainting) destroys value
```

**Treatment:**
```yaml
Ethical approach for museum/high-value objects:

Phase 1: Analysis
  - XRF to confirm material composition
  - Assess glazes and pigments
  - Check for any existing repairs

Phase 2: Joining
  1. Clean fragments: Distilled water only (no detergent)
  2. Dry completely
  3. Test fit without adhesive
  4. Adhesive: Paraloid B-72 in acetone (reversible, conservation-grade)
  5. Apply with fine brush to edges
  6. Clamp carefully (pressure can crack porcelain)

Phase 3: Missing Area
  1. DO NOT fill the missing area
  2. Document loss
  3. If client insists on filling: Use plaster fill, removable
  4. Any fill must be distinguishable (matte vs. original glaze)

Phase 4: Presentation
  1. Store fragments in padded archival box
  2. If displaying: Custom mount supporting all pieces
  3. Display unjoined (shows original condition)

NOT to do:
  - Epoxy (irreversible, yellows)
  - HMG (similar problems)
  - Fill missing areas with ceramic compound (alters authenticity)
  - Overpaint to make seamless
```

## Scenario 7: Map — Acid Migration from Backing

**Context:** A 1780 map has been mounted on acidic cardboard. The map shows browning and the acidic board has migrated damage.

**Assessment:**
```yaml
Condition:
  - Map: Acid-sensitive paper (iron gall ink visible)
  - Backing: Acidic cardboard (pH 4.5)
  - Damage: Browning concentrated near center (from board contact)
  - Ink: Iron gall (candle/quinine test)

Diagnosis:
  - Acid migration confirmed
  - Iron gall ink may be vulnerable to wet treatment
```

**Treatment:**
```yaml
Phase 1: Deacidification
  1. Test ink solubility: Touch corner with damp cotton swab
  2. If ink stable: Aqueous deacidification (calcium bicarbonate)
  3. If ink unstable: Non-aqueous deacidification (magnesium bicarbonate in methanol)

Phase 2: Backing Removal
  1. Humidify entire map uniformly
  2. Slowly peel cardboard away
  3. If stuck: Use spatula or bone folder to separate
  4. May require multiple sessions

Phase 3: Washing
  1. If ink tolerates: Wash in deionized water
  2. If not: Spray deacidification only
  3. Monitor throughout

Phase 4: New Support
  1. Options: Japanese tissue, spun-bonded polyester
  2. Attach with wheat starch paste (or Beva film if heat is safe)
  3. Let dry under weights

Phase 5: Storage
  - Flat storage in archival folder
  - Interleave with glassine
  - Monitor pH regularly
```
