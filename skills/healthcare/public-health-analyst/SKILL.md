### § 1.3 · Thinking Patterns

**Pattern 1: Population Perspective**

```
Focus on groups, not individuals:
├── Rates, not counts (account for population size)
├── Stratification: By age, race, geography
├── Trends over time: Secular changes, seasonality
├── Comparisons: Benchmarks, peer communities
└── Attribution: What explains differences?

Population health is more than the sum of individual health.
```

**Pattern 2: Social Ecological Model**

```
Health is determined at multiple levels:
├── Individual: Behaviors, genetics
├── Interpersonal: Family, social networks
├── Organizational: Workplaces, schools
├── Community: Neighborhood resources, norms
└── Policy: Laws, regulations, systems

Interventions must address multiple levels.
```

**Pattern 3: Health Equity Lens**

```
Examine all analyses for disparities:
├── Stratify by race/ethnicity, income, geography
├── Calculate disparity metrics (rate ratios)
├── Identify modifiable determinants
├── Prioritize vulnerable populations
└── Monitor equity alongside overall trends

Equity is not equality; it's justice in health.
```

**Pattern 4: Evidence-Based Decision Making**

```
Ground recommendations in science:
├── Best available evidence
├── Local context and data
├── Stakeholder input
├── Implementation feasibility
└── Evaluation plan

Good data + good analysis = good decisions.
```

## § 6 · Domain Knowledge

### Outbreak Investigation Steps

```
1. Prepare for Field Work
   - Review literature
   - Establish team
   - Prepare materials

2. Verify Diagnosis
   - Confirm cases
   - Rule out lab error

3. Establish Case Definition
   - Confirmed, probable, possible
   - Clinical and lab criteria

4. Find Cases Systematically
   - Active case finding
   - Line listing

5. Perform Descriptive Epidemiology
   - Person, place, time
   - Epidemic curve

6. Develop Hypotheses
   - Source, mode of transmission

7. Evaluate Hypotheses
   - Case-control or cohort study
   - Statistical testing

8. Refine Hypotheses and Execute Studies
   - Environmental sampling
   - Special studies

9. Implement Control Measures
   - Concurrent with investigation
   - Source control, exposure reduction

10. Communicate Findings
    - Reports, publications
    - Recommendations
```

| **Outputs** | Direct products | # educated, # screened |
| **Outcomes** | Short-term changes | Knowledge increase, behavior change |
| **Impact** | Long-term results | Disease reduction, health improvement |

## § 7 · Scenario Examples

### Scenario 1: Outbreak Investigation

**Context**: 15 cases of Salmonella in 2 weeks; normal is 2-3 per month.

**Investigation**:
1. **Line Listing**: Demographics, onset dates, symptoms, exposures
2. **Descriptive Epidemiology**:
   - Person: Median age 35, 60% female
   - Place: Clustered in north county
   - Time: Epidemic curve suggests point source
3. **Case-Control Study**:
   - Cases: 15 confirmed
   - Controls: 30 matched by age/sex
   - Exposure assessment: Food history
4. **Findings**: Strong association with Restaurant A (OR = 12.5, 95% CI 3.2-48.6)
5. **Environmental Health**: Inspection found improper food holding temperatures
6. **Control**: Restaurant closed temporarily, staff retrained

**Report**: CDC, state health department, local media

---

### Scenario 2: Community Health Needs Assessment

**Context**: Hospital required to conduct CHNA every 3 years.

**Process**:
1. **Data Collection**:
   - Secondary data: Vital statistics, BRFSS, census
   - Primary data: Community surveys, focus groups
   - Key informant interviews

2. **Analysis**:
   - Health status indicators
   - Disparities by race/ethnicity, geography
   - Comparison to state/national benchmarks

3. **Priority Setting**:
   - Criteria: Magnitude, severity, disparity, feasibility
   - Top priorities: Diabetes, mental health, access to care

4. **Implementation Strategy**:
   - Community partnerships
   - Evidence-based interventions
   - Resource allocation

5. **Evaluation Plan**:
   - Metrics for each priority
   - Annual monitoring

**Output**: Published CHNA report, implementation strategy

### Scenario 3: Program Evaluation

**Context**: Evaluate smoking cessation program effectiveness.

**Design**: Quasi-experimental (pre-post with comparison group)

**Methods**:
1. **Participants**: Program enrollees (n=200) vs. waitlist controls (n=100)
2. **Data Collection**:
   - Baseline: Demographics, smoking history
   - 6-month: Self-reported quit status
   - 12-month: Biochemical verification (cotinine)

3. **Analysis**:
   - Quit rates: Program 25% vs. Control 8% (p < 0.001)
   - Adjusted for demographics, addiction severity
   - Cost-effectiveness: $1,200 per quitter

4. **Conclusions**:
   - Program effective; exceeds national benchmarks
   - Recommend continued funding
   - Suggest enhancements: Medication access, text support

---

### Scenario 4: Health Policy Analysis

**Context**: Analyze impact of proposed soda tax on obesity rates.

**Analysis**:
1. **Evidence Review**:
   - Berkeley, Mexico, UK experiences
   - Price elasticity studies
   - Consumption modeling

2. **Local Data**:
   - Current consumption patterns
   - Demographics
   - Revenue projections

3. **Modeling**:
   - 20% price increase → 10-15% consumption decrease
   - Calorie reduction: 50 calories/day average
   - Projected BMI change: -0.5 kg/m² over 5 years

4. **Equity Analysis**:
   - Regressive tax burden
   - Revenue for health programs
   - Targeted benefits for low-income communities

5. **Recommendations**:
   - Support tax with caveats
   - Earmark revenue for obesity prevention in low-income areas

### Scenario 5: Surveillance Dashboard Development

**Context**: Create real-time opioid overdose surveillance dashboard.

**Data Sources**:
- EMS runs (naloxone administration)
- ED syndromic surveillance

- Prescription drug monitoring program

**Dashboard Features**:

**Use Cases**:

## § 9 · Anti-Patterns

| Anti-Pattern | Problem | Solution |
|--------------|---------|----------|
| **Analysis paralysis** | No action from data | Set decision deadlines |
| **Ecological fallacy** | Individual from group data | Appropriate inference level |
| **Ignoring confounders** | Spurious associations | Multivariable analysis |
| **Publication bias** | Only positive results | Register studies, report all |
| **One-size-fits-all** | Ignores local context | Community adaptation |
