# Scenario Examples

## 9.1 Lung Nodule Characterization

**User:** "55-year-old male smoker (30 pack-years) has an 8mm solid lung nodule found on chest X-ray. CT shows same nodule with no calcification. What is the appropriate management?"

**Expert:**
> **Analysis:**
>
> Per Fleischner Society Guidelines (2017) for solid nodules:
>
> 1. **Nodule Characteristics**: 8mm solid nodule in a smoker = moderate-risk profile
>
> 2. **Recommended Management**:
>    - CT scan at 3 months to assess for interval growth
>    - If stable at 3 months, consider PET/CT given smoking history
>    - Nodule doubling time <400 days warrants concern
>
> 3. **PET/CT Interpretation**:
>    - SUVmax >2.5 suggests malignancy but inflammatory conditions can be false-positive
>    - Ground-glass nodules typically low SUV
>
> 4. **Shared Decision-Making**: Discuss biopsy vs. surveillance based on patient preferences and surgical risk
>
> 5. **Documentation**: Report as "8mm solid nodule, upper lobe right lung, Fleischner intermediate-risk category; recommend follow-up CT in 3 months"

## 9.2 Thyroid Nodule Assessment

**User:** "Patient has a thyroid nodule with TI-RADS score of 4 (composed of solid hypoechoic component, irregular margins, 1.5cm). How should this be managed?"

**Expert:**
> **TI-RADS Management:**
>
> 1. **TI-RADS 4 = 4 points** (solid hypoechoic = 2; irregular margins = 3; none of high suspicion features = 0): 5-20% malignancy risk
>
> 2. **Recommended Approach**:
>    - FNA indicated for nodules ≥1.5cm at TI-RADS 4
>    - If <1.0cm, can follow with ultrasound in 12 months
>
> 3. **FNA Preparation**:
>    - Verify coagulation status if off aspirin
>    - Document nodule characteristics and location
>    - Use standardized FNA report template
>
> 4. **Cytology Interpretation (Bethesda System)**:
>    - Class VI (malignant) → surgical referral
>    - Class V (suspicious for malignancy) → surgery
>    - Class IV (follicular neoplasm) → molecular testing or surgery

## 9.3 Incidental Liver Lesion

**User:** "46-year-old female undergoing CT for kidney stone has 2.5cm liver lesion described as 'probably benign hemangioma.' What workup is needed?"

**Expert:**
> **Incidental Liver Lesion Workup:**
>
> 1. **Assess Patient Risk**: CT for stone should have multiphase liver imaging; check portal venous phase for enhancement pattern
>
> 2. **Hemangioma Features**: Peripheral nodular enhancement, centripetal fill-in, typical density matches blood pool
>
> 3. **LI-RADS Classification**: 
>    - LR-1 = definitely benign (cysts, hemangiomas with classic features)
>    - LR-2 = probably benign
>    - If classic hemangioma on CT, no further workup typically needed
>
> 4. **If Indeterminate**:
>    - MRI with hepatobiliary contrast (Eovist) for characterization
>    - Tagged RBC scan for hemangioma confirmation
>
> 5. **Documentation**: "2.5cm liver lesion with imaging features characteristic of hemangioma (peripheral nodular enhancement with progressive centripetal fill-in on portal venous phase); LI-RADS LR-1, no further workup required"
