# Scenario Examples

## 9.1 Common Scenario: Bovine Respiratory Disease (BRD) Outbreak in Feedlot

**User:** Over the past 3 days, 12 animals in a pen of 100 show nasal discharge, coughing, and depression. One has died. The vet is 2 hours away. What do we do?

**Expert:**
> **Analysis:**
> BRD (Shipping Fever) is the most common feedlot disease. Treatment must begin immediately:
>
> **Immediate actions:**
> 1. **Identify all animals:** sort off all animals with clinical signs; move to hospital pen
> 2. **Begin mass medication:** Add tulathromycin (Draxxin) or florfenicol (Nuflor) to water — requires vet authorization
> 3. **Individual treatment protocol for severe cases:**
>    - Day 1: Tulathromycin 2.5 mL/100 lb SC (single dose)
>    - Day 2: Re-evaluate; if not improving, retreat with different class (e.g., florfenicol)
>    - Day 5: If 2nd treatment failed, consider chronic and cull
> 4. **Supportive care:** Provide clean water, high-quality hay; consider banamine for fever
> 5. **Notify veterinarian:** Describe symptoms, mortality, pen conditions; follow treatment plan
>
> **BRD risk factors:**
> - Recent arrival (stress, comingling)
> - Temperature fluctuations >30°F daily
> - Dusty conditions
> - High stocking density
> - Weaned <30 days
>
> **Necropsy protocol (if animal dies):**
> 1. Photograph lung lesions
> 2. Collect lung tissue in formalin for histopath
> 3. Contact vet for diagnostic workup
> 4. Submit lung to diagnostic lab for culture/sensitivity
>
> **Prevention for next shipment:**
> 1. Modified live BRD vaccine at weaning or arrival
> 2. Metaphylaxis: treat all animals on arrival with long-acting antibiotic
> 3. Stress reduction: gradual diet changes, low stocking density on arrival

## 9.2 Complex Scenario: Suspected Anthrax in Pasture Cattle

**User:** One cow died suddenly without prior signs of illness. The pasture was previously used for hay and is near a river. Neighboring herd had similar deaths last year. What do we do?

**Expert:**
> **Comprehensive Solution:**
> Sudden death with no prior illness + flooding history + neighbor deaths = anthrax until proven otherwise:
>
> **Anthrax (Bacillus anthracis) is a ZOONOTIC EMERGENCY:**
> - Humans can contract through contact with infected animals or contaminated soil
> - Wear full PPE: gloves, coveralls, respirator (N95 minimum)
> - Do NOT open the carcass
>
> **Immediate actions:**
> 1. **Do NOT handle the carcass without PPE** — anthrax spores enter through skin cuts
> 2. **Call veterinarian immediately** — they will contact state veterinarian (USDA/State)
> 3. **Isolate the pasture** — do not move surviving animals
> 4. **Report to state veterinarian** — anthrax is a reportable disease in all states
> 5. **Do not move any cattle from this pasture** — quarantine
> 6. **Necropsy only by veterinarian** in a controlled environment
>
> **Treatment of exposed animals:**
> 1. Long-acting penicillin or oxytetracycline — per veterinarian guidance
> 2. Vaccination: Spore vaccine available for exposed herds
> 3. Monitor twice daily for 2 weeks
>
> **Carcass disposal:**
> 1. Incineration preferred (1200°F for 4+ hours)
> 2. Or deep burial (>6 feet) with quicklime
> 3. Do NOT render — spreads spores
> 4. Decontaminate the area with 10% bleach solution
>
> **Long-term management:**
> 1. Annual anthrax vaccination in endemic areas
> 2. Avoid pasturing in known flood-risk areas where anthrax spores may be exposed
> 3. Do not feed bone meal of unknown origin

## 9.3 Edge Case: Organic Dairy Herd Facing Mastitis — Cannot Use Antibiotics

**User:** A cow in our certified organic dairy has acute mastitis. The organic standards prohibit antibiotic use. The cow is in severe pain and may need to be culled. What are our options?

**Expert:**
> **Special Handling:**
>
> Organic dairy has strict antibiotic prohibitions, but animal welfare is paramount:
>
> **Mastitis treatment protocol under organic standards:**
> 1. **Immediate intervention:** Cold therapy (ice packs), frequent milking to remove toxins
> 2. **Supportive care:** Anti-inflammatory (flunixin, which is permitted in organic dairy)
> 3. **Herbal and homeopathic treatments:** Teat sealants, garlic tincture (limited efficacy)
> 4. **Isolate the cow:** Monitor closely; record all treatments
>
> **Organic standards (7 CFR 205.238):**
> - Antibiotics are prohibited for any use in organic livestock
> - If antibiotics are administered, the animal loses organic status permanently
> - Milk from treated cows must be discarded for 9 days after last treatment
> - Meat withdrawal periods: 90 days after treatment
>
> **Humane culling decision:**
> 1. **If mastitis is chronic or non-responsive:** culling is the humane choice
> 2. **Acute toxic mastitis:** may require emergency slaughter under veterinary supervision
> 3. **Farm animal welfare laws** require pain management — treating an animal in distress is required
>
> **Prevention for future:**
> 1. Pre-milking teat disinfection (post-dip) is required in organic
> 2. Dry cow therapy with internal teat sealants (antibiotic dry tubes prohibited in organic)
> 3. Monitor SCC monthly: cull cows with chronic elevated SCC
> 4. Use organic-approved mastitis prevention: herbal intramammary products
