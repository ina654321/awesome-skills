# Scenario Examples

## 9.1 Common Scenario: Tractor Overheating in the Field

**User:** Our tractor is running hot at 100°F ambient temperature while running a heavy disk. Temperature gauge is in the red. What do we do?

**Expert:**
> **Analysis:**
> Overheating under heavy load at high ambient temperature is a serious issue:
>
> **Immediate actions:**
> 1. **Reduce load immediately** — disengage implement or reduce depth
> 2. **Reduce engine speed** to idle or partial throttle
> 3. **Move to a shaded area** if possible
> 4. **Shut down engine** if temperature does not drop within 2 minutes
> 5. **Do NOT open radiator cap** while hot — pressure burn risk
>
> **Cooling system check after cooled:**
> 1. Check coolant level: should be visible in overflow tank
> 2. Inspect radiator screens: remove debris, chaff, dust buildup
> 3. Check fan belt: should deflect < 1/2 inch when pressed
> 4. Inspect coolant hoses: squeeze test; replace if soft/cracked
> 5. Check thermostat: may be stuck closed
>
> **Root causes in agricultural environment:**
> - Radiator plugged with chaff/straw (most common in field)
> - Low coolant level (leak or failure)
> - Thermostat failure
> - Water pump failure (circulation loss)
> - Operating at too high an ambient temperature with heavy load
>
> **Prevention:**
> 1. Clean radiator screens with air pressure daily during harvest
> 2. Check coolant level every 8–10 hours
> 3. Use 50/50 antifreeze-water mix (not pure water in summer — cavitation risk)
> 4. Monitor temperature gauge; reduce load at 210°F

## 9.2 Complex Scenario: Combine Fire During Harvest

**User:** The combine started smoking and we suspect a belt or bearing fire. Engine is still running. What is the immediate protocol?

**Expert:**
> **Comprehensive Solution:**
> Combine fires are common during harvest due to dry conditions, chaff buildup, and mechanical heat:
>
> **Immediate actions (0–30 seconds):**
> 1. **STOP the combine immediately** — disengage header, apply brakes
> 2. **Shut off engine** — turn key to off, pull seat-cutoff if available
> 3. **Get clear of the machine** — move at least 100 feet upwind
> 4. **Call 911** — provide address, field location (nearest crossroads), describe fire
> 5. **Grab the fire extinguisher** — 10-lb ABC dry chemical minimum
>
> **Fire extinguisher use:**
> 1. Pull pin, aim at base of flame
> 2. Squeeze handle, sweep side to side
> 3. Have backup extinguisher ready
> 4. Never open the engine compartment without extinguisher ready
>
> **If fire is small and contained:**
> 1. Attack from the side, not top (heat rises)
> 2. Target the seat of the fire: bearing, belt, electrical
> 3. Once out, use thermal camera or feel for hot spots
> 4. Do NOT restart until cause identified and repaired
>
> **After the fire is out:**
> 1. Let machine cool completely (30+ minutes)
> 2. Inspect all belts, bearings, wiring
> 3. Replace all damaged components
> 4. Report to insurance immediately
> 5. Clean chaff and debris from entire machine before next use
>
> **Prevention:** Carry two fire extinguishers; clean chaff from engine bay daily; check bearings for heat every 2 hours.

## 9.3 Edge Case: Tractor Roll-Over on Steep Hillside

**User:** The tractor started sliding sideways on a 20° slope during disking. What should the operator do to prevent a rollover?

**Expert:**
> **Special Handling:**
>
> Side-slope rollovers occur when the tractor center of gravity passes the tipping point laterally. At 20° slope, this is a critical situation.
>
> **If the tractor is sliding and tilting:**
> 1. **Do NOT try to straighten** — this can accelerate the rollover
> 2. **Keep wheels turned slightly downhill** — helps slow the slide
> 3. **Try to back down the slope** slowly and carefully if at all possible
> 4. **Disengage PTO** to prevent implement from catching
> 5. **Brake gently** — not panic braking, which can tip
> 6. **Keep body inside the cab** — do not jump; roll bars protect
>
> **If rollover occurs:**
> 1. **Stay in the cab** — the ROPS and seatbelt are your protection
> 2. **Call 911 immediately**
> 3. **Do not move the tractor** until emergency services arrive
> 4. **Check for fluid leaks** — fuel, hydraulic, coolant
> 5. **Assess injuries** — call for medical helicopter if needed
>
> **Prevention for future:**
> 1. **Never operate on slopes > 15°** with a 2WD tractor or with heavy side-mounted implements
> 2. **Use 4WD tractor** on any slope > 10°
> 3. **Install duals or wheel weights** to lower center of gravity
> 4. **Use GPS guidance** to maintain consistent row alignment
> 5. **Never carry a passenger** — they are not protected by ROPS
> 6. **Always wear the seatbelt** — ROPS is useless without it
