# Scenario Examples

## 9.1 Common Scenario: Suspected Herbicide Carryover in Rotated Crop

**User:** The soybean field planted after corn shows severe injury: stunted plants, leaf cupping, and abnormal growth. Corn was sprayed with atrazine and mesotrione last year. Is this herbicide carryover?

**Expert:**
> **Analysis:**
> Herbicide carryover injury in rotated crops is a serious concern. The pattern of injury and history help identify the cause:
>
> **Diagnostic steps:**
> 1. **Review herbicide labels:** Atrazine has a 2-season restriction for sensitive crops; mesotrione (Callisto) has a 4-month rotation interval for soybean
> 2. **Assess environmental conditions:** Drought stress in the year of application reduces degradation; soil pH affects herbicide persistence
> 3. **Examine injury pattern:** Is it field-wide (carryover) or in patches (spray overlap, application error)?
> 4. **Compare with neighboring fields:** Same rotation without injury suggests application or soil factors
>
> **Most likely causes:**
> - Atrazine carryover in high-pH soils (>7.0) or drought years
> - ALS-inhibitor (imazethapyr) carryover from previous soybean
> - pH-dependent herbicide persistence: sulfonylureas persist longer in high-pH soils
>
> **Recovery options:**
> 1. **Do NOT apply additional herbicide** — will worsen injury
> 2. **Consider light cultivation** if crop is severely damaged (>40% injury)
> 3. **Assess yield potential:** fields with <50% stand may warrant replanting to different crop
> 4. **Document for insurance or input supplier claim** if cause is confirmed product failure
>
> **Prevention for next season:**
> 1. Use rotation intervals per herbicide label (most conservative)
> 2. Soil test pH: maintain pH 6.5–7.0 for optimal herbicide degradation
> 3. Use PPO-inhibitor herbicides (Flexstar, Warrant) in rotated soybean if carryover risk exists
> 4. Apply LibertyLink or Enlist soybean systems for more flexible rotation

## 9.2 Complex Scenario: Aflatoxin Contamination Suspected in Corn

**User:** Corn samples show bright greenish-yellow fluorescence under UV light. Is this aflatoxin? What do we do?

**Expert:**
> **Comprehensive Solution:**
> Aflatoxin is a serious food/feed safety issue with strict regulatory limits:
>
> **Regulatory limits:**
> | End Use | Aflatoxin Limit |
> |---------|----------------|
> | Human food (FDA) | 20 ppb |
> | Dairy feed | 0.5 ppb |
> | Breeding cattle | 100 ppb |
> | Finishing beef | 300 ppb |
>
> **Diagnostic steps:**
> 1. **UV light test (Blacklight):** Bright greenish-yellow fluorescence indicates Aspergillus — positive for ~80% of samples with >20 ppb
> 2. **Send samples for ELISA or HPLC testing** — only lab analysis gives quantitative result
> 3. **Determine extent:** How many fields, storage bins affected?
>
> **If confirmed (lab results >20 ppb):**
> 1. **Segregate affected grain** — do not blend with clean grain
> 2. **Do not sell for food use** — violates FDA regulations
> 3. **Consider ethanol plants** — aflatoxin is largely destroyed in fermentation
> 4. **Consider price discount** for animal feed sale if within limits
> 5. **Check crop insurance** — aflatoxin may be a covered cause of loss
>
> **Prevention for next season:**
> 1. Select aflatoxin-resistant hybrids
> 2. Avoid drought stress at pollination: irrigation if possible
> 3. Manage insect damage: ear worms create infection sites
> 4. Harvest at moisture >18% and dry to <15% quickly
> 5. Store grain cool and dry: <14% moisture, <50°F

## 9.3 Edge Case: Neighbor Spraying Drift onto Organic Crop

**User:** Conventional neighbor applied 2,4-D near our certified organic soybean field. We suspect drift. What are our options?

**Expert:**
> **Special Handling:**
>
> Pesticide drift onto organic crops is a serious issue that can jeopardize certification:
>
> **Immediate actions:**
> 1. **Document everything:** Photograph affected area; GPS coordinates; date/time of suspected application
> 2. **Preserve evidence:** Collect plant tissue samples in Ziploc bags; freeze
> 3. **Contact applicator:** Request notice of what was sprayed and when
> 4. **Contact state pesticide regulatory agency** (e.g., Department of Agriculture): file formal complaint
> 5. **Contact your organic certifying agency:** They need to know for compliance records
>
> **Economic damage assessment:**
> 1. Estimate yield loss in affected acres
> 2. Document market value of lost premium (organic price vs. conventional)
> 3. Determine if contamination prevents organic certification
> 4. Obtain contractor estimates for crop removal and land recovery
>
> **Legal options:**
> 1. **Negotiate with applicator:** Compensation for lost income
> 2. **Small claims court or civil litigation:** Negligence standard; damages require proof
> 3. **State regulatory action:** State may fine applicator; does not compensate farmer
> 4. **Organic Farming Research Foundation** legal resources
>
> **Recovery time:**
> - 2,4-D injury: crop may recover if <20% defoliation; otherwise total loss
> - Land buffer zones: typically 150–500 feet required near organic operations
> - Organic certification: 36-month transition required if contamination requires land renovation
>
> **Prevention:** Establish written neighbor agreements; plant buffer strips; document aerial application dates; GPS-mark affected areas immediately
