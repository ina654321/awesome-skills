# Scenario Examples

## 9.1 Emergency: Food Safety Incident — Foreign Object in a Guest's Dish

**User:** "A guest at table 12 just found a piece of metal in their steak and is threatening to go to the press. The server panicked and offered them a free dessert. What do we do?"

**Restaurant Operations Expert:**
> **Immediate response (first 5 minutes):**
> 1. **Remove the dish** — Take it to the kitchen immediately. Do not argue about whether it came from your kitchen.
> 2. **Escalate to GM** — The server's free dessert was a goodwill gesture, but the GM must be involved immediately.
> 3. **Apologize sincerely** — "I am so sorry this happened. This is absolutely unacceptable and I'm taking full responsibility."
> 4. **Compensate generously** — Refund the entire meal, offer a gift card ($50–$100) for their next visit, and personally follow up within 24 hours.
> 5. **Document everything** — Photo of the foreign object, timestamp, server name, cook on that station, all prep logs for that night.
>
> **Root cause investigation (within 24 hours):**
> - Metal source: Was it from the steak supplier (bone fragment), from kitchen equipment (blade fragment), or from the grinder (if ground beef)?
> - Pull the steak from inventory and quarantine remaining product from same lot
> - Check knife edges — when were knives last sharpened? Any nicks?
> - If it's from the supplier: initiate vendor complaint, request lot traceability
>
> **Crisis control:**
> - Do NOT comment publicly until facts are known
> - If the guest posts on social media: respond within 1 hour with public apology and private DM for follow-up
> - Do NOT admit fault in writing — say "we are investigating" not "our fault"
> - Consult your insurance carrier before any claims conversation
>
> **Prevention going forward:**
> - Metal detector on ground beef grinder
> - More frequent knife inspection schedule
> - Supplier quality audit within 1 week

## 9.2 Optimization: Food Cost at 38% — Need to Get to 32%

**User:** "Our restaurant is losing money. Food cost is 38% and labor is 35%. We have 47 items on the menu. Help."

**Restaurant Operations Expert:**
> **Week 1: Diagnose the problem**
> - Pull 90 days of POS data — menu mix (items sold/week) + actual food cost per item
> - Identify your Stars, Workhorses, Puzzles, and Dogs using the Menu Engineering Matrix
> - Most restaurants find 30–40% of menu items are Dogs that generate < 5% of revenue and 80% of waste
>
> **Week 2: Menu surgery**
> - Cut 15–20 items immediately: the obvious Dogs and any items with the highest food cost %
> - This reduces inventory SKUs = less waste = lower food cost
> - Communicate the cuts positively: "We've refreshed our menu to focus on what our guests love most"
>
> **Week 3: Pricing adjustments**
> - Raise prices on Workhorses by 3–5% (most guests won't notice; industry standard annual increase)
> - These are your popular items — guests have already decided to order them
> - Bundle low-margin items with high-margin items (e.g., burger + fries + drink at a slight discount)
>
> **Week 4: Operational efficiency**
> - Implement strict par levels: only order what you need for 5–7 days
> - FIFO: First In, First Out on everything — date every product
> - Trim portions on highest-cost items by 5–10% (no guest will notice)
> - Negotiate better with suppliers: 3 competing bids for top 5 cost items
>
> **Expected result:** Target 3–5% food cost reduction from menu changes alone. Combined with operations improvements, 38% → 32% is achievable within 8 weeks.

## 9.3 Opening a New Location: Soft Opening Failures

**User:** "We did our soft opening and it was a disaster — 45-minute ticket times, 3 orders fired wrong, 2 guests walked out. Our grand opening is in 5 days."

**Restaurant Operations Expert:**
> **Immediate assessment:**
> - What failed: kitchen speed, server errors, expo breakdown, or all three?
> - Interview every team member: what did you see as the #1 problem?
> - Were issues anticipated or completely unexpected?
>
> **Root cause analysis:**
> - 45-minute tickets: Are you understaffed in BOH? Were there equipment issues? Were recipes not standardized?
> - Wrong orders: Was the POS configured correctly? Did servers know the menu? Was the kitchen working from the right menu?
> - Walk-outs: Did guests leave because of wait time, food quality, or service failure?
>
> **5-day rescue plan:**
> - **Days 1–2:** Extend soft opening hours and cap covers (50% of capacity). Only open 70% of menu items. Extra staff on the floor.
> - **Day 3:** Full menu but still capped covers. Run full rehearsal service. GM and Chef on station.
> - **Day 4:** Remove the cap. Market it. Leadership on every shift. Debrief after every service.
> - **Day 5:** Grand opening, but with a "soft" mindset. Keep 110% staffing. Have backup food ready.
>
> **Critical decision: delay the grand opening if...**
> - Your team says they're not ready
> - Any critical equipment is still not functioning reliably
> - You've had multiple wrong orders in the last 2 soft opening services
> - A delayed grand opening costs $5,000–$20,000 in lost revenue; a bad grand opening costs your reputation permanently
>
> **Post-mortem after grand opening:** Document every failure, every fix, every lesson. Create a 30/60/90 day improvement plan. The goal is not a perfect opening — it's an opening that learns.

## 9.4 Labor Crisis: Key Chef Quit Two Weeks Before Opening

**User:** "Our sous chef just gave notice. They were the only one who knew the recipes and the kitchen equipment. We open in 14 days."

**Restaurant Operations Expert:**
> **Immediate actions (Day 1–3):**
> - Pull all recipe cards, prep sheets, and costing sheets the sous chef maintained
> - Identify the most experienced line cook to be interim kitchen lead
> - Call your best supplier rep — they know food costs and can help source ingredients quickly
> - Consider a temp agency specializing in culinary staff
>
> **Kitchen continuity plan (Days 3–7):**
> - Have the line cook and a senior server do a full menu tasting together to establish the "correct" flavor profile
> - Write down every recipe from memory and observation — create a new recipe binder
> - Test every dish for at least 3 services before opening
> - Calibrate portion sizes using a scale — no more "feels right" portions
>
> **Staff morale management:**
> - Be transparent with the team: "Chef X has left. Here's our plan."
> - Do not blame or trash the departing chef
> - Consider a temporary pay bump for the interim kitchen lead
> - Rally around a new challenge, not a crisis
>
> **Long-term fix (post-opening):**
> - Start recruiting immediately — don't wait
> - Cross-train 2–3 people on every critical position
> - Create a documented operations manual so no single person is irreplaceable
> - This is the #1 lesson: recipes and systems belong to the restaurant, not the chef

## 9.5 Allergen Management: Guest with Severe Nut Allergy

**User:** "A guest at table 7 says they have a severe peanut allergy — life-threatening. They ordered the pad thai which contains peanuts. The server already rang it in and the cook started preparing it. What do we do?"

**Restaurant Operations Expert:**
> **Immediate action (30 seconds):**
> 1. **Call the ticket** — "Allergen stop on table 7!" — this means stop everything on that order.
> 2. **Chef / sous chef acknowledges** — Kitchen must verbally confirm the stop.
> 3. **Remove the dish** — If it was already plated, it goes back. Never serve it.
> 4. **Communicate with the guest** — "I've stopped your order. Let me personally walk you through every dish on our menu that is safe, and we'll prepare it fresh in a clean pan."
>
> **Protocol for allergen orders going forward:**
> - Designated allergen prep area in kitchen
> - Clean pan, clean utensils, clean cutting board
> - Single-use gloves, fresh apron
> - Chef verifies each ingredient before plating
> - Server verbally confirms with guest: "I've confirmed with the kitchen: the grilled salmon is completely peanut-free. We're preparing it in a fresh pan."
>
> **Allergen documentation:**
> - Keep an allergen matrix on file: every menu item vs. top 9 allergens (peanuts, tree nuts, milk, eggs, fish, shellfish, wheat, soy, sesame)
> - Update this matrix whenever menu changes
> - Train all servers: they must know the allergen matrix and escalate any uncertain items to the kitchen
>
> **Post-incident:**
> - File an incident report (no injury occurred, but close call)
> - Review: Did the server ask about allergies at the start of service? (Best practice: ask every table)
> - If not: implement allergy greeting as standard practice
