# Clinical Physician — Troubleshooting Guide

## Diagnostic Challenges

**Problem: Differential diagnosis too broad — can't narrow down**
- Technique: Apply VINDICATE framework systematically
- Technique: Use anatomical vs. pathophysiologic approach
- Check: Are you anchoring on first impression?
- Strategy: Generate ≥3 differentials before testing
- Bayesian update: Test with highest sensitivity for most likely diagnosis first

**Problem: Test results don't fit the clinical picture**
- Principle: Clinical judgment > test result
- Rule: If it looks like X but test says Y, question the test
- Check: Was the test result a false positive/negative?
- Consider: Repeat testing, different test, or proceed with clinical judgment

**Problem: "Zebra" diagnosis suspected but colleagues dismissive**
- Principle: Common things are common — but zebras exist
- Criteria for pursuing rare diagnosis:
  - Pattern doesn't fit common causes
  - Test results point toward
  - Consequences of missing are serious
- Approach: Present evidence to team, not opinion

## Treatment Problems

**Problem: Standard guideline treatment not working**
- Verify: Is the diagnosis correct?
- Verify: Is the patient actually taking the medication?
- Consider: Drug interactions reducing effectiveness?
- Consider: Resistant organism or atypical presentation
- Escalate: Consult specialist; consider alternatives

**Problem: Patient refusing recommended treatment**
- Principle: Patient autonomy is paramount
- Technique: Shared decision-making
- Steps:
  1. Understand patient's concerns
  2. Explain risks of refusal clearly
  3. Document discussion thoroughly
  4. Offer alternatives if available
- If incompetent patient: Consult ethics, consider guardianship

**Problem: Polypharmacy concerns**
- Review: List ALL medications (prescribed, OTC, supplements)
- Deprescribing framework:
  1. Is each medication still indicated?
  2. Are benefits outweighing harms?
  3. Can any be stopped or dose reduced?
- Use Beers Criteria for elderly patients

## Communication Challenges

**Problem: Delivering bad news to patient/family**
- Technique: SPIKES protocol
  1. S — Setting (private, comfortable)
  2. P — Perception (what do they understand?)
  3. I — Invitation (how much detail do they want?)
  4. K — Knowledge (share clearly, no jargon)
  5. E — Emotions (acknowledge and empathize)
  6. S — Strategy and summary (next steps)

**Problem: Communication breakdown with consulting service**
- Document: Your question clearly in the note
- Include: What you've done so far
- Specify: What you need from consultant
- Timeline: Urgency of response needed
- Follow up: If no response within expected time

**Problem: Family demanding tests you don't think are needed**
- Principle: Patient autonomy; but also physician judgment
- Approach:
  1. Explain why the test isn't indicated
  2. Explain risks of unnecessary testing
  3. If test is harmless and patient insists: shared decision
  4. If test has risk: decline with explanation
  5. Document the discussion

## Monitoring & Follow-up

**Problem: Outpatient test result returns abnormal after discharge**
- Protocol:
  1. Review result and clinical significance
  2. Contact patient within 48 hours
  3. Arrange appropriate follow-up
  4. Document notification and plan
- If patient unreachable: Send certified letter; document all attempts

**Problem: Patient condition deteriorating faster than expected**
- Assessment: ABCs first
- Immediate: Re-evaluate diagnosis
- Consider: Was diagnosis wrong? Is this a complication?
- Escalate: Increase monitoring; consider ICU consult
- Documentation: Detailed nursing and physician assessments

## Hospital-Specific Problems

**Problem: Discharge planning barriers**
- Identify barriers: Social, financial, medical
- Social work: Early involvement for complex cases
- Medication access: Generic alternatives, patient assistance programs
- Follow-up: Ensure appointments made before discharge
- Communication: Clear discharge instructions, 24-hour contact number

**Problem: Escalating pain in hospitalized patient**
- Assessment: Use standardized pain scale
- Verify: Is pain medication being given as prescribed?
- Check: Drug interactions reducing efficacy?
- Consider: Non-opioid adjuncts (acetaminophen, NSAIDs, Gabapentin)
- Avoid: Opioid escalation without clear indication
- Consult: Pain management if complex

## Clinical Reasoning Pitfalls

**Problem: Tunnel vision on initial diagnosis**
- Self-check: "What else could this be?"
- Force: List 3 alternative diagnoses before proceeding
- Red flag: Young patients with "classic" presentations — consider atypical

**Problem: Over-relying on test results**
- Principle: Labs don't make diagnoses — clinical picture does
- Check: Pre-test probability affects interpretation
- False positive: Low pre-test + positive = check again
- Context: Isolated lab abnormality without clinical context may not be actionable

**Problem: Missing subtle signs of deterioration**
- Screening: Regular reassessment of vital signs and mental status
- Early warning: Know your hospital's rapid response criteria
- Escalation: If concerned, call early; better to be wrong than late
