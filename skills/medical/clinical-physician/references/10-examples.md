# Clinical Physician — Real-World Scenarios

## Scenario 1: Urgent Chest Pain Assessment

**Context:** 58-year-old male, 2 hours of substernal chest pressure radiating to left arm, diaphoresis, hypertension, former smoker.

**⚠️ Immediate Actions Required:**
1. **12-lead ECG within 10 minutes** — Rule out STEMI
2. **Aspirin 325mg** — If not contraindicated
3. **Troponin I** — Draw immediately and at 3 hours
4. **IV access, oxygen if SpO2 <94%**, cardiac monitor

**Clinical Reasoning:**
- Pre-test probability for ACS: HIGH
- Classic presentation: pressure, radiation, diaphoresis + risk factors

**HEART Score estimation:**
- History: 2 (highly suspicious)
- ECG: pending
- Age: 2 (58 years)
- Risk factors: 2 (HTN + smoking)
- Troponin: pending
- Estimated minimum: 6 → HIGH RISK → Admit for observation

**Differential (must-not-miss):**
1. ACS (STEMI/NSTEMI) — Most likely given presentation
2. Aortic dissection — Tearing pain, blood pressure differential
3. PE — Pleuritic, tachycardia, hypoxia
4. Tension pneumothorax — Absent breath sounds, hypotension

**Management:**
- Admit to observation unit or cardiology
- Serial troponins q3h
- Cardiology consult
- Risk stratify with HEART score

## Scenario 2: Acute Dyspnea in a Young Adult

**Context:** 26-year-old female, sudden onset dyspnea and right-sided pleuritic chest pain, recent 8-hour flight, on oral contraceptives.

**Risk Assessment:**
- Wells PE Score:
  - PE #1 diagnosis: +3.0
  - HR >100: +1.5 (assuming tachycardia)
  - Immobilization (long flight): +1.5
  - **Estimated score: 6.0 → HIGH probability**

**Why PE is high on differential:**
- OCP use: 3-5× increased VTE risk (estrogen effect)
- Long flight: venous stasis
- Classic presentation: sudden onset + pleuritic + dyspnea

**Differential:**
1. PE — Must not miss; treat as high probability
2. Pneumothorax — Spontaneous, especially tall thin young women
3. Pleuritis — Viral or autoimmune; diagnosis of exclusion
4. MSK — Less likely given onset and travel context

**Workup:**
- CTPA (CT Pulmonary Angiography) — Gold standard
- D-dimer NOT reliable here (high pre-test probability)
- If CTPA contraindicated: V/Q scan

**Treatment while awaiting CTPA:**
- Anticoagulation (heparin) if no contraindications
- Hold OCP pending workup

## Scenario 3: Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA) Management

**Context:** Type 1 diabetic, glucose 520 mg/dL, pH 7.18, bicarbonate 10 mEq/L, ketones 4+, K+ 5.2 mEq/L. Patient is nauseated and vomiting.

**Diagnostic Confirmation:**
- Glucose >250 ✅ (520)
- Anion gap acidosis: AG = 138-(100+10) = 28 ✅ (>20 = elevated)
- pH <7.3 ✅ (7.18)
- Ketosis ✅ (4+)
- **Diagnosis: DKA — Severe (pH <7.2)**

**Immediate Management:**

1. **IV Fluids (hour 0-2):**
   - NS 1-1.5 L in first hour
   - Then 0.45% NaCl at 250-500 mL/hr
   - Switch to D5W when glucose <250

2. **Insulin (hour 2+):**
   - Regular insulin drip: 0.1 units/kg/hr
   - **Hold insulin if K+ <3.5** — Her K+ is 5.2, OK to start
   - Target: glucose drop 50-75 mg/dL/hr

3. **Potassium:**
   - K+ 5.2: Hold potassium initially
   - Recheck K+ at 2 hours
   - Expected K+ to DROP as acidosis corrects
   - Add 20-40 mEq K+ per liter when K+ <5.0

4. **Bicarbonate:**
   - Only if pH <6.9 (controversial even then)
   - pH 7.18: No bicarbonate needed

**Resolution criteria:**
- Anion gap closed (AG <12)
- pH >7.3
- Patient tolerating oral intake
- Transition to subcutaneous insulin

## Scenario 4: Sepsis Recognition and Management

**Context:** 65-year-old female with fever, chills, confusion, BP 85/50, HR 120, RR 28, WBC 18,000, Lactate 4.5 mEq/L.

**Sepsis-3 Recognition (qSOFA ≥2 = high risk):**
- Altered mental status ✅
- RR ≥ 22 ✅
- SBP ≤ 100 ✅
- **qSOFA = 3 → High risk for poor outcomes**

**Full Sepsis Criteria (SIRS + Infection):**
- Infection: Presumed (clinical picture)
- SIRS: WBC 18, HR 120, RR 28 ✅
- Organ dysfunction: Lactate 4.5, hypotension ✅
- **Diagnosis: Septic Shock**

**Hour-1 Bundle ( Surviving Sepsis Campaign):**
1. **Lactate** — Measured, then repeat in 2-4h if elevated
2. **Blood cultures** — Before antibiotics if >3h delay expected
3. **Broad-spectrum antibiotics** — Within 1 hour
4. **Fluid resuscitation** — 30 mL/kg crystalloid for hypotension or lactate ≥4
5. **Vasopressors** — If hypotension persists after fluids

**Source Control:**
- Identify infection source: Urine? Lung? Abdomen? Skin?
- Imaging as needed
- Surgical/interventional source control if indicated

## Scenario 5: Hyponatremia Workup

**Context:** 72-year-old female, routine lab shows Na+ 122 mEq/L. Patient is lethargic but arousable. PMH: hypertension on hydrochlorothiazide.

**Assessment:**
- Severe hyponatremia (Na <125) with symptoms
- Thiazide diuretic use — classic cause of hyponatremia
- Symptoms suggest cerebral edema from osmotic water shift

**Immediate:**
- Assess volume status:
  - Thiazides cause hyponatremia through volume depletion + ADH
  - Most are euvolemic or slightly hypovolemic
- Check: Serum osmolality, urine osmolality, urine sodium

**Expected findings in thiazide-induced hyponatremia:**
- Serum osmolality: Low (hypotonic) — CONFIRMED
- Urine osmolality: Inappropriately high (>100) — CONFIRMED
- Urine sodium: >20 mEq/L — CONFIRMED

**Management:**
- **Hypertonic saline (3% NaCl) if severe symptoms** — Lethargy suggests this
- **Correction rate: ≤8-10 mEq/L in 24 hours** — Too fast causes osmotic demyelination
- Stop thiazide immediately
- Fluid restriction
- Consider demeclocycline or vaptans if chronic

**Critical warning:** Overcorrection → Osmotic Demyelination Syndrome (central pontine myelinolysis). Patient cannot walk, talk, or swallow properly. Treatment is supportive; often permanent.

## Scenario 6: Upper GI Bleeding Management

**Context:** 55-year-old male, hematemesis, HR 115, BP 95/60, skin pallor. History of NSAID use for arthritis.

**Initial Stabilization (ABCs):**
1. **Two large-bore IVs** — 18G or larger
2. **Fluid resuscitation** — Crystalloid bolus
3. **Blood type and crossmatch** — Prepare for transfusion
4. **NG tube** — Can help localize bleeding (bright red = active upper bleed)
5. **Cardiac monitor** — For patient with hemodynamic instability

**Risk Stratification:**
- GBS (Glasgow-Blatchford Score): High (hemodynamically unstable, melena, etc.)
- Rockall Score: Can use pre- and post-endoscopy components
- **High risk → Admit to ICU or step-down**

**Diagnostic:**
- **EGD (Esophagogastroduodenoscopy)** — Within 24 hours; sooner if unstable
- Look for: Peptic ulcer, varices, esophagitis, malignancy

**Medical Management:**
- Proton pump inhibitor IV bolus → drip
- Octreotide for suspected varices
- Antibiotics if cirrhosis suspected
- Anticoagulation/antiplatelet: Hold vs. bridge per risk assessment

**If EGD fails:** Consider interventional radiology (angiography + embolization) or surgery.
