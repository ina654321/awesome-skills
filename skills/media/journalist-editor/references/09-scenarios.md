# Scenario Examples

## 9.1 Politician's Out-of-Context Quote

**User:** "A politician claims their opponent 'voted against funding for veterans' three times. We find they voted against a specific bill that had problematic provisions but also voted for veteran funding later. What's the rating?"

**Expert:**
> **Analysis:**
>
> Contextual claims require nuanced handling:
>
> 1. **Determine the core claim**: Did they vote against veteran funding? Yes and no.
> 2. **Assess the framing**: Is the claim accurate given the full context?
> 3. **Rate appropriately**: This is "Half True" or "Misleading"
> 4. **Explain fully**: "The politician voted against bills that included veteran funding, but later voted for other veteran funding measures"
> 5. **Give context**: Explain the specific bills and their provisions
> 6. **Don't editorialize**: Stick to what evidence shows
>
> Nuance matters. "Technically true" can still be misleading.

## 9.2 Viral Image Is Old But Real

**User:** "A viral image shows a real event from 10 years ago but is being shared as current. How do we rate this?"

**Expert:**
> **Comprehensive Solution:**
>
> Old images in new contexts:
>
> 1. **Verify authenticity**: Is this a real image from the claimed event?
> 2. **Verify recency**: When did the event actually occur?
> 3. **Assess intent**: Is this intentional misinformation or genuine mistake?
> 4. **Rate the claim**: "This image is from [date], not [current claim]"
> 5. **Explain**: The event is real, but the date/context is false
> 6. **Provide correction**: Link to original context
>
> Real events shared as current events is a common manipulation tactic.

## 9.3 Expert Consensus vs. Controversy

**User:** "We find 90% of climate scientists agree that human activity causes climate change, but one credentialed expert disagrees publicly. How do we rate the claim 'climate change is not caused by humans'?"

**Expert:**
> **Special Handling:**
>
> Minority expert positions:
>
> 1. **Acknowledge the outlier**: Yes, this expert disagrees
> 2. **Context matters**: What is the consensus among relevant experts?
> 3. **Credential comparison**: Is the dissenter equally qualified in the relevant field?
> 4. **Rate the claim**: "False" - contradicts expert consensus
> 5. **Explain nuance**: You can note debate exists without giving false equivalence
> 6. **Be clear**: "X% of experts in [field] agree that..."
>
> Scientific consensus doesn't mean no debate exists, but minority positions don't equal legitimate controversy.

## 9.4 Fact-Checking a Fact-Check

**User:** "A reader asks us to fact-check our own fact-check from last year that we're now unsure was fully accurate. How do we handle this?"

**Expert:**
> **Special Handling:**
>
> Self-correction:
>
> 1. **Take it seriously**: Reader feedback is valuable
> 2. **Re-review the evidence**: Is the original rating still justified?
> 3. **Consult if needed**: Senior reviewer or additional experts
> 4. **If the rating was wrong**: Correct it publicly
> 5. **Be transparent**: "We revisit our fact-checks when new evidence emerges"
> 6. **Don't defend the indefensible**: Correction builds credibility
>
> Credibility comes from accuracy, not defensiveness.

## 9.5 Fact-Checking Satire

**User:** "A satirical article from The Onion is being shared as real news. What do we do?"

**Expert:**
> **Special Handling:**
>
> Satire vs. news:
>
> 1. **Verify it's satire**: Check The Onion's website, publication date
> 2. **Assess spread**: How widely is this being taken seriously?
> 3. **Rate the spread**: "Satire being shared as news"
> 4. **Identify the source**: Clearly identify The Onion and that it's satire
> 5. **Don't over-rate**: This is a context failure, not a factual claim
> 6. **Consider the satire angle**: Why is this being shared as real?
>
> Satire is protected speech. Your job is to clarify context, not debunk.
