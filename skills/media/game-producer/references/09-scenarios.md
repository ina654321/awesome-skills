# Scenario Examples

## 9.1 Scope Creep Destroys Timeline

**User:** "Three months before launch, the CEO wants to add multiplayer and three new game modes. This would require 6+ months. What do you do?"

**Expert:**
> **Analysis:**
>
> Scope creep at critical stage:
>
> 1. **Quantify the impact**: "Adding multiplayer requires [X] months and [Y] budget"
> 2. **Present options**:
>    - Post-launch multiplayer DLC
>    - Reduced scope multiplayer
>    - Sequels as multiplayer platform
> 3. **Stakeholder communication**: Executive reality check
> 4. **Risk assessment**: What fails if we add scope?
> 5. **Professional recommendation**: Launch solid single-player; multiplayer later
> 6. **Document decision**: Whatever is decided, get it in writing
>
> Feature creep kills games. A delayed game can be great; a bad game stays bad.

## 9.2 Lead Developer Leaves Mid-Project

**User:** "The lead programmer gives two weeks' notice. They're essential to the game's core systems. What do you do?"

**Expert:**
> **Comprehensive Solution:**
>
> Key personnel departure:
>
> 1. **Immediate assessment**: How much are they responsible for?
> 2. **Knowledge transfer**: Two weeks of documentation sprint
> 3. **Recruitment**: Can someone be hired/found quickly?
> 4. **Timeline impact**: Can deadline hold?
> 5. **Team morale**: Address concerns, don't hide situation
> 6. **Contingency planning**: Can you ship without their feature?
>
> Prevention: Code reviews, shared knowledge, good documentation culture.

## 9.3 Publisher Wants to Delay Ship

**User:** "The publisher says they want to delay launch by 4 months. The team thinks the game is ready. How do you mediate?"

**Expert:**
> **Special Handling:**
>
> Release date disagreements:
>
> 1. **Get their reasoning**: Is it marketing calendar? Quality concerns?
> 2. **Assess your readiness**: Are you sure it's ready? Really sure?
> 3. **Calculate cost of delay**: Burn rate, team impact, market timing
> 4. **Present data**: What does QA say? What bugs remain?
> 5. **Negotiate middle ground**: 6-week polish vs. 4-month delay?
> 6. **Consider alternatives**: Day-one patch, post-launch fixes
> 7. **Decision matrix**: Risk of shipping vs. risk of delay
>
> Publishers have marketing insight. Developers have quality insight. Both matter.

## 9.4 Studio Announces Game Publicly Before Ready

**User:** "Marketing announced our game at E3 without telling the dev team. The game is 18 months from ship. Now we're getting constant pressure. How do you handle this?"

**Expert:**
> **Special Handling:**
>
> Public announcement pressure:
>
> 1. **Calm the team**: Public announcements happen
> 2. **Get clear timeline**: When is the game really coming?
> 3. **Communicate up**: Marketing needs realistic expectations
> 4. **Set external communication boundaries**: Developer blogs can show progress
> 5. **Manage hype carefully**: Set appropriate expectations
> 6. **Don't promise what you can't deliver**: Conservative estimates
>
> Announcements create pressure. Channel it into productive work.

## 9.5 QA Finds Game-Breaking Bug at Gold Master

**User:** "Two days before gold master submission, QA finds a bug that crashes the game in 5% of playthroughs. How do you handle this?"

**Expert:**
> **Special Handling:**
>
> Late-stage critical bug:
>
> 1. **Assess scope**: Can it be reproduced consistently?
> 2. **Time estimate**: How long to fix?
> 3. **Fix vs. delay decision**: Cost of delay vs. day-one patch
> 4. **Platform communication**: Can cert timeline adjust?
> 5. **Day-one patch plan**: Submit with patch ready for day one
> 6. **Transparency**: Get studio/exec aligned on risk
>
> Day-one patches are industry standard now. But don't abuse the trust.
