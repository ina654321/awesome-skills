# Scenario Examples

## 9.1 Using Indigenous Cultural Elements

**User:** "I want to incorporate traditional Native American patterns into a commercial fashion line. How do I do this respectfully?"

**Expert:**
> **Analysis:**
>
> Commercial use of indigenous cultural elements:
>
> 1. **Research the specific tradition**: Not all traditions are shareable
> 2. **Identify the source community**: Which nation/tribe?
> 3. **Engage directly**: "I'd like to learn about appropriate use"
> 4. **FPIC process**: Free, Prior, Informed Consent required
> 5. **Benefit sharing**: Revenue sharing, credit, community investment
> 6. **Licensing options**: Work with cultural tourism offices
> 7. **Consider alternatives**: Collaborate with indigenous artists directly
>
> Not all cultural elements are appropriate for commercial use. Respect boundaries.

## 9.2 Reporting on Sensitive Historical Events

**User:** "I'm producing a documentary about a traumatic historical event that affected a specific community. Some survivors don't want it covered. How do I proceed?"

**Expert:**
> **Comprehensive Solution:**
>
> Traumatic history sensitivity:
>
> 1. **Understand the resistance**: What specifically concerns them?
> 2. **Assess your purpose**: Why does this story need telling?
> 3. **Explore alternatives**: Can survivors tell their own story?
> 4. **Public interest vs. harm**: Is there compelling reason to proceed?
> 5. **Compensation**: Are affected communities benefiting?
> 6. **Do no harm standard**: Would this cause ongoing harm?
> 7. **Final decision**: If community strongly opposes, reconsider
>
> Sometimes the most ethical choice is not to tell a story, even if you can.

## 9.3 Cultural Elements in Game/Tv Development

**User:** "Our fantasy game/TV show is inspired by real-world cultures but we don't want to 'appropriate.' How do we do this?"

**Expert:**
> **Special Handling:**
>
> Inspired by vs. appropriating:
>
> 1. **What's your inspiration source?** Direct cultural use vs. general inspiration
> 2. **Consult specific cultures**: Don't assume one person represents all
> 3. **Hire cultural consultants**: Budget for expertise
> 4. **Avoid stereotypes**: Don't flatten complex cultures
> 5. **Credit your sources**: Be transparent about inspiration
> 6. **Consider benefit sharing**: Donations, collaborations
> 7. **Authentic casting**: Hire from represented cultures
>
> Fantasy can honor real cultures without exploitation.

## 9.4 Community Claims Ownership of Your Work

**User:** "After publishing research on traditional crafts, a community claims the traditional knowledge is theirs and demands you stop distribution."

**Expert:**
> **Special Handling:**
>
> Traditional knowledge ownership:
>
> 1. **Review your agreements**: Did you obtain proper consent?
> 2. **Understand TK frameworks**: WIPO, UNESCO guidelines
> 3. **Consult cultural IP attorney**: This is legally complex
> 4. **Open dialogue**: Listen to their concerns genuinely
> 5. **Negotiate solution**: Attribution, co-authorship, revenue share
> 6. **Document pre-existing agreements**: Were they clear?
> 7. **Know your rights**: Published research has protections too
>
> TK ownership is evolving legally and ethically. Engage respectfully.

## 9.5 Media Coverage Misrepresents Culture

**User:** "Your cultural community was misrepresented in a major publication. They want you to respond publicly. What do you do?"

**Expert:**
> **Special Handling:**
>
> Community defense:
>
> 1. **Verify the misrepresentation**: Is it actually inaccurate?
> 2. **Assess the venue**: Who can correct this?
> 3. **Community consensus**: Does the community want response?
> 4. **Craft response**: Factual corrections, respectful tone
> 5. **Choose channels**: Direct to editor, public statement, social media
> 6. **Follow up**: Did they publish corrections?
> 7. **Long-term relationship**: Can you work with this outlet going forward?
>
> Community representation is a responsibility. Respond thoughtfully.
